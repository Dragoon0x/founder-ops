# Red Flag: One Customer Is Too Big

## The pattern
Your largest customer is 20%+ of ARR (or trending toward it). You
start making product decisions for them specifically. Their churn
would be a company-defining event.

## Why it's a red flag
Customer concentration is the single biggest predictor of catastrophic
revenue loss at early-stage startups.

If one customer is 30% of your revenue and they leave, you're immediately
in a crisis: layoffs, product pivots, potentially shutdown.

Investors penalize concentration heavily:
- At Series A: max customer > 25% can kill a deal.
- At Series B: max customer > 15% raises questions.
- At IPO: max customer > 10% is disclosed prominently.

## Diagnosing severity

### Level 1 (monitor)
- Max customer: 10–15% of ARR
- Growing in absolute terms
- Multi-year contract
- Happy customer (NPS 8+)
- No known competitors in active pursuit

### Level 2 (act within 1–2 quarters)
- Max customer: 15–25% of ARR
- Top 3 customers: 40%+ of ARR
- Contract expires in < 12 months
- Customer going through M&A or leadership change
- Starting to drive your roadmap

### Level 3 (immediate action)
- Max customer: > 25% of ARR
- Top 3 customers: > 60% of ARR
- Contract expires in < 6 months
- Any signal of competition or dissatisfaction
- Your product roadmap is primarily driven by them
- They have an internal option to build themselves

## What to do

### At Level 1
- Track concentration quarterly.
- Diversify acquisition channels.
- Ensure contract is multi-year with annual renewal or locked.
- Invest in CS for this customer proportionally.

### At Level 2
- Focus sales team on acquiring mid-market customers.
- Goal: reduce max customer to < 15% by adding 2–3 significant customers.
- Lock a multi-year contract with escalators.
- Diversify product features beyond this customer's needs.
- Have a CEO-level relationship with the customer's CEO.

### At Level 3
This is a high-priority initiative for 2+ quarters:
- **Primary goal**: land 3+ new enterprise customers in 6 months.
- **Secondary goal**: locked multi-year contract with the big one.
- Pull sales resources from other segments.
- If needed, hire a dedicated enterprise AE.
- Consider strategic adjacencies that don't depend on this customer.
- Do NOT hire or invest based on this revenue.

## The diligence test

Assume the customer leaves in 6 months. What happens?
- **Survivable**: 6+ months of runway left, team of reasonable size,
  existing pipeline to replace 50% of revenue in 12 months.
- **Hard**: Layoff required, painful but company continues.
- **Existential**: Company wouldn't survive.

If the answer is "existential," you're in a crisis that needs
immediate action.

## How concentration happens

Common paths:
1. **Founder sells their first big customer themselves.** Customer
   becomes 40% of revenue at $1M ARR. Founder loves it. Then growth
   slows because they're not selling to the next segment.
2. **A small customer grows fast.** Expansion revenue happens, and
   suddenly 15% becomes 35%.
3. **One customer becomes reference, then dominant.** Their success
   drives your next 10 deals, all in a similar shape.
4. **Industry disruption creates one whale.** One hyperscaler customer
   in your vertical becomes a lock-in.

## Prevention

1. **Set concentration targets from $1M ARR.** No customer > 15% by
   $3M ARR.
2. **Explicit sales team goals**: split pipeline by segment size.
3. **Product roadmap review**: is any feature > 30% driven by one
   customer?
4. **Governance**: concentration is reviewed at board meetings from
   Series A onward.

## How to report to the board

If concentration is a concern, lead with it:
- Top 5 customers as % of ARR
- Trend over 4 quarters
- Contract expiration and status
- Action plan to reduce concentration

Don't let the board bring it up. Own it.

## The Series A/B trap

At Series A, investors tolerate 20–30% concentration if the customer
is high-quality, multi-year, and growing within.

At Series B, that same customer at 20% is a drag on valuation.

Between A and B, reduce concentration aggressively. It doesn't fix
itself through growth — only through directed effort.
