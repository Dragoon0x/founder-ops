# Red Flag: Founders Disagreeing Publicly

## The pattern
In the last 3 months, cofounders have:
- Given contradictory guidance to different teams
- Publicly disagreed in team meetings without resolution
- Been seen venting about each other to individual contributors
- Overturned each other's decisions
- Avoided scheduling 1:1s

Employees have started asking things like "which founder said that?"
or "what does [other founder] think?"

## Why it's a red flag
Founder breakdown is the #1 killer of companies between Seed and
Series B. More than market, more than competition, more than funding.

Team psychology reads founder dynamics accurately. If cofounders
aren't aligned, the team spends cycles navigating the conflict
instead of building.

## Diagnosing severity

### Level 1 (monitor)
- Occasional disagreement visible in meetings
- Both founders still participate in executive sessions
- Team still goes to either for decisions
- 1:1s still happening weekly
- Public comms are coordinated

### Level 2 (act within 1 month)
- Frequent public disagreement or contradictory guidance
- Team is starting to go to one founder and not the other
- 1:1s happening but tense
- Different executive team members are aligned with different founders
- Decisions are getting stuck or re-opened

### Level 3 (immediate action)
- Founders are avoiding each other
- Team is openly discussing the dynamic
- Decisions are being overturned days later
- Exec attrition has started
- Contracts or board members are being pitched differently by each founder

## What to do

### At Level 1
- Schedule a 90-minute "state of the relationship" conversation monthly.
- Have a shared doc for ongoing disagreements with a resolution deadline.
- Make roles and decision rights explicit (who decides what).

### At Level 2
- Get professional help. Not optional. Options:
  - **Executive coach** who has worked with cofounder teams.
  - **Therapist** experienced with business partnerships.
  - **Board member** who can mediate (risky but sometimes works).
- Reduce contact points: weekly founder sync + async on everything else.
- Document who decides what (Areas of Responsibility).
- Establish tiebreaker process (usually: CEO decides; if still stuck,
  board escalation).

### At Level 3
This is an existential situation.

Step 1: 72-hour pause. Both founders stop making public decisions.
Exec team is informed that there's a founder-level conversation underway.

Step 2: Bring in a neutral third party (experienced coach, ideally
with board support).

Step 3: Honest assessment. Options:
- **Recommit**: Clarify roles, write new agreements, resume operating
  with professional support.
- **Restructure**: One founder takes a different role (e.g., Chairman,
  advisor) with reduced operating responsibility.
- **Depart**: One founder exits with dignity, equity handoff, and
  narrative support.

Step 4: Communicate to the team with clarity. Don't drip information.
Don't lie. Don't over-share.

## The "recommit" path

Requires:
- Genuine willingness from both founders to change behavior.
- A clear Areas of Responsibility doc.
- Pre-committed decision rules.
- Monthly check-ins with a coach for 6+ months.
- Board support (knowing, not necessarily involved day-to-day).

Success rate: around 50% in my observation. Works when the underlying
values are aligned and the conflict is about style or scope.

Fails when: the underlying trust is gone, or one founder has lost
respect for the other.

## The "depart" path

If one founder leaves:
- **Equity handling**: honor vested shares, repurchase unvested per
  agreement.
- **Title transition**: "founder stepping back" is often cleaner than
  "founder fired."
- **Narrative for the team**: 1–2 sentences, consistent across audiences.
- **Narrative for investors**: call personally before any announcement.
- **Board transition**: decide whether departing founder keeps board seat.
- **Timing**: usually 4–8 weeks from decision to public.

## What NOT to do

1. **Don't pretend it's fine publicly.** The team knows.
2. **Don't involve employees as proxies.** They don't work for this.
3. **Don't make it about who's right.** The damage is the dynamic.
4. **Don't go to investors before you've decided on a path.** Present
   options, not problems.
5. **Don't defer indefinitely.** Founder conflict doesn't self-heal.

## How investors read this

Investors watch founder dynamics closely. Signals:
- Both founders don't show up at board meetings.
- Communication inconsistency between founders on the same call.
- Exec team turnover.
- Strategic inconsistency over quarters.

If investors ask, "how's the founder relationship?", take it seriously.
It's not small talk.

## Prevention from day 1

- Pre-commit: write founder agreement with roles, tie-breakers, exit
  terms.
- Weekly founder sync, protected time, no meeting.
- Annual "state of the relationship" review with a coach.
- Shared board relationship (both founders attend board meetings).
- Separate 1:1s with each investor (catches misalignment early).

## The hard truth

65% of cofounder teams see a cofounder depart within 4 years
(Wasserman, Founder's Dilemmas). It's common. It's survivable if handled
with dignity. The damage isn't the departure — it's the ambiguity and
the drift that often precede it.

If you see the pattern, act early. The kindest thing you can do for
your cofounder, your team, and yourself is to name it.
