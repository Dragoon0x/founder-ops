# Mode: International Expansion

## Purpose

Decide whether, when, where, and how to expand internationally.
Most companies expand too early or to the wrong country.

## When to Expand (the bar)

You're ready when ALL of these are true:

- [ ] Product-market fit confirmed in your home market
- [ ] $5M+ ARR (or equivalent traction signal)
- [ ] Net retention > 100% in home market
- [ ] You have customers from another region inbound (= demand exists)
- [ ] Operations team that can manage complexity (or you're hiring them)
- [ ] 12+ months runway after factoring expansion costs

**If any are false, fix that first.** Premature international = expensive distraction.

## Country Selection Framework

Score each candidate market 1-5:

| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Market size for our product** | 1-5 | TAM in this country |
| **Cultural similarity to home market** | 1-5 | Less translation = faster |
| **Language overlap** | 1-5 | English-speaking? Existing translations? |
| **Regulatory complexity** | 1-5 | GDPR? Local data residency? Industry-specific? |
| **Currency stability** | 1-5 | Hyperinflation risk? Capital controls? |
| **Tax/legal complexity** | 1-5 | VAT, employment law, tax treaty quality |
| **Existing demand signals** | 1-5 | Inbound from this market? |
| **Competitive landscape** | 1-5 | Local incumbents? Saturation? |
| **Time zone overlap with home** | 1-5 | Can you support customers? |
| **Talent availability for local hiring** | 1-5 | Can you hire here? |

**Total: /50**
- 40+: Strong candidate, prioritize
- 30-39: Worth a beachhead test
- < 30: Not yet

## Standard Expansion Sequences

**For US-based B2B SaaS:**
1. Canada (#1, easiest — same language, similar tax/legal)
2. UK / Ireland (English, GDPR baseline, good talent)
3. Australia / New Zealand (English, similar enterprise practices)
4. Western Europe (Germany, France, Netherlands — bigger but harder)
5. Latin America (timezone overlap, growing market, Spanish/Portuguese)
6. APAC (Singapore as hub, then JP/KR as targeted)

**For European SaaS:**
1. UK or Germany (depending on home market)
2. Other Western Europe
3. US (different beast — usually 2-3x rest of world combined)
4. Then APAC/LATAM as scale

## Expansion Models (pick ONE per market)

### 1. Distributed Remote (lowest cost)
- No local entity
- Hire as contractors via Deel, Remote.com, or local PEO
- Customer support via async tooling
- Sales via remote AEs
- **Cost:** ~$15-30K setup, ongoing employer-of-record fees
- **Best for:** First international expansion, soft launch

### 2. Local Beachhead Team (medium cost)
- Local entity (LLC equivalent)
- 1-3 local hires (sales lead + support)
- Local bank account, local entity, local payroll
- **Cost:** ~$50-150K setup, $200-500K/year minimum
- **Best for:** $1M+ in market revenue or pipeline

### 3. Full Subsidiary (highest cost)
- Multiple offices, full org structure
- 10+ employees, dedicated leadership
- Local marketing, sales, support, sometimes engineering
- **Cost:** $1M+ setup, $2-5M/year minimum
- **Best for:** $5M+ in market revenue

## The Localization Stack

What "going international" actually requires:

### Product
- [ ] UI translation (8-15 languages typically)
- [ ] Right-to-left support if Arabic/Hebrew
- [ ] Date/time/number formatting per locale
- [ ] Currency display + multi-currency support
- [ ] Local payment methods (SEPA in EU, Pix in Brazil, etc)
- [ ] Tax calculation (VAT in EU, GST elsewhere)

### Marketing
- [ ] Translated website (not Google Translate — proper translation)
- [ ] Local SEO content
- [ ] Local social media presence
- [ ] Local case studies (will take 6+ months)

### Sales/Support
- [ ] Local time zone coverage (or async-acceptable)
- [ ] Local language support (or English-acceptable)
- [ ] Local references for case studies
- [ ] Local payment terms (Net 60 in some markets vs Net 30 in US)

### Legal/Compliance
- [ ] Local entity (PEO acceptable for first stage)
- [ ] Privacy policy compliance (GDPR, CCPA, LGPD, etc)
- [ ] Data residency requirements
- [ ] Employment law compliance (huge variance)
- [ ] Contractor vs employee classification
- [ ] VAT/GST registration where required

### Operations
- [ ] Multi-currency invoicing
- [ ] Local payroll provider
- [ ] Local benefits provision
- [ ] Local accounting/bookkeeping
- [ ] Tax filings in each jurisdiction

## The First Local Hire (it matters more than anything)

Profile to hire FIRST in a new market:
- 5+ years experience in this market
- Has built/scaled something before (player-coach)
- Strong personal network in target customer base
- Comfortable with ambiguity and reporting to remote HQ
- Either sales OR partnerships background (NOT marketing as first hire)

**Do NOT hire as first international hire:**
- Senior strategist without operational experience
- Pure marketing without sales/BD
- Anyone who needs significant US/HQ ramp time
- Anyone who'll require a full local team to be productive

**Comp:** Pay 70-90% of local market rate for the role + equity. Going below = bad hires. Going above = signal to internal team that you're desperate.

## Common Expansion Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence | Fix |
|---------|-------------|-----|
| Translating without localizing | Looks foreign, doesn't convert | Cultural localization, not just language |
| Same pricing in $ everywhere | Misaligned with local purchasing power | Price per market |
| Same product in every market | Misses local needs | Local adaptation per market |
| Hiring HQ people who "moved to" target market | Won't have local network | Hire LOCAL people |
| Underestimating regulatory cost | $250K+ surprise bills | Talk to lawyers in target market BEFORE entering |
| Going too many places at once | Spreads too thin | One market at a time, beat it before next |
| Centralizing decisions in HQ | Local team has no agency | Empower local hires to make decisions |
