# Mode: Mergers & Acquisitions

## Purpose

Whether you're acquiring or being acquired. Most M&A deals destroy value.
Don't be one of them.

## Being Acquired (the more common case)

### Stage 1: Receiving Inbound Interest

Someone (corp dev person, banker, executive) reaches out about acquisition.

**First-call rules:**
- It's never "just a chat" — every call is data collection
- Don't volunteer numbers (revenue, customers, terms)
- Ask: who's the executive sponsor, what's their thesis, what's their timeline
- Don't commit to follow-up materials on first call
- Get advice before second call (lawyer, banker, advisor)

**Inbound types:**
- **Banker fishing:** Bank trying to win mandate. Low signal, high time-cost. Politely defer.
- **Strategic exploration:** Real interest from a buyer. Worth a 30-min call.
- **Acqui-hire interest:** Interested in your team, not your product. Different conversation.
- **Executive ego call:** CEO/founder wants to meet. May be real, may be ego. Probe.

### Stage 2: Should You Engage?

Reasons to engage:
- Your strategic option set is narrowing (struggling to raise, market shifting)
- An acquirer offers genuine acceleration of your mission (resources, distribution)
- Founder fatigue is real and current investors agree it's time
- Multiple inbound interest in 90 days (signal of market timing)

Reasons NOT to engage:
- "Just to see what we'd be worth" — wastes 6 months, leaks signals
- Pressure from one investor without board alignment
- During active fundraise — kills the raise
- Within 6 months of major company milestone — you'll be undervalued

### Stage 3: If Engaging, Run a Process

**Banker (mandatory for $50M+ deals):**
- Boutique bank if $50M-$500M (Qatalyst, GP Bullhound, etc)
- Bulge bracket if $500M+
- Cost: 2-5% of deal value, with minimum fees
- Their job: create competitive tension, optimize price + terms

**Process structure:**
1. **Preparation (4-8 weeks):** Banker prep, financial cleanup, narrative dev
2. **Outreach (2-4 weeks):** Approach 10-30 strategic + financial buyers
3. **Initial bids (4-6 weeks):** IOIs (Indications of Interest)
4. **Management presentations (2-4 weeks):** Top 3-5 buyers do diligence
5. **Final bids (2-4 weeks):** LOIs (Letters of Intent) with terms
6. **Negotiation + diligence (8-16 weeks):** Best bidder gets exclusive period
7. **Signing + closing (4-12 weeks):** Definitive agreements, regulatory approval

**Total: 6-12 months from engagement to close.**

### Stage 4: Term Sheet Review

Critical terms beyond the headline price:

| Term | What to Watch |
|------|--------------|
| **Total Consideration** | Cash vs stock vs earnout split |
| **Earnout** | Targets achievable? Control to hit them? |
| **Stock consideration** | Public vs private, lockup periods |
| **Escrow** | Amount, duration, indemnification scope |
| **Working capital adjustment** | Methodology can shift price by millions |
| **Founder equity treatment** | Acceleration, retention package, vesting |
| **Employee equity treatment** | Acceleration, retention pool, repricing |
| **Retention pool** | Size, vesting, allocation discretion |
| **Non-compete** | Duration, geographic scope, scope of activity |
| **Indemnification cap** | Total liability if reps & warranties breach |
| **Reps & warranties insurance** | Who pays, what's covered |
| **Closing conditions** | Regulatory, employee retention, customer consent |
| **Break fees** | If buyer walks, what do you get |

**Rule:** A $100M deal can become $60M or $130M based on these terms.

### Stage 5: Diligence Process

**What buyers will ask for:**
- Cap table + option grants + waterfall analysis
- Financial statements (3+ years)
- Customer contracts (every one, especially top 20)
- Employee agreements (every one)
- IP assignments (every contractor, every employee)
- Vendor contracts
- Litigation history
- Tax returns
- Insurance policies
- Privacy/security audits
- Open source license inventory
- Source code review

**Common diligence killers:**
- Missing IP assignments from early contractors
- Verbal co-founder agreements
- Customer contracts with non-assignment clauses
- Tax compliance issues (sales tax especially)
- Open source license violations (GPL contamination)
- Pending litigation, even small
- Major customer concentration (>20% from one customer)

**Run a "diligence pre-mortem" before going to market — 90% of issues are fixable in advance.**

## Being the Acquirer (less common at startup stage)

If you're acquiring (typically post-Series B):

### Pre-deal questions
- What problem does this acquisition solve that we can't solve organically?
- What would it cost to build this internally? In time + dollars?
- What's our integration plan, day 30?
- What happens to their team in year 1?
- What happens to their product in year 1?
- What happens to their customers in year 1?

### Common acquirer mistakes
- Buying for revenue (it'll churn)
- Buying for technology (you can't integrate it)
- Buying without an integration owner
- Letting the founder run the acquired entity unchanged
- Promising autonomy that violates your operating model

### Integration playbook
- **Day 0:** All-hands at acquired company, integration plan announced
- **Day 30:** Comp aligned, systems migrated, integration owner clear
- **Day 90:** Initial product integration milestones
- **Day 180:** Sales team fully integrated or fully autonomous (decision made on day 0)
- **Day 365:** Retention check — if founders/key people still there, it worked

## Acqui-Hires (different game)

If acquired primarily for the team:

- Price: typically $1-3M per engineer, $500K-$1M per non-engineer
- Founders: 1-2 year retention agreements, refreshed equity
- Product: usually wound down within 6-12 months
- Customers: notified, transitioned to refunds or alternative
- Investors: get returns proportional to liquidation preference (often <1x for late-stage)

**Founder reality check:** Acqui-hire is a soft landing, not a win. If your investors return less than they put in, they remember. Plan accordingly.
