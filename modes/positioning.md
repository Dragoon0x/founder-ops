# Mode: Positioning

## Purpose

Define the market you're competing in, the customer you serve, and what
makes you uniquely valuable. This is the foundation for every landing
page, sales call, and product decision.

## The Positioning Framework

Based on April Dunford's "Obviously Awesome":

1. **Competitive alternatives** — What would customers use if you didn't exist?
2. **Unique attributes** — Features/capabilities only you have
3. **Value** — Benefits that matter because of those attributes
4. **Who cares** — Customers who care MOST about that value
5. **Market category** — The context that makes your value obvious

## Step-by-Step

### Step 1: List Competitive Alternatives

Not just "competitors" — what do people do today?
- Direct competitors
- Indirect competitors (different category, same problem)
- DIY solutions (spreadsheets, scripts)
- Status quo (do nothing)

### Step 2: Isolate Unique Attributes

For each feature/capability, ask:
- Do competitors have this?
- Do alternatives have this?
- Is it genuinely hard to copy?

Keep only the attributes that are BOTH unique AND hard to replicate.

### Step 3: Map Attributes to Value

For each unique attribute, answer:
- So what? (What benefit does this create?)
- Why does the customer care?
- What outcome does this drive?

### Step 4: Identify Who Cares Most

Some customers value this more than others. Who:
- Has the pain most acutely?
- Will pay the most for it?
- Will evangelize it?
- Has the shortest sales cycle?

That's your ICP (Ideal Customer Profile).

### Step 5: Choose Market Category

The category frames HOW people think about your value.

| Category Choice | Effect |
|-----------------|--------|
| Existing category | Familiar but crowded |
| Adjacent category | Leverages existing demand |
| New category | Differentiation but education needed |

Pick the category where your unique value is MOST valuable.

## Positioning Statement

```
For [target customer]
who [specific pain or situation]
[Product] is a [market category]
that [key benefit]
Unlike [primary competitive alternative]
we [key differentiator]
```

**Example:**
> For engineering teams at mid-stage startups
> who are drowning in incident noise,
> IncidentIQ is an AI-powered incident response platform
> that routes only the incidents that matter to the right engineer.
> Unlike PagerDuty, we prioritize signal over volume —
> reducing false alarms by 80%+.

## ICP Definition

Beyond "our customer is a startup" — be specific:

```
## Ideal Customer Profile

### Firmographic
- Company size: [specific range]
- Industry: [specific]
- Revenue stage: [range]
- Geography: [markets]

### Technographic
- Current tools: [what they use now]
- Tech stack: [relevant stack indicators]

### Behavioral
- How they discover solutions: [channels]
- Who makes the buying decision: [role]
- How long their sales cycle is: [weeks/months]

### Psychographic
- What they value: [time/quality/price/simplicity]
- Their risk tolerance: [conservative/aggressive]
- Their pain intensity: [urgent/important/nice-to-have]

### Disqualifiers (NOT our customer)
- [Specific characteristic that makes them bad fit]
- [Specific characteristic that makes them bad fit]
```

## Value Proposition Testing

For every value prop, test:
- **Specificity**: Can you put a number on it?
- **Credibility**: Do you have evidence/case studies?
- **Relevance**: Does the target ICP care about this?
- **Differentiation**: Is it actually different from alternatives?

**Weak value prop:** "We help you save time and money"
**Strong value prop:** "Cut invoice processing from 3 days to 15 minutes"

## Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "For everyone" | Nobody |
| Feature-focused positioning | Commoditized |
| "Like [competitor] but better" | Uncreative |
| Vague benefits ("grow your business") | Meaningless |
| Positioning that never changes | Market shifts — you should too |
