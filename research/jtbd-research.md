# Jobs-to-be-done research

## Purpose
Find the situation that triggers people to look for a solution. JTBD reframes
"what feature" into "what job is the customer hiring this product to do."

## Switching interview (the core JTBD method)

Interview people who recently switched TO your product (or to a competitor) and
walk them backwards through the timeline of how they decided. The goal is to
identify the **forces** that drive switching:

1. **Push** of current situation (what made you start looking)
2. **Pull** of new solution (what attracted you)
3. **Anxiety** about switching (what made you hesitate)
4. **Habit** of present (what kept you stuck)

The hire-fire moment is when push + pull > anxiety + habit.

## Interview structure (90 min)

Walk the timeline in reverse:

1. "Tell me about the first day you used [our product]." — the hire moment
2. "Walk me back to when you decided to try it." — the trigger
3. "What was happening before you started looking?" — the push
4. "What did you try first? Why didn't it work?" — the consideration set
5. "What almost stopped you from switching?" — the anxiety
6. "What are you doing now that you couldn't do before?" — the success metric

## What you're looking for

- The **first thought** ("I should look for something else") — the trigger
- The **passive looking** vs **active looking** transition
- The **deciding event** — usually a specific failure of the old solution
- The **anxiety set** — the reasons not to switch (often more revealing than
  the reasons to switch)

## Outcomes

After 8-12 switching interviews per segment, you should have:
1. A taxonomy of triggers (the situations that put your category on the table)
2. A list of jobs (the outcomes people are trying to achieve)
3. A force diagram per segment (what gets people unstuck)
4. The competitive set people actually consider (often not what you think)

## Common errors

1. Interviewing only happy customers — you miss the anxiety set
2. Letting them generalize — "in general I look for..." Real research uses
   "the last time" stories only
3. Confusing job with feature — "I want a calendar" is a feature; "I want to
   stop double-booking my Saturdays" is a job
4. Stopping at the first job — most products serve a primary job and 2-4
   secondary jobs. Map all of them.

## When to revisit

- Quarterly for early-stage (PMF still forming)
- After significant price/positioning change
- After major competitive entrant
- When churn pattern shifts unexplainedly
