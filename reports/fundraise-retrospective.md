# Series A Fundraise Retrospective (Example)

**Round:** Series A, $14M at $55M post
**Started:** March 15, 2025
**Term sheet:** May 8, 2025 (8 weeks)
**Closed:** June 17, 2025 (13 weeks total)
**Investors:** 1 lead (Vesper Capital), 3 follow-ons

---

## What we wanted vs. what we got

| Target | Actual |
|---|---|
| $12-15M raise | $14M |
| $50-65M post | $55M |
| Close in 8 weeks | 13 weeks |
| 2-3 term sheets | 2 term sheets |
| Keep existing investors pro-rata | Yes, kept all |

Overall: good outcome, took longer than planned.

## What worked

1. **Deep prep before launching.** We spent 6 weeks before the process getting metrics clean, rebuilding the data room, rehearsing the pitch with 8 friendly investors, and getting to a "no more than 10% confusion" state in our story.
2. **Narrow investor list (22 firms).** We targeted funds with exactly the right stage thesis and 2-3 portfolio overlaps we liked. 18 agreed to first meetings.
3. **Tight timeline with hard deadline.** We told every firm the same timeline (4 weeks from first meeting to term sheet decision). 12 dropped out. The 6 who stayed were serious.
4. **Two concurrent partner meetings.** By structuring our process, we got two firms to partner meeting in the same week, which created price tension.

## What didn't work

1. **We underestimated how much our NDR story would get pushed on.** Our NDR had been declining and we thought we had a good explanation. Investors didn't buy it on first pass. We spent 3 weeks doing data work to produce cohort charts and a convincing remediation plan.
2. **We pitched too early.** We launched the process 2 weeks earlier than ideal because we were nervous about runway. Metrics were "fine" not "strong." In retrospect we should have waited for the Q1 close.
3. **Our second-choice lead had a faster process but worse terms.** We almost took it out of impatience. Glad we didn't.
4. **Reference calls took longer than expected.** Our lead did 8 customer references. Scheduling took 10 days.

## Advice to our future selves

1. Start prep 3 months before you plan to launch, not 6 weeks
2. Do 4-5 "test pitches" with friendly investors before you start the real process. Listen for objections. Fix them before they hit real deals.
3. Have a written answer to your 10 hardest questions before any first meeting
4. Don't launch in a soft month for your business — you'll get pushed on 1-quarter anomalies that don't predict anything
5. Ask for term sheet by a specific date. "This week" is not a deadline.

## By the numbers

- 22 firms in first meeting
- 11 advanced to second meeting
- 7 advanced to partner meeting
- 4 went to DD
- 2 term sheets
- 1 signed

## Time by activity

- Fundraise prep (before launch): 6 weeks
- First meetings: 3 weeks
- Partner meetings and diligence: 4 weeks
- Term sheet negotiation: 2 weeks
- Legal close: 4 weeks
- **Total founder time:** ~75% for 13 weeks

## What the process cost the business

- Two features slipped
- One key hire delayed by 6 weeks
- Two customer escalations I wasn't present for
- My co-founder handled 80% of the business while I fundraised

## Decision: would we do it again the same way?

Yes, with 3 tweaks:
1. Prep 3 months (not 6 weeks) before
2. Wait for stronger quarter before launching
3. Pre-commit a weekly co-founder sync to prevent context loss
