# Enter New Market or Deepen Current

## Context
You've got traction in one segment/vertical/geography. Expand or go deeper?

## The tree

### Step 1: Have you saturated your current market?
Saturation test:
- You've reached > 30% of addressable customers in your current ICP: saturation real. Expand.
- You've reached < 10%: huge room to grow. Deepen.
- Between: depends on growth rate + competitor moves.

### Step 2: Why do you want to expand?
- Growth is flatlining, need a new S-curve: legitimate reason. Test carefully.
- Bored or shiny-object: dangerous motivator.
- Big customer in new segment asked: one customer isn't a segment. Test more broadly.
- Competitive pressure in adjacent space: maybe. Study what they're doing first.
- Investors pushing expansion: investors don't live your operations. Think carefully.

### Step 3: Does the new market share customer / workflow / product DNA?
- YES (same customer, adjacent workflow): high leverage. Expand.
- NO (new customer, new product, new GTM): this is a new company inside your company. Hard.

### Step 4: Do you have the bandwidth to run two markets?
- Team of < 20: probably not. Focus wins.
- Team of 20-50: maybe one clear GM for the new segment.
- Team of 50+: yes if leaders identified.

### Step 5: What's the cost of failure?
Expand:
- Cost: $500K-$3M sunk in R&D/GTM for a new segment, 12-18 months
- Team cost: senior people pulled off core, distracted
- Focus cost: current market grows slower
- Reversibility: expensive but possible

Deepen:
- Cost: mostly opportunity
- Risk: competitor takes adjacent market while you deepen
- Reversibility: easier to pivot to expansion later

## Expansion types ranked by difficulty
- Same customer, new feature/upsell: easy
- Same customer, new pricing tier: easy
- Same buyer persona, adjacent vertical: medium
- Different buyer persona in same company: hard
- New geography (same vertical): hard
- New ICP entirely: very hard (often pivot-level)

## Common wrong answers
- "We'll just duplicate what worked in segment A into segment B." (Playbooks don't transfer clean.)
- "Bigger TAM is always better." (Bigger TAM with no clear path is just a bigger hallucination.)
- "Focus is for small companies." (Focus is what lets you dominate.)

## Frameworks to consult
- Geoffrey Moore: Crossing the Chasm
- Christensen: Innovator's Dilemma (careful when adjacent might be disruptive)
- Dunford: Obviously Awesome (positioning mechanics)

## Reversibility
- Expand and fail: you lose 12-18 months and often key hires. Recoverable but painful.
- Deepen and miss expansion window: competitor locks market, hard to recover.
