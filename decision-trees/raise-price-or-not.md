# Raise Price or Not

## Context
You're considering raising prices. New customers? Existing? By how much?

## The tree

### Step 1: Where does your pricing sit vs market?
- Below market (customers say "cheap"): raise. Probably 20-50%.
- At market: raise is optional. 5-15% annual is normal.
- Above market but strong differentiation: hold.
- Above market with weak differentiation: danger. Fix differentiation before raising.

### Step 2: What's your churn rate?
- < 2% monthly SMB / < 10% annual enterprise: healthy. Raising ok.
- Higher: raising pours fuel on churn fire. Fix retention first.

### Step 3: What's your win rate on price?
- Winning > 50% of qualified deals: price is not the constraint. Raise safely.
- Winning 30-50%: mixed. Raise for new logos, grandfather existing.
- Winning < 30%: price may be correct; your problem is conversion. Don't raise.

### Step 4: When did you last raise?
- Never: you're almost certainly too cheap. Raise.
- > 18 months: overdue.
- 6-12 months: may be too soon. Need specific reason.
- < 6 months: too soon unless under-priced badly.

### Step 5: Are new customers paying more than old?
If yes and the gap is > 20%, grandfathered pricing is leaving money on the table. Raise legacy prices with notice.
If no, your market may not support a raise.

## Mechanics of raising

### New customers only
Easiest. No customer conversations needed. Flip price today.

### Existing + new
Notice period: 60-90 days minimum for enterprise, 30 days for SMB.
Communication:
- Lead with "why": improvements shipped, costs rising, value proven
- Show the math (per-seat, per-unit)
- Offer loyalty discount: lock in at current for 12 more months if they sign annual
- Offer upgrade paths: higher tier unlocks more at small premium

### Raising on annual renewals only
Good middle ground. Existing customers keep old price until renewal. Easy to communicate.

## Expected fallout
- 5-15% price increase on existing: < 5% churn reaction typical
- 20-30% price increase: 5-15% churn reaction
- > 30% price increase: 15-30% churn reaction (if communicated poorly)

Model it. Is net revenue positive? Usually yes at modest increases.

## Common wrong answers
- "If we raise, customers will leave." (Small minority will. Most absorb.)
- "Let's wait until we ship [feature]." (Now is better than later.)
- "Everyone will renegotiate." (Some will. Handle one at a time.)
- "Don't raise because one customer complained." (One customer != signal.)

## Don'ts
- Don't raise prices AND cut support AND ship breaking changes at once. Choose your timing.
- Don't raise without notice. Never spring a surprise.
- Don't raise prices for current customers paying for themselves if you have churn issues elsewhere. Fix first.

## Reversibility
- Raise → backtrack: painful, signals weakness. Avoid.
- Hold and regret: common. Raising slightly too often is less damaging than raising too rarely.
