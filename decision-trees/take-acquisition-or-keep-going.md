# Take Acquisition or Keep Going

## Context
You have an acquisition offer. Take it or keep building?

## The tree

### Step 1: Does the offer clear your cap stack?
Calculate preferred stack × multiples. If offer < preferred stack, founders and common holders get little or nothing. You may have a fiduciary duty to consider but it's a bad offer.

### Step 2: What does the founder outcome look like?
- At the offer price, after preferred pays out: what does each founder net?
- Life-changing amount (> 5x your current liquid net worth)? Take more seriously.
- Modest amount (< 2x)? Usually not enough to sell unless you're burned out or company is stuck.

### Step 3: What's the counterfactual in 3-5 years?
Honest model:
- Base case: what are you reasonably on track for in 3-5 years?
- Upside: what's the 10% outcome? 1%?
- Downside: what if growth stalls or market turns?

If base case is 2-3x this offer in 3-5 years, keeping going is reasonable. If base case is equal or less, selling rational.

### Step 4: What's your personal state?
- Excited, energy rising, team strong: push on.
- Tired, losing faith, no clear next chapter: acquisition may be right exit.
- Burned out but still committed: take a sabbatical before deciding. Don't sell while exhausted.

### Step 5: Who else is in the decision?
- Co-founders: unanimous? Split decision usually breaks companies either way.
- Investors: some may block below preferred stack liquidation threshold.
- Team: they're not in the decision but you hold the bag for their outcome.

### Step 6: What's the acquirer's track record?
- Great home (strategic, cultural fit, founders stay, products grow): take seriously.
- Mid home (strategic fit, culture mismatch, typical 2-year retention expected): take at the right price.
- Bad home (acquirer that kills acquisitions, will mistreat team): high price required to justify.

## Red flags on the offer
- Earn-outs > 30% of price. Assume earn-outs pay 50% of stated at best.
- Retention > 24 months with tight restrictions.
- Key-person clauses that trap you personally.
- No reps and warranties insurance (they want recourse against founders personally).
- Contingent on customers/revenue they haven't diligenced.

## Green flags
- Full cash or highly-liquid stock.
- Earn-outs structured with clear, measurable milestones you control.
- Reasonable retention (12-18 months at minimum autonomy).
- Good home for the team (roles defined, no layoffs day 1).
- Reps & warranties insurance (industry standard).

## Tiebreakers
- What would you regret more in 10 years: selling and walking away, or not selling and watching the company fade?
- Talk to 3 founders who sold their company — not to their friends who always say "you should have held." To people who saw the decision up close.

## Common wrong answers
- "We can always raise more." (Until you can't.)
- "This is just the first offer; better will come." (Sometimes. Often not.)
- "Let's see if they'll pay 50% more." (Counter-offer reasonable. Gaming repeatedly kills deals.)
- "We should take it; it's a life-changing amount." (For how many people? If only 2 founders, not enough reason.)

## Reversibility
- Sell: fully irreversible for the company. Personal reversibility depends on how free you are post-sale.
- Don't sell: reversible if offer stays open, often doesn't.
