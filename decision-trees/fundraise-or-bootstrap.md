# Fundraise or Bootstrap

## Context
You're at the point where you can raise external money but you also might not need to. Or: you're bootstrapped and someone just offered a term sheet.

## The tree

### Step 1: Can your business unit economics be funded by customers?
- YES, clearly (gross margin > 60%, CAC payback < 12 months, existing cash flow supports growth): proceed to Step 2
- NO (high burn to acquire each customer, long payback, negative cash gross margin): you probably need capital. Proceed to Step 4.
- MAYBE (economics are improving but not clearly there): proceed to Step 2 but flag honestly.

### Step 2: What's your desired outcome?
- **Control of life + modest wealth + flexibility**: bootstrap-friendly path is real. Skip to "Bootstrap checklist."
- **Maximum outcome at any cost**: fundraising usually correlates with bigger outcomes in large markets. Proceed to Step 3.
- **Category-defining company**: fundraise. You can't category-define on bootstrap pace in 95% of markets.

### Step 3: Is the market timing-sensitive?
- YES (winner-take-most, land grab, network effects, regulatory window closing): fundraise or lose.
- NO (steady market, long-term buyer relationships, no strong network effects): bootstrap is real option.

### Step 4: Have you actually tested bootstrap viability?
Most people who say "we need to raise" haven't tested:
- Can you get to $50K MRR by selling to 10 customers over 6 months while bootstrapping?
- Have you tried pricing 2-5x higher to a narrower customer?
- Can you do consulting/services to fund the product build for 12-18 months?

If you haven't tested, the answer isn't "fundraise." The answer is "test bootstrap for 3-6 months."

### Step 5: What do you actually need the money for?
Be honest:
- Talent (engineers / sales): bootstrap slower but possible
- Market land-grab (branding, SEM, distribution): usually requires capital
- R&D with long payoff (deeptech, hardware, biotech): almost always requires capital
- Runway for optionality: you should raise only if you have a plan, not for comfort

## Tiebreakers
- **Your personal financial situation.** If you need equity liquidity in < 3 years, bootstrap is harder (no exit, no secondary usually). If you can wait 7-10 years, both work.
- **Co-founder alignment.** If one of you wants VC, the other wants bootstrap — this will break you. Decide together or fracture later.
- **Your learning curve.** Bootstrapping teaches sales, finance, and ops viscerally. VC teaches scaling, hiring, and team-building. Which do you need more?

## Common wrong answers (under pressure)
- "We should raise because everyone else is." (Peer pressure)
- "We can always raise later if bootstrap doesn't work." (Sometimes true, often not. Your first year of metrics anchors your narrative.)
- "Investors called us, so we should take it." (Inbound != signal. Check the fit.)
- "Bootstrap is for lifestyle businesses." (Not true. 37signals, Mailchimp, Calendly — bootstrap champions.)

## Reversibility
- Bootstrap → raise later: easy if you grow well, hard if you stall.
- Raise → bootstrap: not really reversible. You've taken fiduciaries.
