# Pivot or Persevere

## Context
Your current strategy isn't working as hoped. Pivot, persevere, or die.

## The tree

### Step 1: How long have you actually tested the current bet?
- < 6 months: too early to pivot unless catastrophic signal. Persevere with better experiments.
- 6-18 months: actionable window. Proceed.
- 18+ months with no product-market-fit: high bias to pivot.

### Step 2: What's your evidence of traction?
Signal of product-market-fit (any two of):
- 40%+ of users would be "very disappointed" without you (Sean Ellis test)
- Organic pull — users bringing others without your effort
- Retention curve flattens above 20-40% depending on category
- Negative churn cohorts (expansion > contraction)
- NPS > 40 consistently
- Inbound demand outpacing sales capacity
- Someone is paying you meaningfully without heavy discount

None of the above? You don't have PMF. Pivot pressure is real.
Some? Deepen and iterate. Not pivot.
All? Don't pivot — scale.

### Step 3: What's the nature of the miss?
- **Market wrong**: target audience doesn't actually have the pain. Pivot hard. Change WHO you serve.
- **Problem wrong**: target has pain but it's a different pain. Pivot. Change WHAT you solve.
- **Solution wrong**: right problem, wrong approach. Iterate solution, not pivot.
- **Pricing wrong**: people want it but can't buy at your price. Repricing, not pivot.
- **GTM wrong**: product works but you can't reach customers. GTM pivot (channel, motion), not product pivot.
- **Team wrong**: can't execute. Different problem — personnel, not strategy.

### Step 4: What's the cost of pivot?
Calculate:
- Runway impact: pivots take 3-12 months to show new traction
- Team impact: some people joined for the original mission
- Investor impact: loss of narrative consistency
- Brand impact: you've built name recognition in current space

If pivot requires > 12 months of runway and you have < 15 months: different conversation (raise bridge, cut costs, small pivot rather than reset).

### Step 5: Is there a pivot that preserves assets?
Great pivots keep:
- The team's skills (not requiring new technical DNA)
- Part of the tech (not a full rewrite)
- Customer relationships (not starting from zero)
- Brand (compatible repositioning)

Slack from Tiny Speck, Twitter from Odeo, Instagram from Burbn — all preserved skills/tech/audience.

If pivot discards 80%+ of what you've built, it's not a pivot. It's a new company. Be honest about that.

## Tiebreakers
- **Do you actually believe in the pivot?** If you don't, don't do it. Half-hearted pivots fail.
- **Does your best hire stay?** If they leave on the pivot, the pivot is weak.
- **Does your best customer follow?** If your strongest user won't use v2, that's a signal.

## Common wrong answers
- "Let's pivot because growth is slow this month." (Noise, not signal.)
- "Customer said they want X, let's build X." (One customer != market.)
- "Everyone in our space is doing Y now, let's do Y." (Late = expensive + undifferentiated.)
- "Persevere — we just need more effort." (Effort doesn't fix wrong-market.)

## Reversibility
- Pivot → unpivot: possible in 3-6 months if signal is clear. Painful on team.
- No pivot → pivot later: always possible but runway erodes while you wait.

## Running the actual decision
- Write your bear case honestly. Share with 2-3 advisors.
- Run a 30-day "pre-pivot" sprint: what would v2 look like? Mock it up, talk to 20 customers.
- Set a specific date: "By [date], if [metric] isn't [threshold], we commit to pivot."
