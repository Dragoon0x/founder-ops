# Decision: Single Product vs Multi-Product

## Context
You've found PMF with product A. Should you build product B?

## Decision Framework

### Stay single-product when:
- You haven't fully captured your TAM (< 30% market share)
- Your core product still has significant feature gaps
- Your team is < 50 people (focus is your biggest advantage)
- NRR is below 110% (fix retention before adding products)
- You don't have a clear distribution advantage for product B

### Go multi-product when:
- You're seeing diminishing returns on core product investment
- Customers are asking for adjacent capabilities and buying elsewhere
- You have a distribution moat (existing customer base > 1,000 accounts)
- Product B has a natural cross-sell / upsell motion from product A
- You can fund a dedicated team (5-8 people) without starving product A

## The $100M ARR litmus test
Can your current product reach $100M ARR on its own?
- If yes: stay focused until you're at $50M+ before adding products
- If no: either the market is too small (consider pivot) or you need product expansion

## Multi-product pitfalls
1. **Premature diversification**: Building product B at $2M ARR because product A growth is slowing. The problem is usually distribution, not product breadth.
2. **Cannibalization**: Product B competes with product A for the same budget. Make sure they expand the pie, not slice it differently.
3. **Org complexity**: Each product needs a PM, designer, and eng team. At 30 people, you can barely staff one product well.
4. **Integration tax**: Products that don't work well together are worse than separate point solutions. Integration is the value prop, not the afterthought.

## Review triggers
- Revisit annually
- Revisit if NRR drops below 100% (expansion within product A failing)
- Revisit if a competitor launches a multi-product bundle
