# Retention Curves

The cohort retention chart is the single most honest view of product quality.

## The chart

X-axis: time since signup (days, weeks, or months).
Y-axis: % of that cohort still active.

Each line = one cohort. Stack many cohorts to see trend.

## What shape should it be

**Good retention curve:**
- Drops steeply in first period (onboarding churn)
- Flattens to a stable band
- Stays flat or rises (for subscription + expansion)

**Bad retention curve:**
- Keeps dropping indefinitely (no retention floor — no PMF)
- Flattens near zero (product doesn't stick)
- Varies wildly between cohorts (no consistency — often due to changing onboarding)

## Smile curve (the best)

The "smile curve" drops initially, then rises. Why it rises:
- Users who stuck around activate more features
- Reactivated users come back
- Expansion (for revenue-based retention)

Companies with smile curves are nearly impossible to kill. Facebook, Slack, Figma all have smile curves.

## Retention floor

The flattened level of a retention curve = retention floor.

- < 20% floor at 6 months: unlikely PMF
- 20-40% floor: probable PMF for that product category
- 40-60% floor: strong product
- > 60% at 6 months: exceptional

Floor levels vary by category. SaaS B2B floors 60-80%. Consumer social 20-40%. Consumer content 10-30%.

## Cohort analysis by dimension

Segment cohorts by:

- **Acquisition channel** — is organic better than paid?
- **Signup persona** — admin vs end user
- **Feature usage at activation** — what correlates with retention?
- **Cohort month** — is retention improving over time as product gets better?

## Retention vs churn

Retention = % of users still active at time N.

Churn = 1 - retention (at any given period).

Churn rate usually reported monthly or annually. Retention curves show the shape.

Both are useful. Churn rate is for reporting. Curves are for understanding.

## Week-1 retention (consumer)

For consumer products, week-1 retention is the single most predictive metric.

Median among top-quartile consumer apps: 40-60% week-1.
Median bottom quartile: <20%.

If week-1 is low, everything else is hypothetical.

## Day-1 retention (consumer mobile)

Mobile is even more retention-sensitive. Benchmarks:

- Day 1: 30-50%
- Day 7: 10-25%
- Day 30: 5-15%

These are brutal numbers. Games are best-case; utility apps are worst-case.

## Retention improvements

Biggest levers (by impact):

1. **Onboarding redesign.** Often 10-30% absolute improvement.
2. **Activation optimization.** Compounds through retention.
3. **Habit-forming features.** Streaks, notifications, reminders.
4. **Core product quality.** Slow to move but structural.
5. **Content/value cadence.** Fresh reasons to return.

## Cohort maturity

Don't compare young cohorts to old cohorts on total revenue. Compare at same age.

A 3-month-old cohort with $10 cumulative revenue per user vs a 12-month-old cohort with $40 cumulative = probably same underlying pattern, just different maturity.
