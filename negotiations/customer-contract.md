# Negotiation: Enterprise Customer Contract

## Preparation
- Know your standard pricing and discount authority
- Understand the customer's budget cycle and decision timeline
- Research the customer's company size, growth, and competitive alternatives
- Know your BATNA: what if this deal doesn't close?

## Key Terms

### Pricing & Payment
- List price is the starting point. Expect enterprise customers to ask for 10-30% discount.
- Discount authority: Sales rep up to 10%, VP Sales up to 20%, CEO up to 30%
- Anything beyond 30% requires a strategic justification (case study, logo value, multi-year commitment)
- Payment terms: Net 30 is standard. Large enterprises may push for Net 60 or Net 90.
- Annual prepay vs quarterly: offer 5-10% discount for annual prepay

### Contract length
- Standard: 1 year, auto-renewing
- Multi-year: offer for 5-15% additional discount
- Never do monthly for enterprise (too much churn risk, too little commitment)
- Include a 90-day termination-for-convenience clause (protects both sides)

### SLA
- Commit to specific uptime (99.9% for most B2B SaaS)
- Define response times by severity
- Include credits for SLA breaches (typically 5-10% of monthly fee per breach, capped at 30%)
- Never commit to SLA you can't measure and deliver

### Data & Security
- Be ready for security questionnaire (50-200 questions typical)
- Data processing agreement (DPA) for GDPR/CCPA compliance
- SOC 2 Type II report (if you have it, share it; if not, timeline to get it)
- Data residency requirements (some customers require data in specific geography)

### Legal
- Limitation of liability: cap at 12 months of fees paid (standard)
- Indemnification: mutual indemnification for IP claims
- Insurance: carry $1-5M in cyber liability insurance

## Tactics
1. Negotiate on value, not price. "The ROI is $X, the cost is $Y, that's Z:1 return."
2. Bundle: if they want a discount, ask for something in return (case study, referral, multi-year)
3. Walk the procurement process early: "What does your buying process look like?" Understanding their internal process prevents surprises.
4. Find the champion AND the economic buyer. Champion wants the product. Buyer controls the budget. You need both.
5. Set a decision deadline: "We can hold this pricing through [date]." Not a threat, a genuine capacity constraint.
