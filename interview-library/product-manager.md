# Interview Questions: Product Manager (Senior/Staff)

## Overall loop
1. Recruiter (20 min)
2. Hiring manager (45 min)
3. Product sense case study (60 min)
4. Analytical/data case study (60 min)
5. Execution / craft interview (45 min)
6. Cross-functional: eng, design, sales (45 min each)
7. CEO or VP Product (30 min)
8. References (3 minimum)

Total: 5–7 hours of candidate time, 10–15 hours of team time.

## Questions by category

### Product sense (pick 3)
1. "Pick a product you use regularly. What would you change and why?"
2. "How would you improve [our product's flow]?"
3. "Tell me about a product decision you made that turned out to be wrong."
4. "Walk me through how you'd prioritize the roadmap for the next
   quarter given these 5 opportunities."
5. "A customer is asking for feature X. Walk me through your thought
   process."

**What good looks like:**
- Starts with user and problem, not solution.
- Explicit tradeoffs.
- Acknowledges what they don't know.
- Opinions rooted in specific user signals or data.
- Suggests testing rather than building.

**Red flags:**
- Jumps to solutions.
- Copies what competitors do.
- "The user said they want X, so we'll build X."
- No framework for prioritization.
- Everything feels important.

### Analytical / data (pick 3)
1. "Walk me through a metric you own. How do you measure it? What
   drives it?"
2. "You see MAU drop 15% month over month. Walk me through your
   investigation."
3. "How do you size a new opportunity?"
4. "Tell me about an A/B test you ran. What did you learn?"
5. "Describe a metric you pushed back on. Why?"

**What good looks like:**
- Numbers come out unprompted.
- Investigations are structured (decompose, hypothesis, test).
- Knows the limits of their data.
- Skeptical of vanity metrics.
- Can talk about metrics beyond "conversion rate."

**Red flags:**
- "We didn't really measure it."
- Single framework for every investigation.
- Treats all data as gospel.
- No experience pushing back on a metric.

### Execution (pick 3)
1. "Walk me through a feature you shipped end-to-end. Start to launch."
2. "Tell me about a deadline you missed. What happened?"
3. "Describe a time you had to kill a feature partway through. Why?"
4. "How do you handle disagreements with engineering or design?"
5. "What's your approach to writing specs? Show me an example."

**What good looks like:**
- Specific timeline (weeks, not "some time").
- Clear ownership and handoffs.
- Killed features in their history (rare and good).
- Disagreements resolved through data, not seniority.
- Specs are thoughtful but not overly long.

**Red flags:**
- "We shipped on time, everything was great."
- Never killed a feature.
- Disagreements resolved by "aligning with leadership."
- Specs are either non-existent or 30 pages.

### Leadership and communication (pick 2)
1. "Tell me about a time you aligned stakeholders on a controversial
   decision."
2. "Describe how you communicate with engineering vs executives."
3. "How do you handle a request from a salesperson to build a specific
   feature for a specific customer?"
4. "Tell me about your relationship with design."

**What good looks like:**
- Alignment through data + shared context.
- Adjusts communication style to audience.
- Handles sales requests with framework (is this strategic? is this
  replicable? will other customers want it?).
- Respects design as a partner, not a service.

**Red flags:**
- Alignment through authority.
- Same communication to everyone.
- Builds what sales asks for, always.
- Design is "helpful."

### Startup-specific (if relevant)
1. "Why startup over big company?"
2. "What's your comfort with ambiguity?"
3. "How do you prioritize when everything is important?"
4. "Tell me about the smallest team you've shipped with."
5. "What broke at your last company as it scaled?"

## Product sense case study (60 min)

Pick one:
- "Design an X for Y" (e.g., "Design a feature for our product that
  increases activation.")
- "Improve our product." (With public access to your product.)
- "Launch a new product in an adjacent space."

Frame:
1. First 5 min: goals, users, constraints (do they ask?)
2. Next 20 min: exploration of solution space (breadth)
3. Next 20 min: deep-dive on one solution (depth)
4. Last 15 min: metrics, risks, rollout

**What good looks like:**
- Spends first 5–10 min on problem, not solution.
- Names 3+ user segments and picks one intentionally.
- Explores multiple solutions before committing.
- Picks one and justifies (not necessarily the flashiest).
- Lists metrics that would tell them it worked (and didn't).
- Identifies risks and mitigation.
- Acknowledges tradeoffs.

**Red flags:**
- Jumps to a solution in the first 5 min.
- One-user-segment thinking.
- Solution is a rebuild of a competitor feature.
- No metrics proposed.
- No risk assessment.

## Analytical case study (60 min)

Format: given real or realistic data, investigate a problem.

Example: "Our weekly active users dropped 18% last month. Walk me
through your investigation."

**What good looks like:**
- Decomposes: overall vs segments.
- Forms hypotheses before looking at data.
- Tests hypotheses systematically.
- Knows which hypotheses to pursue first (impact × likelihood).
- Communicates findings clearly.

**Red flags:**
- Random data exploration.
- "I'd look at everything."
- Doesn't articulate a hypothesis.
- Stops at first plausible cause.

## Cross-functional interviews

### Engineering
- "How do you work with eng on estimates?"
- "Give me an example of when you changed scope mid-project."
- "How do you handle a PM/eng disagreement?"

### Design
- "How do you collaborate with designers?"
- "Tell me about a decision where you and the designer disagreed."
- "How do you give design feedback?"

### Sales/CS (for B2B PMs)
- "How do you handle customer feature requests?"
- "Tell me about time you said no to a big customer."
- "How do you prioritize across customer segments?"

## Reference check script
3 references minimum, including:
- Direct manager (most important)
- Engineering lead they worked with
- Peer PM or cross-functional leader

Ask:
1. "What was their biggest impact?"
2. "What did they struggle with?"
3. "How did they handle disagreement?"
4. "Would you hire them again at a more senior level?"
5. "Anything I should know that I probably haven't asked?"

## Decision framework

After the loop:
1. Would I want this person driving a critical product area in 3
   months?
2. Will they grow into the next level in 18 months?
3. Do they raise the bar for product craft on the team?
4. Do they bring a perspective our team is missing?

All four should be yes.

## Common mistakes in PM hiring

1. **Over-indexing on background.** Big company PM ≠ startup PM.
2. **Under-testing execution.** Sense is necessary but not sufficient.
3. **Not testing analytical rigor.** Most PMs fail here, not in sense.
4. **Skipping references.** Most PM hiring mistakes surface in
   references.
5. **Hiring the best sense with weakest execution.** At early stage,
   execution wins.
