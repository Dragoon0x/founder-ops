# Term Sheet Economics

The valuation number gets all the attention. The terms do most of the damage.

## Liquidation preferences

Standard: 1x non-participating preferred.

- **1x non-participating:** at exit, investor chooses either (a) their money back, or (b) convert to common and take their pro rata.
- **1x participating:** investor gets their money back AND their pro rata of what's left.
- **2x, 3x preferences:** investor gets 2-3x money back before anyone else.

Scenario: $10M invested at $40M pre ($50M post, 20% ownership). Exit at $60M.

| Term | Investor gets | Common gets |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| 1x non-participating | Chooses $12M (20% of $60M) | $48M |
| 1x participating | $10M + 20% of $50M = $20M | $40M |
| 2x non-participating | $20M | $40M |
| 2x participating | $20M + 20% of $40M = $28M | $32M |

Participating preferred after $60M exit takes ~$8M more from founders than non-participating. Compounded across multiple rounds, this is enormous.

## Anti-dilution

Protects investor if you raise a down round (next round at lower valuation).

- **Full ratchet:** investor's conversion price ratchets DOWN to new price. Very punitive to founders. Rare outside distressed situations.
- **Broad-based weighted average:** formula-based adjustment, less punitive. Market standard.
- **Narrow-based weighted average:** harsher than broad-based. Uncommon.

Accept broad-based. Fight full ratchet hard.

## Board composition

At seed: founder-friendly (2 founders, 1 investor, or similar).
At A: often 2-2-1 (founders, investors, independent).
At B+: usually investor-majority or balanced.

The independent seat is usually the swing vote on hire/fire CEO, approve budget, approve sale. Pick carefully.

## Protective provisions

Actions that require investor approval:
- Sale of company
- Changes to certificate of incorporation
- Issuing new preferred with senior preferences
- Increasing option pool size
- Major debt
- Material change to business

These are reasonable. Fight to keep list short. Watch for provisions that require consent for normal operating decisions (hiring above X, spending above Y) — these limit you.

## Pro rata rights

Investor's right to invest in future rounds to maintain their %. Standard. Give it to lead + any strategic angels. Don't give it to every small check.

## Redemption rights

Investor can force you to buy back their shares after N years (usually 5-7). Rare in US venture, common in some EU and Asian deals. Avoid. If forced, negotiate long timeline and low redemption multiple.

## Founder vesting reset

Many term sheets reset or extend founder vesting at round close. Accept reset only in exchange for material concessions elsewhere. Never accept longer than 4-year vest from round.

## Drag-along

Majority preferred can force minority to sell in acquisition. Standard. Make sure the threshold is reasonable (usually majority of preferred AND majority of common).

## Information rights

Investor gets monthly/quarterly financials, annual budget, audited statements. Standard. Minor effort to comply.

## MFN (Most Favored Nation)

Investor gets same terms as any future investor if better. Common for early angels. Rare in priced rounds.

## The real question

Which terms would matter if things go badly? That's where to spend negotiation capital.

- Liquidation preference (hugely matters if exit is low)
- Anti-dilution (matters if down round)
- Board composition (matters in disputes)
- Protective provisions (matters in pivots)

Valuation matters only if you exit well. Terms matter most if you don't.
