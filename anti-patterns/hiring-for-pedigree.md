# Anti-Pattern: Hiring for Pedigree

## What It Looks Like
Optimizing for brand-name employers (FAANG, McKinsey, Goldman) and top-tier universities over relevant skills, culture fit, and startup aptitude.

## Why It Happens
- Social proof bias: "If Google hired them, they must be good"
- Investor signaling: "Our team is from [impressive companies]"
- Risk aversion: "At least we know they can work at a high bar"
- Founder's own network is pedigree-heavy

## Detection Signals
- Job descriptions emphasize "X years at a top-tier company"
- Interview process tests for knowledge breadth, not execution depth
- Rejection reasons are often "didn't feel senior enough" without specific evidence
- New hires struggle with ambiguity and lack of process
- Team homogeneity (same schools, same companies, same backgrounds)

## Why It Fails at Startups
Big-company operators are trained to optimize existing systems. Startups need people who build systems from zero. These are fundamentally different skills.

The best startup hire is someone who:
- Has done this specific job at a similar-stage company
- Can work without infrastructure, process, or a team of 50
- Gets energy from ambiguity (not stressed by it)
- Moves fast without permission
- Has a portfolio of work, not just a list of employers

## Recovery Playbook
1. **Rewrite job descriptions**: Remove company-name requirements. Add: "Show us what you've built"
2. **Work-sample interviews**: Give a realistic task. Evaluate the output, not the resume.
3. **Reference-check for startup fit**: Ask references: "How does this person handle ambiguity? Do they wait for direction or create it?"
4. **Diverse sourcing**: Recruit from non-traditional backgrounds. Bootcamp grads, career changers, community college → state school paths.
5. **90-day evaluation**: Judge based on output at your company, not reputation from their last one
