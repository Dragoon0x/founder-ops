# Anti-Pattern: Vanity Metrics

## What It Looks Like
Celebrating numbers that look impressive but don't correlate with business health. Total signups, page views, downloads, social media followers. Numbers that only go up.

## Why It Happens
- Vanity metrics are easy to measure and always look good
- They make for great press releases and investor updates
- The real metrics are harder to define and sometimes embarrassing
- Teams optimize for what's measured, and vanity metrics are what's measured

## Detection Signals
- Board deck leads with "total users" or "total revenue" (cumulative, not periodic)
- Growth is measured in absolute terms, not rates
- Retention metrics are absent or buried
- Team celebrates signups without tracking activation
- Revenue charts use a different y-axis scale every quarter (visual manipulation)

## Vanity vs Actionable Metrics

| Vanity Metric | Actionable Alternative |
|--------------|----------------------|
| Total signups | Weekly activated users |
| Total revenue (cumulative) | MRR + MoM growth rate |
| Page views | Conversion rate by page |
| App downloads | DAU/MAU ratio |
| Email list size | Email engagement rate |
| Social followers | Social → signup conversion |
| Features shipped | Feature adoption rate |
| Total customers | Net revenue retention |

## Recovery Playbook
1. **Define your 3-5 core metrics**: These should directly measure business health (revenue, retention, activation, efficiency)
2. **Kill vanity from reporting**: Remove total/cumulative numbers from board decks and internal dashboards
3. **Rate over absolute**: Always report growth rates, not just numbers
4. **Cohort everything**: Don't look at blended averages. Look at how each customer cohort behaves over time
5. **One metric that matters**: At any given stage, identify the single most important metric and focus the company on it
