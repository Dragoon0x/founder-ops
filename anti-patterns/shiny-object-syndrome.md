# Anti-Pattern: Shiny Object Syndrome

## What It Looks Like
The founder has a new strategic priority every 2-3 weeks. The team is constantly pivoting between initiatives. Nothing gets finished. Everyone is exhausted and confused about what matters.

## Why It Happens
- Founder is an "idea person" (lots of vision, less discipline)
- New information triggers new direction (meeting with one customer → "we should pivot!")
- Fear of missing out on market opportunities
- Boredom: the hard middle part of execution isn't as exciting as the start
- Confusing activity with progress

## Detection Signals
- Company priorities change > 2x per quarter
- Multiple "top priority" projects running simultaneously (> 3)
- Team can't articulate the company's current strategic focus
- Meeting notes from 6 months ago describe a completely different strategy
- Founder uses phrases like "I just had a great idea" more than weekly

## The Cost
Every strategy change costs:
- 1-2 weeks of team context-switching
- Abandoned work (sunk cost)
- Credibility erosion (team stops believing the current priority will last)
- Hiring difficulty (candidates ask "what's the strategy?" and get a different answer each time)

## Recovery Playbook
1. **Commit to a 90-day bet**: Write down the one thing that matters most for the next 90 days. Put it on the wall. Every new idea gets evaluated against it: "Does this help the 90-day bet?"
2. **Idea parking lot**: When the founder has a new idea, it goes in the parking lot doc. It gets reviewed quarterly, not immediately.
3. **Decision journal**: For each strategy shift, write down: why the old strategy was wrong, what evidence supports the new one, what would have to be true for the new one to work. This forces rigor.
4. **Team feedback loop**: Ask the team quarterly: "How clear is our strategy?" If the answer is < 7/10, you're changing too often.
5. **Separate ideation from execution**: Founder can brainstorm freely. But nothing enters the roadmap without meeting specific criteria (market size, customer evidence, resource requirement).
