# Jobs-to-be-Done (JTBD) Framework

## The Core Idea

Customers don't buy products. They "hire" products to do a job for them.
Understanding the JOB reveals the real competition and the real opportunity.

Famous example: People don't buy a quarter-inch drill. They hire it to make
a quarter-inch hole. Actually, they hire it to hang a picture. Actually, they
hire it to make their wife happy. Actually, they hire it to feel like a good
husband.

## The JTBD Statement

```
When [situation],
I want to [motivation],
so I can [expected outcome].
```

**Example (project management tool):**
> When my team is starting a new project,
> I want to align everyone on goals and deliverables,
> so I can avoid the painful re-explanations and missed expectations later.

## Discovery via JTBD Interview

Don't ask: "What features do you want?"
Ask: "Tell me about the last time you [situation]."

**Interview structure:**
1. **The trigger** — What was happening when you decided you needed a solution?
2. **The criteria** — What were you looking for? What didn't matter?
3. **The decision** — Why did you choose [current solution]?
4. **The hire** — How did you start using it?
5. **The outcome** — What changed for you?
6. **The disappointment** — Where did it fall short?

## Forces of Progress (Decision Drivers)

Customers switch when 4 forces align:

```
Push of current situation         Pull of new solution
(unhappy with status quo)         (something better)
        \                        /
         \                      /
          \      DECISION      /
          /                    \
         /                      \
        /                        \
Anxiety about new                 Habit of current
(scared to change)                (sticking with what works)
```

For a customer to switch:
- Push must be strong (current pain)
- Pull must be clear (new value)
- Anxiety must be low (trust + low risk)
- Habit must be weak (low switching cost)

## Job Categories

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| Functional | The practical task | Hang a picture |
| Emotional | How they want to feel | Feel competent / capable |
| Social | How they want to be seen | Be seen as handy |

Most jobs have all three. Designing for only functional misses why people actually buy.

## Application: Discovery
- "Tell me about the last time you [job]"
- "What made you decide to start looking for a solution?"
- "What did you try first?"
- "What were the trade-offs you considered?"
- "What was your moment of truth — when you knew this would or wouldn't work?"

## Application: Positioning
Position around the job, not the features:

**Feature-positioning (weak):**
> "Project management software with Gantt charts, kanban boards, and timeline views"

**JTBD-positioning (strong):**
> "Stop re-explaining what's expected. Get your team aligned on day 1."

## Application: Competition
Real competition is anything customers might "hire" instead — including:
- Direct competitors
- Indirect alternatives (different category, same job)
- Hacked workarounds (spreadsheets, shared docs)
- Doing nothing
- Hiring a person to do it

## Application: Pricing
Price to the job's value, not the cost to deliver:

- What does failure of this job cost the customer?
- What's the alternative cost (time, money, frustration)?
- Where on this spectrum is your "fair" price?

## Common Mistakes
- Confusing job with task (task is too small)
- Confusing job with persona (persona is who, job is what)
- Stopping at functional job (missing emotional/social)
- Designing features that don't serve the job
- Comparing to direct competitors only (missing the workaround)
