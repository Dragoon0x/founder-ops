# SWOT Analysis

## The Four Quadrants

|   | Helpful | Harmful |
|---|---------|---------|
| **Internal** | **S**trengths | **W**eaknesses |
| **External** | **O**pportunities | **T**hreats |

## Honest SWOT Rules

Most SWOT analyses fail because they're optimistic fantasy. Rules:

### Strengths (internal, helpful)
- Things you do BETTER than competitors
- Capabilities that are HARD to replicate
- Resources that are TRULY differentiating

**Not Strengths:**
- "We care about customers" (everyone says this)
- "Our product is great" (vague)
- "We move fast" (every startup claims this)

**Good Strengths:**
- "Proprietary dataset of 10M labeled examples"
- "Team of 4 ex-Google engineers with patent in this space"
- "3-year head start on index partnerships"

### Weaknesses (internal, harmful)
- Things competitors do BETTER
- Resources you LACK
- Capabilities that NEED investment

**Not Weaknesses:**
- "We're small" (could be strength — depends)
- "We move fast" (same)
- "We don't have all features yet" (vague)

**Good Weaknesses:**
- "No enterprise sales experience on team"
- "Customer acquisition cost trending up 40% YoY"
- "Single cloud provider dependency (AWS)"
- "No engineer in EU timezone, slows international support"

### Opportunities (external, helpful)
- Market shifts you can capitalize on
- Competitor mistakes creating openings
- Technology shifts enabling new value
- Regulatory changes opening new markets

**Good Opportunities:**
- "New regulation requires X, our product already compliant"
- "Main competitor just pivoted away from our segment"
- "Enterprise budgets shifting from old category to new (30% YoY)"

### Threats (external, harmful)
- Competitor moves
- Market contractions
- Technology disruption
- Regulatory risks
- Macro conditions

**Good Threats:**
- "Two well-funded competitors raised Series B in last 90 days"
- "Key channel (X) changing algorithm, could reduce traffic 40%"
- "Industry shifting to open source alternative"

## The Real Use of SWOT

Basic SWOT is a list. Strategic SWOT is a **matrix of actions**:

| | Leverage Opportunities | Defend Against Threats |
|---|---|---|
| **Using Strengths** | S-O: How do we use our strengths to capture opportunities? | S-T: How do we use strengths to defend against threats? |
| **Addressing Weaknesses** | W-O: Which weaknesses block us from opportunities? Fix those first. | W-T: Where do weaknesses make threats dangerous? Urgent fix. |

### Example (analytics startup)

**SWOT Inputs:**
- S: Proprietary data pipeline, 3x faster than competitors
- W: No enterprise sales experience
- O: Large enterprises exploring modern data stack
- T: Google launching competing product Q3

**Strategic Actions:**

- **S-O:** Productize the speed advantage, target enterprises (6-month goal: 5 enterprise logos)
- **S-T:** Race to enterprise features before Google; build moat with integrations
- **W-O:** HIRE VP Sales with enterprise experience in next 90 days (blocker to S-O)
- **W-T:** If we don't hire enterprise leader before Google launches, we lose the window

## Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| SWOT done once, shelved | Useless without action |
| No specificity (vague items) | No decisions possible |
| Over-focus on strengths | Misses urgent weaknesses |
| Listing opportunities without assessing winability | Wishful thinking |
| Ignoring threats (head-in-sand) | Surprised by inevitable |
| SWOT without the matrix (S-O, S-T, etc) | Just a list, not strategy |

## When to Use
- Annual strategic planning
- Entering new market / segment
- Post-mortem after losing strategic battle
- Deciding major organizational changes
- Board preparation

## When Not to Use
- Tactical decisions (too high-level)
- Feature prioritization (use RICE/ICE)
- Meeting decisions (overkill)
