# CAC Payback Period

## Definition
The number of months it takes to earn back the cost of acquiring a customer.

## Formula
```
Payback Period (months) = CAC / (ARPA × Gross Margin %)
```

Where ARPA = Average Revenue Per Account per month.

## Benchmarks

| GTM Motion | Target Payback | Red Flag |
|-----------|----------------|----------|
| PLG / Self-serve | < 6 months | > 12 months |
| Inside sales (SMB) | < 12 months | > 18 months |
| Inside sales (mid-market) | < 15 months | > 24 months |
| Field sales (enterprise) | < 18 months | > 24 months |

## Why payback period matters
Payback period determines how much capital you need to grow.

Short payback (< 6 months): You can fund growth from revenue. Less dependent on fundraising.
Long payback (> 18 months): You need significant capital to grow. Every new customer is an investment that takes 1.5+ years to pay off.

At scale, the difference is massive:
- 100 new customers/month × $5K CAC × 6-month payback = $3M in "working capital" deployed
- 100 new customers/month × $5K CAC × 18-month payback = $9M in "working capital" deployed

## Improvement levers
1. Reduce CAC (better conversion, cheaper channels)
2. Increase ARPA (pricing power, upsell at point of sale)
3. Improve gross margin (reduce COGS, automate support)
4. Front-load value (annual prepay reduces effective payback)

## Common mistakes
1. Not including gross margin (overstating payback speed by 20-40%)
2. Using blended payback when segments differ significantly
3. Not accounting for sales rep ramp time (new reps have longer payback)
4. Ignoring expansion revenue (payback improves if customers expand quickly)
