# Enterprise Customer Negotiation (6-figure ACV)

## Before you enter the pricing conversation
- Decide your walk-away ACV and your target ACV.
- Know whether this deal is strategically valuable beyond ARR (logo, case study, reference, category entry).
- Know their budget (ask procurement; look at Section 263 disclosures if public).
- Know their FY end. Q4 creates urgency for them.
- Understand the decision tree: who's champion, who's economic buyer, who's procurement, who's security, who's legal.

## Pricing anchoring
- Price high on the initial proposal. Leave room to concede. Never lead with your floor.
- Deliver pricing verbally before written if possible. Let them react.
- Wrap in value: "Based on [X] seats and [Y] volume, the annual commitment is $Z. Here's the math on ROI given your [metric]..."

## Procurement's tactics

### "We never sign at list."
Response:
> "Understood. We have a standard discount ladder based on commitment length and volume. At your volume, we can offer [X]% off list for a 12-month commitment, [Y]% for 24 months, [Z]% for 36. Which time horizon fits your planning?"

This reframes: the discount is for THEIR commitment, not their toughness.

### "Competitor X offered us Y% less."
Response:
> "What's in their proposal? Scope matters — I want to make sure we're comparing apples to apples. If you send the scope of their proposal, I'll come back with a revised number that reflects the real comparison."

Often their proposal is narrower than yours. Reveal scope gaps.

### "We need net 60 terms, not net 30."
Cost of longer terms: ~1-2% (time value of money). Offer:
> "Net 60 works if we move from quarterly to annual upfront billing. If monthly billing is required, net 30 stays."

### "We need [specific custom integration]."
Decide if it's strategic or bespoke:
- If strategic (multiple customers want it): fund it through this contract at a premium. "The integration is $X one-time. We'll amortize it in the ACV as [terms]. It becomes part of the product for your tier."
- If bespoke (just them): either charge full cost loaded with margin, or decline. Say no more than you think you should.

### "We need indemnification uncapped."
Response:
> "Our liability cap is [2x annual contract value or $Y million, whichever is greater] for general claims, uncapped for IP infringement we cause, gross negligence, and willful misconduct. That's in line with market for SaaS contracts. Can you share why uncapped is needed?"

Often the counterparty doesn't actually want uncapped. Procurement checked a box. Push and they fold.

### "We need a MFN clause" (most favored nation pricing)
Never accept. Polite but firm:
> "We don't offer MFN because pricing varies with scope, volume, and timing for each customer. What we can offer is a price commitment: no increase greater than [5]% at renewal for the next [3] years."

### "We want perpetual license, not SaaS."
For SaaS, almost always say no. Exception: regulatory requirements for on-prem.
> "We don't sell perpetual licenses because our value depends on continuous updates, security patches, and improvements. We can offer annual licenses with your choice of cloud deployment or dedicated VPC."

## Escalation patterns

### They "escalate to the VP / CFO"
Two scenarios:
1. They want you to escalate on your side to match. Don't take the bait unless deal is worth your CEO's time.
2. They're using seniority theater to squeeze. Match their energy: "Happy to talk to [their VP]. Can we set a 15-min call with my VP of Sales and theirs? We can align on the scope and timeline together."

### They ghost for 2 weeks
One-time follow-up:
> "Checking in on the proposal. Anything I can clarify? Also, our pricing for Q[X] expires on [date] — want to make sure you have time if you're moving forward."

Urgency without whining. If no response, pipeline slip to next quarter, don't chase further.

## Closing

### Verbal commitment
> "Great. To confirm: $[ACV], [term], [start date], [key SLA]. Our contract will match what we've discussed plus our standard terms. I'll send it today. What's the signature path on your side — one signer or multiple?"

### Timing
- Same day: send contract
- Next day: confirm receipt
- Day 3-5: check on legal review status
- Day 7: if silence, call the champion directly and ask what's stuck

## Walking away
> "This isn't going to work at the terms you need. I appreciate the conversation. If things change on your side, keep my number."

Put them in a quarterly nurture cadence. 20-30% of walked-away deals come back within 18 months.

## After close
- Send signed contract + welcome email same day
- Kick off implementation within 7 days
- Schedule exec sponsor 1:1 at day 30, 90, renewal-minus-90
