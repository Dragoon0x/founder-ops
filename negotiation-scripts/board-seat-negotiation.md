# Board Seat Negotiation

## The default
Most term sheets will propose a board seat for the lead. At pre-seed and seed, you often don't need to give one. At Series A, typically yes. After Series A, inevitable.

## When to push back on a board seat
- Check size doesn't warrant it (< 10% ownership, < $2M check, and they're not bringing unique governance value).
- You already have a committed director from the last round who plays the role they'd play.
- They've shown up poorly in due diligence (ghosted, unprepared, needs hand-holding).

## Scripts

### "We'd rather not add a board seat right now."
> "We value your investment and we want you engaged. We're trying to keep the board tight — 3 people right now. We propose giving you board observer rights for the duration of your investment, plus monthly 1:1s with me directly. When we raise Series A, we'll revisit."

### "We'd like the board to remain founder-majority."
> "At this stage, operational velocity matters more than governance depth. The people who need to vote on day-to-day decisions are the operators closest to the business. I know that changes over time — here's what I'd propose as the glide path:
> - Today: 2 founder, 1 investor, 0 independent
> - Post-Series-A: 2 founder, 2 investor, 1 independent (jointly chosen)
> - Post-Series-B: revisit based on company stage"

### If they insist on a seat and you're ok-ish
Lock the following:
- **Observer rights for no one else.** "Super observers" from other investors balloon meeting size and leak information.
- **Term of service.** Board seat should end when their ownership falls below a threshold (e.g., 5%) or at a specific later round.
- **Replaceable.** You can request replacement if the specific person isn't performing (not just "the firm" has a seat).
- **Conflict protocol.** If they invest in a competitor later, they automatically step off.

### When they want MORE than a seat (control, veto on hires, etc.)
Rare at seed, more common in difficult markets or down rounds. Script:
> "I want to give you what you need to feel comfortable, but I can't give you operational control. What's the actual risk you're protecting against? If it's capital efficiency, let's build budget discipline into protective provisions. If it's founder turnover, let's talk about founder vesting. What specifically is worrying you?"

## After the call
- Document everything. Board composition and rights are mechanical in term sheets; long-form docs expand them 10x. Watch drift.
- Make sure observer rights (if granted) are clearly defined: attendance only, no vote, can be excluded from executive sessions.
