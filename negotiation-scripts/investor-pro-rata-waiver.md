# Asking an Investor to Waive Pro-Rata

## When you need this
- New lead investor wants more of the round than available.
- Strategic investor wants to join the round but dilutive to existing.
- Secondary sale constrained by pro-rata rights.

## Before
- Know who has pro-rata rights and what % of the round those represent.
- Identify your "must keep happy" vs "nice to keep happy" investors.
- Understand why they have pro-rata (historical contribution, fund construction).

## Script: early-stage investor who no longer adds much

> "Wanted to run something by you. We're putting together the Series [X] and have strong demand, which creates a nice problem: the round is oversubscribed. [New lead] wants to take [Y%] of the allocation. To get there, we need to ask some early investors to waive part of their pro-rata. You were incredibly helpful at [stage], and I want to be transparent: we're asking early backers who've already realized significant paper markup to consider waiving all or part of pro-rata so we can build the right syndicate. What's your take?"

## Script: strategic logic

> "The round structure depends on [new investor] getting enough ownership to justify their level of engagement. They're writing a $[X] check and bringing [specific value]. Without pro-rata adjustments from existing investors, they can't get there, and we lose the strategic upside. I know this is a real ask. Would you consider waiving [full / half / 25%] for this round? Next round, we'll prioritize your allocation."

## What to offer in exchange
- Written commitment to priority allocation next round (non-binding but creates norm)
- Additional information rights (board observer, monthly updates) for strategic investors
- Secondary sale opportunity at a later date
- Nothing formal — just goodwill. Works surprisingly often.

## If they refuse
- Respect it. They have legal rights.
- Explore alternatives: reduce new lead's allocation, run a smaller round, delay secondary, etc.
- Don't poison the relationship. They're long-term partners.

## Do not
- Threaten about future allocations.
- Characterize their participation as an inconvenience.
- Approach one investor and not others similarly situated.
