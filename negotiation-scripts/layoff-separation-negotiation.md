# Separation/Severance Negotiation

## Context
You're the employer. Employee is being laid off or let go. They may negotiate.

## Defaults to offer (US, no plan)
- Severance: 2 weeks + 1 week per year of tenure, minimum 4 weeks for non-performance terminations.
- COBRA subsidy: 1-3 months.
- Option exercise extension: 6-12 months for terminated employees with vested options (compassionate but check tax implications).
- Transition support: 1-3 month career coaching stipend.
- PTO payout: per state law.

## Standard agreement asks from company
- Release of claims (standard)
- Non-disparagement (mutual or one-way)
- Return of property (standard)
- Confidentiality reaffirmed (standard)

## Things they may ask for

**"I want 3 months instead of 6 weeks."**
Decide in advance what you'd go to. If they're senior/long-tenured/risk of claim: stretch. Script:
> "We've been consistent across the team at [X]. I can make exceptions for specific circumstances. What's driving the ask — job market, health coverage, something else? Let's solve the actual need if we can."

**"I want my unvested options."**
Default no. Options are for active employees. Exception: exec with written agreement.
> "Unvested shares stay in the pool. What I can do is [extend exercise window on vested / offer cash equivalent on 1 year of unvested / nothing more, honestly]."

**"I want positive references / LinkedIn recommendation."**
Negotiable. Free to you. But don't commit to what you can't deliver.
> "I'll provide a neutral reference confirming dates and role. If you want a stronger one, I can do that for roles where you excelled — let me know the specifics."

**"I want non-compete removed."**
If the non-compete is meaningful and your state enforces it: hold firm or trade for something.
If it's CA (unenforceable) or junior role: remove it, costs nothing.

**"My bonus / commission wasn't paid."**
Pay what they earned. Don't negotiate earned money; it's a trap.

## What not to negotiate
- Whether the termination happens. Decision is made. "I hear you. This decision is final. Let's focus on making your transition as good as we can."
- Whether they can talk to a lawyer. They can. Encourage it: "Take 21 days, run the agreement past a lawyer, come back with questions." That protects the release too.
- Past disciplinary events. Don't re-litigate.

## Red flags for escalation
- They mention discrimination or retaliation. Stop. Involve employment counsel. Do not continue the conversation without one.
- They mention public whistleblowing. Stop. Counsel.
- They mention a regulator (EEOC, DOL). Stop. Counsel.

## Tone
- Respectful, not cold.
- Don't over-apologize (it signals negotiability on the decision).
- Don't pretend this is easy — it isn't.
- End clearly: "Here's what happens next. [HR will send the agreement within 24 hours.] Your deadline is [X]. Talk to a lawyer. Come back with questions or sign when ready."

## After
- All commitments in writing.
- Final paycheck per state law (some states require immediate).
- Benefits wind-down coordinated by HR within 48 hours.
- Access termination timed correctly (usually end of last day, not before).
