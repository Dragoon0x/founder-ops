# Hardware — Industry Overview

Last updated: 2026-Q1

## Why hardware is different

Software iterates in days. Hardware iterates in months and eats capital while it does. A hardware company is three businesses stacked: R&D, supply chain, and distribution. Most founders are good at one and fail at the others.

## Sub-verticals

| Sub-vertical | Capital intensity | Typical first-product timeline |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Consumer electronics | Medium-high | 18-30 months |
| Industrial / B2B devices | High | 24-36 months |
| Robotics | Very high | 30-60 months |
| Medical devices | Very high | 24-60 months (regulatory) |
| IoT / connected | Medium | 12-24 months |
| Wearables | Medium-high | 18-30 months |
| Semiconductor | Extreme | 36-60 months |

## Key numbers (2026-Q1)

- Prototype to DVT (Design Validation Test): 6-12 months typical
- DVT to PVT (Production Validation Test): 2-4 months
- PVT to ramp: 1-3 months
- Typical gross margin at launch: 30-45%; mature: 45-60%
- Tooling cost for plastic injection parts: $10k-150k per part
- PCBA NRE (non-recurring engineering): $25k-250k typically
- Certifications (FCC, CE, UL, etc.): $30k-200k depending on scope
- Inventory turns to aspire to: 4-8/year consumer, 6-12 industrial
- Cash tied up in inventory: 90-180 days of COGS typical

## The hardware stack

Every hardware company deals with:

1. **Industrial design (ID).** How it looks/feels
2. **Mechanical engineering (ME).** How it is physically built
3. **Electrical engineering (EE).** Circuits, PCBA
4. **Firmware.** Embedded software
5. **Cloud / apps.** If connected
6. **Manufacturing engineering.** DFM, DFA, DFT
7. **Supply chain.** Sourcing, logistics, customs
8. **Quality.** QC, reliability, field returns
9. **Operations.** Fulfillment, service, support
10. **Compliance.** Country-by-country certifications

## Stage-by-stage

**Pre-seed.** Prototype in hand, founder can build it alone or with a contractor, bench tested.

**Seed.** EVT (Engineering Validation Test) unit built and tested. Bill of materials priced. Contract manufacturer selected. First customer pilots.

**Series A.** DVT complete, pilot production run of 100-1000 units, early revenue, clear path to PVT.

**Series B+.** PVT passed, ramping production, real distribution (retail, channel, direct), second product on roadmap.

## Capital planning

Hardware burns differently than SaaS. Expect:

- $2-5M for seed stage prototype to pilot
- $5-15M for Series A to volume production
- $15-50M+ for Series B for scale, second product, working capital

Working capital math is brutal: if COGS is $100 and you ship 10k units/month, you need approximately $1M of standing inventory (3 months × $100 × 10k × 30%). Scaling from 10k to 50k units requires a step-function cash injection often in the middle of a growth quarter.

## Common business models

- **Direct consumer sale** (highest margin, hardest distribution)
- **Retail** (lower margin, volume)
- **Channel / reseller** (complex incentives, 15-40% dealer margin)
- **Hardware + SaaS (razor/blade)** (ideal; hardware at low margin or break-even, recurring SaaS)
- **Hardware as a service** (subscription, you retain ownership)
- **Enterprise licensing + hardware** (industrial, medical)

Hardware + SaaS is the modern hardware model. Pure hardware is hard to sustain without recurring revenue.

## What good looks like

- Gross margin path to 45%+
- Inventory turns 4+
- Field return rate under 2% (consumer), under 0.5% (industrial)
- Second-source on critical components
- Contract manufacturer relationship > 2 years with minimum 2 sites
- Product-specific certifications current in all sold markets
- Firmware updatable in the field (OTA for connected products)
