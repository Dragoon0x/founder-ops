# Marketplace — Industry Overview

## What "Wins" Looks Like

A two-sided marketplace connects supply (sellers, providers, hosts) with demand
(buyers, customers, guests). The platform takes a cut of each transaction.

Win conditions:
- Liquidity: most listings/users get matched within X timeframe
- Take rate: 5-30% depending on category
- Network effects: each new participant adds value for the others
- GMV growth (Gross Merchandise Volume) — the gold standard metric

## The Three Sub-Categories

### Vertical Marketplaces (specialized)
- **Examples**: Etsy (handmade), Reverb (musical instruments), StockX (sneakers)
- **Pros**: Easier to dominate a niche; defensible; pricing power
- **Cons**: Smaller TAM ceiling
- **GTM**: Concentrated on the niche; community-driven

### Horizontal Marketplaces (general)
- **Examples**: eBay, Amazon Marketplace, Facebook Marketplace
- **Pros**: Massive TAM
- **Cons**: Hard to start (need scale immediately); competing with giants
- **GTM**: Expensive, requires major capital

### Service Marketplaces
- **Examples**: Uber, Airbnb, Upwork, TaskRabbit, Fiverr, Doordash
- **Pros**: High-frequency use; recurring revenue from users
- **Cons**: Operational complexity (trust, safety, payments, disputes)
- **GTM**: Geographic concentration first, then expand

## The Cold Start Problem

The single hardest thing about marketplaces. Without supply, demand has nothing
to buy. Without demand, supply has no reason to list.

### Cold Start Strategies

**1. Single-side seeding (the Andrew Chen approach)**
- Manually fake one side of the marketplace until the other side is real
- Example: Reddit founders posted under fake usernames in early days
- Example: Yelp paid reviewers in early markets

**2. Geographic concentration**
- Don't try to launch nationally
- Pick ONE city/region with high density
- Get to liquidity there before expanding
- Example: Uber launched only in San Francisco for 18 months

**3. Niche concentration**
- Don't try to be all things
- Pick ONE narrow category
- Win it completely
- Then expand horizontally
- Example: StockX started with sneakers only

**4. Existing audience leverage**
- Already have a relevant audience? Use it.
- Build community/content first; convert audience to marketplace users
- Example: Product Hunt → AngelList Talent

**5. SEO arbitrage**
- Generate content/inventory programmatically that ranks
- Inventory pages collect demand
- Demand attracts supply
- Example: Yelp built city pages before having reviews

## Ideal Founder Profile

- Operations background (marketplaces are operational businesses)
- Comfort with chicken-and-egg problems
- Patience (cold start can take 12-24 months)
- Strong intuition about supply/demand dynamics

## Founder Mistakes (Common)

1. **Trying to launch both sides simultaneously** — Pick one to seed, hand-craft the other
2. **National launch out of the gate** — Liquidity matters more than coverage; start hyperlocal
3. **Take rate too high too early** — Suppliers leave; reduce friction first, monetize later
4. **Ignoring trust and safety** — One bad incident kills trust for everyone
5. **No payments infrastructure** — Have to build escrow/disputes/refunds eventually
6. **Off-platform leakage** — Once buyer + seller meet, they transact directly; build switching costs
7. **Optimizing for total users, not active users** — Inactive users don't matter for liquidity
