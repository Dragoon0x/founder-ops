# Fintech — Common Pitfalls

### 1. Treating compliance as overhead
Compliance *is* the product in fintech. Founders who treat it as an annoying tax get caught flat-footed by the first examination or SAR volume spike.

### 2. Launching before you understand fraud
First 1000 users include fraudsters. They move fast, coordinate, and will empty your treasury. Model fraud before launch; set limits; have an incident runbook.

### 3. Bank partner concentration
Single sponsor bank = single point of failure. Synapse/Evolve-class events kill companies that assumed the partner relationship was permanent. Target at least two bank partners by Series A.

### 4. Misunderstanding the float economics
Float (interest earned on customer deposits) looks like free money until rates drop 300 bps and your gross margin halves. Plan for a rate cycle.

### 5. Ignoring state-by-state licensing
Money transmitter licenses are state-by-state. Launching nationally without the stack creates enforcement liability. Build a phased state rollout.

### 6. Under-resourcing the BSA officer role
Designating the CTO or founder is a bad idea. Real BSA compliance takes real time. Underinvestment here causes regulatory action.

### 7. Retail bias in credit models
Training credit models on consumer data and applying to SMB lending (or vice versa) produces systematic misestimation. Losses follow.

### 8. Confusing gross and net revenue
Announcing "we processed $1B TPV" while net revenue is $2M is meaningless and misleads your own team. Report net throughout.

### 9. Growth above unit economics
Fintech tolerates less LTV/CAC drift than SaaS because losses compound. A 2:1 ratio that looks fixable in SaaS is a company-ender in lending.

### 10. Consumer marketing for subprime products
Aggressive marketing of high-APR products triggers UDAAP and state AG interest. Compliance reviews every channel.

### 11. Ignoring chargeback economics
Chargebacks cost the fee + the disputed amount + scheme fines + operational time. They scale nonlinearly. Under 0.5% is table stakes.

### 12. No reconciliation discipline
Money moves through ledgers, banks, processors, card networks. Every daily recon is a canary. Companies that skip recon discover 6-figure holes months later.

### 13. Underpricing during 0% rate era
Companies that priced on cheap capital assumptions found their model underwater when rates rose. Stress-test pricing for 400 bps rate moves.

### 14. Over-promising on data
"We use AI to detect fraud" in a pitch deck invites regulator scrutiny on model bias. Do not put this in marketing unless you can defend the model.

### 15. Ignoring OFAC / sanctions screening
Onboarding a sanctioned entity is a federal felony. Screening is not optional, and it is not a checkbox; it is a program with maintenance.

### 16. Crypto-adjacent without the crypto stack
Building products that touch crypto without FinCEN registration, proper AML, or wallet screening is how companies get shut down overnight.

### 17. Customer support as cost center
In fintech, a slow support response on a frozen account triggers BBB complaints, CFPB complaints, and churn cascade. Customer support is compliance-adjacent.

### 18. No incident response playbook
Fraud wave, data breach, outage, wire reversal. These *will* happen. Having a playbook means losing 2 days not 2 weeks.

### 19. Regulatory arbitrage as strategy
Banking on a loophole (state vs. federal, fintech vs. bank) is a time-limited strategy. Regulators close loopholes.

### 20. Trust recovery is slow
A single security or fraud incident takes 12-24 months to recover from in user trust. Prevention beats PR.
