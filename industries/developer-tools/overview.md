# Developer Tools — Industry Overview

## What "Wins" Looks Like

Selling to developers requires fundamentally different motion than selling to other
buyers. Developers research, evaluate, and adopt — they don't get sold to.

Win conditions:
- Bottoms-up adoption (individual devs evangelize within their company)
- Low friction to try (free tier, generous trial, instant signup)
- Strong technical content and community
- Eventually: enterprise upsell (governance, SSO, compliance)

## Categories

### Open Source First (COSS / Open Core)
- **Examples**: GitLab, MongoDB, Elastic, HashiCorp, Vercel
- **Model**: Open source core + paid commercial features
- **Pros**: Massive distribution, community, defensibility
- **Cons**: Slow monetization, hard to convert OSS users to paid
- **Capital required**: Significant runway needed

### API / Infrastructure
- **Examples**: Twilio, Stripe, Auth0, Algolia, Pinecone
- **Model**: Usage-based or seat-based for the API
- **Pros**: Scales with customer growth, sticky once integrated
- **Cons**: Hard to differentiate at the start
- **Sales motion**: PLG with enterprise upsell

### Developer Productivity / IDEs
- **Examples**: Cursor, Replit, Linear, Postman, GitHub Copilot
- **Model**: Per-seat subscription
- **Pros**: Daily-use products = sticky
- **Cons**: Need rapid product velocity
- **Differentiation**: Speed, UX, integrations

### Observability / DevOps
- **Examples**: Datadog, Grafana, Honeycomb, Sentry
- **Model**: Usage-based (data volume) or per-seat
- **Pros**: Mission-critical = high retention
- **Cons**: Crowded space, deep technical sales

### Security
- **Examples**: Snyk, Wiz, Vanta
- **Model**: Per-developer or per-asset pricing
- **Pros**: Compliance-driven budget, must-have
- **Cons**: Long sales cycles

## The Developer Adoption Curve

Developers adopt new tools in this order:
1. **Discover** (Twitter/HN/blog/word of mouth)
2. **Try** (free, no signup, quick win)
3. **Adopt** (use for personal/side project)
4. **Advocate** (recommend to coworker)
5. **Bring to work** (use in company project)
6. **Champion** (push for company-wide adoption)
7. **Procure** (work through procurement)

Skipping or breaking any step kills the funnel. Most devtool deaths happen at #2
(too much friction to try) or #6 (no enterprise features).

## Ideal Founder Profile

- Strong engineering background (devs respect builders)
- Active in dev community before launching
- Comfortable writing (devs read; marketers don't reach them)
- Patience for community-driven growth

## Founder Mistakes (Common)

1. **Marketing-led GTM** — Devs distrust marketing; lead with product and content
2. **Closed-source posturing without OSS commitment** — Devs see through it
3. **Sales calls before product trial** — Kills bottoms-up motion
4. **Forms before docs** — Devs leave the moment they hit a signup wall
5. **No public docs** — If devs can't find docs via Google, they assume product is bad
6. **Ignoring DX (Developer Experience)** — DX is THE moat for devtools
7. **Pricing per-seat for shared infra** — Doesn't match how devs use the tool
