# Term Sheet Decoder

## What a term sheet is
A non-binding summary of the key terms of an investment. It is not the
final legal document — the Stock Purchase Agreement and related docs
follow. But a term sheet is hard to change once signed.

**A term sheet is usually 2–5 pages. Read every line.**

## The sections that matter, ranked by impact on founders

### 1. Valuation (pre-money or post-money)
The pre-money is the company's agreed value before the new money is added.

```
Post-money = Pre-money + Investment amount
Investor ownership % = Investment / Post-money
Founder dilution = Investment / Post-money (roughly)
```

Example:
- Pre-money: $20M
- Investment: $5M
- Post-money: $25M
- Investor ownership: 20%
- Founder dilution: 20% (but option pool refresh adds more)

### 2. Option Pool (pre- or post-money)
**The trap.** The pool is almost always carved out of the pre-money,
not the post-money. This dilutes you, not the new investor.

Example:
- Pre-money: $20M
- Target option pool: 15% of post-money
- Effective pre-money (after pool): $20M - $3.75M = $16.25M
- Investor ownership: $5M / $25M = 20%
- Your effective dilution: 20% (new investor) + 15% (pool) = 35%

Negotiate the size of the pool. Every 1% reduction saves you ~1% ownership.

### 3. Liquidation Preference
In a sale, investors get paid back before anyone else.

- **1x non-participating**: Investor gets their money back OR their
  ownership %, whichever is greater. Standard.
- **1x participating**: Investor gets their money back AND their
  ownership %. Worse for founders.
- **2x+ preferences**: Red flag. Founder-unfriendly.

Target: 1x non-participating. Walk away from 2x participating.

### 4. Anti-dilution
Protection against down rounds.

- **Broad-based weighted average**: Standard. Modest protection.
- **Narrow-based weighted average**: Worse for founders.
- **Full ratchet**: Very bad. Converts investor shares at the lowest
  price ever issued. Rare in 2025-2026 except in distressed rounds.

Target: broad-based weighted average. Walk away from full ratchet
in non-distressed rounds.

### 5. Board composition
At seed: often no change or 1 investor seat.
At Series A: typically 3-person board — 2 founders/common, 1 lead investor.
At Series B: typically 5-person board — 2 common, 2 investors, 1 independent.

**Key: control of the board.** Founders want a majority until Series B.
The tie-breaker is the independent director, which you should nominate.

### 6. Protective provisions (investor veto rights)
Investors can veto certain actions. Standard list:
- Amending charter
- Taking on debt above $X
- Issuing new preferred stock
- Selling the company
- Increasing the option pool
- Changing the size of the board
- Paying dividends

This is mostly fine. Watch for:
- **Veto on budget** (founder-unfriendly)
- **Veto on hiring specific roles** (founder-unfriendly)
- **Veto on ordinary course operations** (red flag)

### 7. Pro-rata rights
Investor can participate in future rounds to maintain %.

Standard. Grant to lead investors. Be careful with smaller investors —
at Series B, having 8 pro-rata participants makes rounds complex.

### 8. Founder vesting
New investors often require or re-paper founder vesting. Standard:
- 4-year vest with 1-year cliff.
- Some acceleration on change of control (single vs double trigger).

**Double trigger acceleration** (standard): you accelerate only if
acquired AND you're terminated without cause.
**Single trigger acceleration** (rare, better for founders): you
accelerate automatically on acquisition.

### 9. Information rights
Investors get regular financial and operational updates.
Standard: monthly KPIs, quarterly financials, annual plan.
Negotiate out: unlimited inspection rights, access to employee data.

### 10. Right of first refusal (ROFR) and co-sale
If you try to sell shares, the company (then investors) has the right
to buy first, or to sell alongside you.

Standard. Doesn't materially matter until secondary.

### 11. Drag-along rights
If a majority of preferred wants to sell the company, they can force
common to vote for it.

Standard at Series A+. Watch the threshold (majority of preferred is
standard; majority of preferred AND common is more founder-friendly).

### 12. Dividends
Non-cumulative is standard. Cumulative (8% compounding) is a red flag.

### 13. Registration rights
IPO-related. Not worth negotiating — use the standard NVCA template.

### 14. Expense reimbursement
Investors bill you for their legal fees. Standard cap: $25K–75K at
Series A, $75K–150K at Series B.

Negotiate the cap. Don't agree to uncapped.

### 15. No-shop / Exclusivity
Investor gets a period (typically 30–60 days) where you can't talk to
other investors.

Fair at the final stage. Don't sign one before you have a real term
sheet in hand.

## The hidden traps

1. **Option pool carve-out from pre-money.** Covered above. 3–8% of founder
   dilution hides here.
2. **Participating preferred.** Looks like a detail, can be 20%+ of
   exit proceeds at the wrong moment.
3. **Undefined "founder" vesting acceleration.** If the definition of
   "terminated without cause" is narrow, you might get nothing.
4. **Broad veto provisions on "material" actions.** "Material" is
   subjective. Try to get specific thresholds.
5. **Board matters vs stockholder matters.** Some votes go through the
   board (you can lose even with majority stockholder control). Read
   carefully which is which.

## Who should read every term sheet
1. You
2. Your cofounder(s)
3. Your startup lawyer (not a general corporate lawyer)
4. One experienced founder friend who's raised a similar round

Never sign a term sheet without #3. The hourly is $500–1,200 and worth it.

## Time to decode
- First read: 1 hour
- Lawyer review: 2–4 hours of their time
- Negotiation back-and-forth: 1–2 weeks
- Final signing: a relief
