# IP Assignment Basics

## Why this is the most important legal doc in your company
Without clean IP assignment, you don't own your product. If a founder,
contractor, or employee wrote code without signing an IP assignment,
that code might legally belong to them, not the company.

At diligence for any round, investors will check. Missing IP
assignment is a deal-breaker.

## The three scenarios you must cover

### 1. Founders
Signed at incorporation, as part of the stock purchase agreement.

Language: "All prior work and intellectual property related to the
Company's business is hereby assigned to the Company."

If a founder worked on the idea before incorporation (at a previous
employer, on their own time), this matters enormously:
- Work at previous employer: might be owned by that employer
- Work on own time with own tools, unrelated to employer: usually owned by founder, assignable
- Work on own time with own tools, related to employer: gray area, legally risky

Some employment contracts assign ALL work (even on-own-time) to the
employer. Some states (California, Washington) have laws limiting
this. If you built the initial prototype while employed, talk to a
lawyer before incorporating.

### 2. Employees
Every employment offer letter and employment agreement must include
an IP assignment clause.

Standard language: "All inventions, discoveries, works of authorship,
and other intellectual property created during your employment, using
Company resources, or relating to the Company's business shall be the
sole property of the Company."

### 3. Contractors
Contractor agreements default to the contractor owning the IP unless
explicitly assigned. You must include a work-for-hire clause AND
explicit IP assignment.

Template: "The parties agree that all Work Product is a work made for
hire under U.S. Copyright Law, and to the extent it is not, Contractor
hereby assigns all right, title, and interest in the Work Product to
the Company."

Both clauses matter because not all IP is covered by work-for-hire
(e.g., some patentable inventions).

## The foreign contractor trap
U.S. work-for-hire doesn't always transfer cleanly internationally.
For contractors outside the U.S.:
- Use the explicit assignment clause.
- Also include a "present assignment of future inventions" clause,
  because some jurisdictions don't recognize forward-looking
  assignments without specific language.
- Get a lawyer to review at least the first few contractor agreements.

## What you need in your data room
By seed stage, your data room should include:

1. Founder stock purchase agreements (with IP assignment)
2. Employee offer letters (with IP assignment clause)
3. Contractor agreements (with work-for-hire AND assignment)
4. Advisor agreements (with IP assignment if they produce any work)
5. Anyone who ever contributed code, design, or content to the
   product — must have something signed.

If anyone who wrote code for you isn't on this list, find them,
sign something, and update the data room.

## The retroactive fix
If someone worked for you without an IP assignment and is no longer
around, you have a problem.

Options:
1. **Track them down and sign retroactively.** Works if they're
   friendly. Typically requires a token payment to be enforceable
   (a few hundred dollars).
2. **Rewrite their contribution.** If their code is still there and
   they won't sign, rewrite it from scratch by someone who signed.
3. **Disclose to investors.** Better to disclose than be discovered.

Don't ignore. Diligence will find it.

## Special cases

### Open source contributions
If the company contributes code to an open source project, standard
OSS licenses (MIT, Apache) work. But you need internal clarity:
- Is the contribution during work hours? (Company time = company IP.)
- Is the project business-critical? (Different calculus.)
- Do employees have a policy for OSS contributions? (They should.)

### University IP
If you started at a university, the university's IP policy may claim
ownership of what you built. Get a written release before
incorporating. University tech transfer offices do this routinely
but slowly. Start early.

### Prior employer IP
The most common trap. If you built the prototype while employed:
- Read your employment contract.
- Read your state's laws (California Labor Code 2870 carves out
  specific protections for employees).
- Get a written release from prior employer if there's any doubt.
- If no release is possible, rebuild cleanly.

## Common founder mistakes
1. **Assuming they own their work.** They might, but the documentation
   isn't there.
2. **Using generic contractor agreements.** Off-the-shelf templates
   often have weak IP assignment.
3. **Not tracking contributors.** At month 24, they can't remember
   who wrote what. Document as you go.
4. **Open source without a policy.** Engineers contribute to OSS
   projects that compete with your product. Surprise.
5. **International contractors without lawyer review.** Enforcement
   is harder.
6. **Letting advisors write code without IP assignment.** Advisor
   agreements usually cover advice, not code.

## Time investment
- Founder assignment at incorporation: 1 hour
- Employee assignment in standard offer letter: 0 additional time
- Contractor assignment per contract: 15 minutes
- Retroactive cleanup: hours to weeks
- Diligence fix under time pressure: deal-breaker
