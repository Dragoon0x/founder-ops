# Playbook: First Enterprise Customer Expansion

## The moment
Your first enterprise customer is 6 months in. Usage is strong. There's
expansion opportunity (more seats, more teams, more products). The
account exec is asking what to do.

## The tension
Expansion in your first enterprise account is high-leverage, high-risk.
Done well, it establishes a pattern that funds your next year. Done
poorly, it distracts the team and damages a reference customer.

## Week-by-week plan (8 weeks)

### Weeks 1–2: Discovery
- Founder or senior AE attends a "state of the partnership" meeting
  with the customer champion.
- Review: what's working, what's not, what's on the customer's
  roadmap, what budget is available.
- Identify 2–3 expansion vectors (seats, new teams, additional
  products).
- Rank by customer demand and our execution capacity.

### Weeks 3–4: Pitch
- Write a specific proposal. Not a generic pricing page; a proposal
  with their logo, their teams, their use case.
- Bring to customer champion. Refine based on their feedback.
- Identify the economic buyer (usually a level up from the champion).
- Schedule a meeting with the economic buyer.

### Weeks 5–6: Negotiate
- Economic buyer meeting. Bring CEO or senior leader.
- Discuss: success criteria, commercial terms, timing.
- Align on contract length, pricing, and expansion scope.
- Offer 10% discount for multi-year commit.
- Do NOT offer discount without commit.

### Weeks 7–8: Close
- Legal review (both sides).
- Sign.
- Communicate the expansion internally (customer success, product).
- Start the new scope with a kickoff meeting.

## Expansion pricing logic

### Seat expansion
- Price per seat: same as original or 5–10% lower for multi-year.
- Don't discount more than 10% without significant commit (2+ years).
- If they want 50% off because "we're buying in volume," say no. The
  volume is already in the math.

### New team expansion
- Could be same pricing model or different.
- If different teams have different needs, consider a different tier.
- Avoid creating a custom tier for them. It sets precedent.

### New product expansion
- Ideal: your next product.
- Price 20–50% of the main product as a good default.
- Bundle discount if multi-year.

## Common mistakes

1. **Asking for expansion too early.** Before month 6, you don't have
   the usage data to justify it.
2. **Expanding without executive alignment.** Champion-only expansions
   break when champion leaves.
3. **Over-discounting.** Sets a precedent for all future expansion.
4. **Not writing a proposal.** "We can add seats" is weaker than
   "here's a 40-seat expansion proposal for Q3."
5. **Letting the customer design the expansion.** Your job is to
   propose, their job is to approve.

## Success metrics

- Expansion ACV: > 40% of original ACV is strong.
- Time from discovery to close: < 8 weeks is strong.
- Multi-year commit: gets you a better lock on the account.
- Reference willingness: they should say yes to a public case study.

## What to do after close

- Update CRM with expansion details.
- Schedule a customer success kickoff.
- Set expectations: what they should see in 30, 60, 90 days.
- Write an internal postmortem: what worked, what to repeat.
- Share the win with the team. This is important institutional
  knowledge.

## The long-term pattern

Your goal isn't the single expansion. It's the repeatable expansion.
After 3 successful expansions in year 1, you have a playbook:
- What usage data signals expansion readiness
- Who the economic buyer is
- What the commercial terms look like
- How long the cycle takes

Codify this. It becomes your customer success playbook at Series A.
