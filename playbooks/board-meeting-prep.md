# Playbook: Board Meeting Prep

## The Meta-Goal

Most founders treat board meetings as performances. Wrong frame.
Board meetings are working sessions. The goal is to:
1. Get the board's best thinking on hard decisions
2. Build trust through honesty
3. Align on priorities for the next quarter
4. NOT to impress them with progress

## Pre-Meeting (T-7 days)

### Send pre-read 48-72 hours before
The board should arrive with the deck already read. Meeting time is for discussion.

Pre-read should include:
- Last quarter's metrics (vs. last quarter, vs. plan)
- Key wins (3-5 bullets, brief)
- Key losses / challenges (BE HONEST — this is what builds trust)
- Strategic decisions you want input on (frame as questions)
- Hiring update
- Financial summary (cash, burn, runway)

Don't include:
- Daily operational details
- Things you've decided already and don't want input on
- Vendor tactics or feature lists

### Schedule the meeting properly
- 90 min for normal quarter
- 2 hours for major strategic discussion
- Always end on time (signals respect)
- Coffee/snacks if in person, water if remote
- Recording: Yes, with permission

## The Deck (10-15 slides MAX)

### Slide 1 — Title + Agenda
- Date, attendees
- Agenda with time allocations
- "Decisions needed today" callout

### Slide 2 — TL;DR
The 3 things you want them to take away. If they only read one slide, this is it.

### Slide 3 — Metrics Dashboard
One page, all the numbers:
- ARR (with growth rate)
- New / churned customers
- Net retention
- Burn / runway
- Team size
- Trend lines (last 6+ months)

### Slide 4 — Quarter Highlights
3-5 bullets. Things you're proud of.

### Slide 5 — Quarter Challenges
3-5 bullets. Things that didn't work or are still hard.
**This is the most important slide.** Hide nothing.

### Slides 6-8 — Deep Dive Topics
1-3 topics where you want board input. Each with:
- The situation
- Options considered
- Your recommendation
- What you're asking from them

### Slide 9 — Hiring
- Recent hires with brief intro
- Open roles with timeline
- Comp/equity issues if any

### Slide 10 — Financials
- P&L summary
- Cash position
- 18-month forecast (key assumptions)
- Anything financially noteworthy

### Slide 11 — Next Quarter Goals
3-5 specific, measurable goals.

### Slide 12 — Discussion Topics
Bullet list of things to discuss (in addition to deep dives).

### Slide 13 — Decisions Needed Today
Specific decisions you need from the board (binary, with options).

## Running the Meeting

### Time allocation (90-min meeting)
- 0-5 min: Quick context, recap of last meeting decisions
- 5-15 min: Metrics walkthrough (let them ask questions)
- 15-20 min: Highlights + challenges (skim — they read it)
- 20-65 min: Deep dives + discussion (the main event)
- 65-80 min: Hiring + financials
- 80-90 min: Decisions needed + next steps

### Discussion management
- Direct questions to specific board members ("Sarah, what's your take?")
- Don't get defensive on hard questions (they're trying to help)
- "I don't know" is a fine answer (better than fake-knowing)
- Take notes ON the issues being raised (visibly — shows you're listening)

### Decision tracking
For every decision made:
- Write it down on the deck or in shared notes
- Get explicit board acknowledgment ("So we're agreed on X?")
- Assign owner + timeline
- Send recap within 48 hours

## Post-Meeting (T+48 hours)

Send recap:

```
Subject: [Company] Board Recap — [Date]

Thanks for [Date]'s meeting. Quick recap:

Key decisions:
- [Decision 1] — owner: [name], timeline: [date]
- [Decision 2]

Action items:
- [Action 1] — owner: [name], date: [date]
- [Action 2]

Open questions to follow up on:
- [Question 1] — [name] to share thoughts by [date]

Next meeting: [date] (will send pre-read by [date - 3])

Thanks again for the time and the candid input.

[Founder]
```

## Between Meetings

The biggest mistake: only talking to your board at quarterly meetings.

### Cadence by board member
- **Lead investor:** Monthly 30-min call + ad hoc
- **Other investors:** Monthly written update + 60-min call quarterly
- **Independent directors:** Monthly call (often the most useful relationships)
- **Observer board members:** Monthly written update

### Surprise tax
The unwritten rule: NO surprises in board meetings.
If something significant happens between meetings, board hears it before the meeting.

## Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| Sandbagging metrics so you can "exceed plan" | Insulting; investors see through it |
| Surprise bad news at meeting | Trust killer |
| Avoiding hard questions | Board can't help if they don't know |
| Asking for permission instead of input | You're the founder, you decide |
| 50-slide deck | Nobody reads it; hides the important stuff |
| Reading slides aloud | Wastes everyone's time |
| Not preparing for the obvious questions | Looks unprepared |
| Not having decisions teed up | Meeting becomes a status report, not a working session |
| Treating it like a presentation | It's a discussion |

## What Board Members Wish You Knew

(From talking to board members across many companies)

1. We can tell when you're hiding something. Just tell us.
2. We want to help with hard decisions. Bring them to us.
3. Don't tell us about every tactical decision. Tell us about strategic ones.
4. We don't know your company as well as you do. Frame the context.
5. If we ask the same question twice, it's because the first answer wasn't satisfying.
6. We're often more conservative than founders should be — push back if you disagree.
7. The best founders give us actionable asks, not status updates.
