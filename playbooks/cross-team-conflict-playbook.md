# Cross-Team Conflict Playbook

Engineering and product. Sales and engineering. CS and product. Founders and finance. Cross-team conflict is the most common operational failure in startups 20–150 people. Most of it is solvable in 60 minutes.

## The four patterns

### Pattern 1: Priority conflict
Two teams want different things and both are reasonable. Example: engineering wants to pay down tech debt; sales wants new features for Q4 deals.

### Pattern 2: Incentive misalignment
Teams are measured on different things that pull against each other. Example: CS measured on retention, product measured on new feature shipping.

### Pattern 3: Unclear ownership
Both teams think the other owns something. Example: new customer onboarding owned by sales? CS? product?

### Pattern 4: Interpersonal breakdown
Specific people can't work together. Example: VP Eng and VP Product are in an active cold war.

Each pattern has a different fix. Most founders treat all four as pattern 4.

## Diagnosis

Sit in a 1:1 with both leaders separately. Ask:

1. What decision needs to be made?
2. What does "good" look like in 90 days?
3. What are they doing that's making this harder?
4. What are you doing that's making this harder?
5. What would need to be true for this to work?

If answers to 3 and 4 are specific behaviors, it's pattern 1, 2, or 3. If answers are personal ("they don't respect me"), it's pattern 4.

## Pattern 1 fix: forced prioritization

Get both leaders in a room. 90 minutes. Agenda:

1. State the decision (10 min)
2. Each leader presents their view and costs of the other's view (15 min each)
3. Quiet reading of both written pitches (10 min)
4. Questions only, no advocacy (20 min)
5. You decide (10 min)
6. Commitments on both sides (10 min)

The deliverable: a written decision with deadline, tradeoffs, and review date.

Use this when: the conflict is about a real tradeoff, both leaders are reasonable, and you have the information to decide.

## Pattern 2 fix: rewrite the scorecards

Run a workshop with leadership:

1. List each team's top 3 metrics
2. Map dependencies: team A's metric depends on team B doing what?
3. Find the conflicts
4. Add shared metrics (team A and B both compensated on same outcome)
5. Remove orthogonal metrics (team A not responsible for B's KPI)

Example: CS measured on NPS + retention. Product measured on DAU + feature ship velocity. Misalignment. Add: both measured on retention of customers using shipped features.

Use this when: conflict recurs in new forms every quarter. Root is structural.

## Pattern 3 fix: RACI with deadline

Run a 60-minute session. List the 5–10 contested responsibilities. For each, decide:

- **Responsible**: who does the work
- **Accountable**: who is on the hook (one person)
- **Consulted**: who must be asked
- **Informed**: who must know

Publish. Review in 60 days.

Use this when: same friction, different projects. Ownership is genuinely unclear.

## Pattern 4 fix: clear, kind, fast

This is the hardest. When two specific people can't work together, options:

1. **Repair**: mediated conversation, ground rules, 90-day trial
2. **Separate**: reduce interaction surface, change reporting structure
3. **Exit**: one of them leaves

Don't wait. Six weeks of interpersonal breakdown costs you two quarters of velocity.

### The mediated conversation

You facilitate. 90 minutes. Structure:

1. Ground rules: no interrupting, no attacks, focus on behavior not character
2. Each person states: "What I need from you" and "What I think you need from me"
3. Other person reflects back what they heard
4. Each person commits to 3 specific behavior changes
5. You set check-in dates: 2 weeks, 6 weeks, 12 weeks

If they can't agree on the 3 behaviors, you're in pattern 4 type 2: irreparable.

### The separation

Reporting structure changes so they don't directly interact. New interface: chief of staff, VP, or program manager. Temporary measure for 90 days; if conflict reduces, reconsider.

### The exit

Pick one of them. Usually the one who isn't open to feedback. Exit package, honest internal comms, move forward.

## When you are one of the two in conflict

Same patterns apply. Harder to diagnose objectively.

- Get a coach or board member to mediate
- Ask for a 1:1 with the other person, structured
- If it's your co-founder: separate coaching
- Do not fire the other person without a peer CEO or board conversation
- Do not quit without a 90-day cool-down

## Signals you need to intervene as CEO

- Slack channels created for "alignment" that the other team isn't in
- "I'd rather not have [X] in this meeting"
- Projects stalling at specific handoffs
- Skip-level meetings requested by direct reports of both leaders
- Glassdoor or peer feedback mentions the conflict

## Communication to the organization

- Do not litigate publicly
- Announce decisions, not deliberations
- If people leave as a result, mutual respect in the comms
- Model what you want teams to do
- The org takes its temperature from how you handle conflict

---

*Unresolved conflict is a tax on every decision. Pay it down.*
