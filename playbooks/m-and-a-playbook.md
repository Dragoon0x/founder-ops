# M&A Playbook: Getting Acquired (or Acquiring)

The median startup acquisition closes in 4–6 months from first conversation to wire. 30–40% of deals die between LOI and close. This playbook is for founders on either side.

## When acquisition makes sense for you (the seller)

- Burn trajectory means raising is the only alternative and raising is not clean
- Strategic value to a larger player is 3–5x what a financial buyer would pay
- Team is fatigued and a soft landing creates more value than a forced restart
- Product-market fit is soft but the team/tech has durable value
- Personal liquidity is a real consideration and honesty about this is healthier than pretending

## When you should say no to acquisition

- Revenue is compounding and the multiple implied is below 6x forward ARR with real growth
- The acquirer's integration plan destroys what made you valuable
- Your team will vest into cultural misery
- The premium over continuing is less than 30% risk-adjusted

## Typical timeline (6-month case)

| Week | Phase |
|------|-------|
| 0–2 | Initial conversations, NDA, high-level fit |
| 3–6 | IOI (indication of interest), preliminary diligence |
| 7–10 | LOI negotiation and signing, exclusivity begins |
| 11–18 | Deep diligence (financial, legal, tech, customer) |
| 19–22 | Definitive agreement drafting |
| 23–24 | Signing, announcements, closing |

## LOI must-haves before you sign exclusivity

1. Price with clear mechanics (cash vs stock vs earnout split)
2. Escrow percentage and duration (typically 10–15%, 12–18 months)
3. Employee retention package size (target 15–20% of deal value)
4. Exclusivity period (45–60 days is standard, not 90)
5. Break-up fee if buyer walks without cause
6. Walk conditions you can invoke
7. Key employee definition and their terms

## Deal value realities

- Stock consideration from private acquirers is often worth 50–70% of claimed value
- Earnouts pay out under 40% of target on average
- Escrow is usually reduced by 10–30% via indemnification claims
- Assume 75% of headline deal value is real

## Red flags in buyer behavior

- Repeated scope expansion during exclusivity
- Due diligence questions that feel like competitive intelligence
- Reluctance to share their board's actual approval
- Evaluation of multiple targets concurrently (fine to know, concerning if they won't commit)
- Pushback on standard reps and warranties that their own lawyers wrote last quarter

## As the acquirer: what to actually diligence

- Top 10 customer concentration and churn risk
- Cohort retention, not just ARR growth
- Actual code quality (buy a code audit, 15k gets you real signal)
- Key person dependencies (what breaks if founder 1 leaves)
- IP assignment hygiene (every contributor, especially early contractors)
- Pending litigation and employment issues
- Customer contracts' assignability clauses

## Integration planning starts before close

Decide day 1:

- Does the brand survive or fold in?
- Who reports to whom on day 1?
- Which customers get a personal call from whom?
- What's the 100-day integration plan and who owns each workstream?

## Founder psychology

Selling is grief even when it's the right move. Expect:

- Identity crisis at 60–90 days post-close
- Retention package that feels like golden handcuffs by month 6
- Cultural friction you did not predict
- Loss of autonomy you underestimated

Plan a real off-ramp. Know your earn-out trigger dates. Have a project waiting.

---

*Acquisitions are decisions made once and lived with for years. Spend the time.*
