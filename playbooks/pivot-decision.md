# Playbook: Pivot Decision

## The Temptation
When things aren't working, pivoting feels like progress. It's often a
way to avoid the harder work of fixing what you have.

## First: Is It Actually Not Working?

A pivot is only valid after you've ruled out:

1. **Execution problem** — the idea is right, you've executed it poorly
2. **Timing problem** — it's too early (market not ready) or too late (window closed)
3. **GTM problem** — right product, wrong audience or channel
4. **Sales problem** — you have product-market fit but can't sell it
5. **Retention problem** — you acquire but can't retain (usually product-market fit issue in disguise)

Test: Talk to 20 ex-customers. If they tell you the idea is great but the
product didn't deliver, it's execution, not idea. Don't pivot — fix.

## The Real Pivot Triggers

Only pivot when ALL these are true:

- [ ] You've been working on it for 12+ months
- [ ] You've run 3+ major iterations on the current concept
- [ ] Top of funnel is broken (not just conversion) — people don't even care to try it
- [ ] Retention is near-zero after product fixes
- [ ] Team has lost conviction
- [ ] A clear alternative hypothesis has emerged from user conversations

Five out of six = serious pivot conversation. Six out of six = go.

## Types of Pivots (Ranked by Severity)

### Zoom-in pivot (cheapest)
"One feature is the whole product." You had 5 features; 4 are waste.
Example: Burbn → Instagram (social check-in app → just photo sharing)

### Zoom-out pivot
"Our product should do more than we thought." Rarely works at small scale.
Usually means you were solving a micro-problem.

### Customer segment pivot
"Right product, wrong users." You're selling to SMB; real buyer is enterprise.
Example: Flickr's photo management → camera-phone social

### Platform pivot
"Application → platform" or vice versa. Very expensive.

### Technology pivot
"Same problem, different tech." Radical.
Example: Groupon started as a petition site (The Point)

### Full pivot
"Different problem entirely." 2 years in, starting over. Possible, but
consider starting a new company with new equity math.

## The Pivot Process (4-6 weeks)

### Week 1: Diagnose
- [ ] Read the last 6 months of board decks — what did you say?
- [ ] 20 user interviews — what do they really want?
- [ ] Team conversations — who still believes, who doesn't?
- [ ] Financial check — runway to support pivot period

### Week 2: Generate hypotheses
- List 3-5 pivot directions. For each:
  - What's the problem?
  - Who has it?
  - What would you build?
  - Evidence you have that this is real?

### Week 3: Test top 2
- Landing pages or mockups
- 10 customer conversations per direction
- Do any resonate immediately (unprompted "when can I buy this")?

### Week 4: Decide
- Board meeting with pivot proposal
- Team alignment conversation
- Investor communication
- Runway check — can you afford this pivot?

### Week 5-6: Execute
- Kill old product (but not sudden; communicate with users)
- Start new product
- Reset metrics baseline

## The Hard Conversations

### With your board
Don't minimize. Say: "We're pivoting from X to Y. Here's what we learned.
Here's the evidence for Y. Here's the plan. Here's the runway. Here's
the decision we need your buy-in on."

### With your team
Early, not late. Before rumors. "We're pivoting because [specific
evidence]. Here's what changes. Here's what doesn't. Here's what I need
from each of you."

Expect 1-3 people to leave. That's healthy. Team who doesn't believe in
the new direction should not be there.

### With your customers
Existing customers deserve honesty. Options:
- Open source the old product
- Hand customers to a friendly company
- Continue to support existing customers for 6-12 months while building new
- Refund pro-rata remaining time

### With investors
Lead investor first. "We're pivoting. Here's the analysis. Here's what
we need from you." Follow-up email to full cap table.

## Post-Pivot Reality Check

First 30 days post-pivot:
- Do users of the NEW product pull harder than users of the OLD product at equivalent stage?
- Is team energy up or down?
- Are investors engaged or disengaged?

If energy isn't visibly different by day 30, you may have pivoted to
another version of the same problem.

## Anti-Patterns
- Pivoting after 3 months (too early — haven't tested execution)
- Pivoting 3+ times (probably a founder problem, not an idea problem)
- Pivoting to a "bigger market" without evidence anyone there wants it
- Pivoting without runway for 6+ months of new idea
- Pivoting silently — team/board/investors only hear when things look good
