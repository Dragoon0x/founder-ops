# Playbook: Firing Someone

## The First Question
"Have I given this person a real chance to succeed?"

- Clear expectations (written)
- Direct feedback on performance (multiple times, documented)
- Resources and time to improve
- 30+ days of concerted improvement effort

If yes to all: proceed. If any no: fix that first, then revisit in 30 days.

## The Second Question
"If I could wake up tomorrow and this person wasn't at the company,
would I feel relief?"

Unambiguous yes = proceed. Maybe = wait and clarify feedback first.

## The Third Question
"Have I told them directly that their performance is not acceptable?"

Most firings are a surprise because managers soften feedback. If they
don't know they're at risk, you didn't actually manage them.

## The Timeline

### Week -4 to -1: Be clear or don't fire

If you haven't had 2+ direct conversations about underperformance, YOU
are the problem, not them. Have the conversations. Give them 30 days.
Document everything.

If you have had those conversations and they haven't improved, proceed.

### Day -7: Prep

- **Legal review:** Run by employment counsel. Even small companies need this.
- **Severance:** Standard is 2 weeks for <1 year, 4 weeks for 1-2 years, more for senior. Add more if you want a clean separation.
- **Insurance:** COBRA info ready
- **Equipment:** What's theirs to keep, what to return
- **Access:** Plan for deactivating accounts
- **Announcement:** Draft the team statement

### Day 0: The conversation

**When:** Monday or Tuesday, mid-morning. Not Friday afternoon (worse weekend).

**Where:** Private room. Video call if remote.

**Who:** You + HR or witness if team is >10. Just you if <10.

**How long:** 15-30 minutes. Don't drag it out.

### The Script

Open with the decision. Don't bury the lede.

"[Name], I'm letting you know that today is your last day at [company].
I've made the decision to end your employment."

Pause. Let them respond.

If they ask why:
"We've talked about [specific performance issue] over the past [period].
Despite the conversations, the situation hasn't improved to where I
believe it can. This is the right decision for the company."

If they get emotional:
Give space. Don't argue. Don't explain more than once. Don't try to
make them feel better — they won't.

If they argue:
Listen. Don't debate. Say "I understand. This is my decision. Let's
talk about what happens next."

### The Logistics (next 5 minutes)

- Severance details (written, handed to them)
- Final paycheck timing
- Insurance continuation
- Equipment return
- Today's access changes (immediate or end of day)
- Departure communication (what you'll say to team)
- Reference policy (most companies: dates of employment only; for
  performance-based firings, sometimes "please reach out to [HR]")

### Immediate After

- They leave (often cleared out immediately; sometimes given 1 hour to
  pack up)
- You deactivate: email, Slack, tools, GitHub, etc.
- You tell the team.

## The Team Announcement

**Short. Boring. Clean.**

Bad:
"[Name] is no longer with the company due to [specific performance issues]."

Good:
"[Name] is no longer with [company]. I want to thank them for their
contributions. [Remaining colleague] will be taking over [their
responsibilities] for now. If you have questions, come find me."

Never disparage. Never explain the firing. Never apologize for it
either. Clean and confident.

### The Hallway Questions

People will ask: "Why?"

Your answer: "I'm not going to get into details out of respect for
[name]. It was my decision. I believe it's right for the company."

Repeat as needed.

## What Not to Do

### ❌ Fire for "not a culture fit" without specifics
Either it's performance (say so) or it's bias (fix yourself).

### ❌ Fire on Friday afternoon
They ruminate all weekend with no recourse. Cruel.

### ❌ Fire publicly or via Slack
Even fully remote, this is a 1:1 video call.

### ❌ Give false hope
"We might bring you back" — don't say this unless you mean it.

### ❌ Overpromise on references
"I'll give you a great reference" — only if true. Otherwise "I'll confirm
dates of employment."

### ❌ Undershare with HR / legal
Document the conversation immediately after. Memories are unreliable.

### ❌ Avoid the topic afterward
Address the team's uncertainty head-on. Don't let it fester.

## If You're Firing For Cause

Stealing, harassment, severe misconduct — the playbook is different:

- Immediate suspension (paid or unpaid per employment law)
- Investigation
- Termination with no severance (check employment agreement)
- Legal counsel ESSENTIAL
- Possibly report to authorities (e.g., fraud, harassment)

Do not try to wing this. Get legal help same day.

## The Aftermath

**First 48 hours:**
- Team is watching you. Be calm. Be normal. Don't over-explain.
- Whoever is absorbing the work needs clear priorities.
- Customer-facing changes communicated immediately (if they were customer-facing).

**First 2 weeks:**
- Monitor team sentiment. Usually relief outweighs shock if firing was overdue.
- If unexpected by team, you need to explain context better.
- Redistribute work or start hiring for backfill.

**First 90 days:**
- Check that the performance issue you fired over doesn't recur with others.
- If it does, you may have a systemic problem (hiring, management, or role design).

## For You (The Manager)

Firing someone is hard. Even when it's right.

- Don't pretend it's nothing.
- Don't flagellate yourself.
- Talk to another founder or mentor.
- Take 24 hours before making any other big decisions.
