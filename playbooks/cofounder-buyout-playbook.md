# Co-founder Buyout Playbook

Roughly 60% of co-founder pairs experience a serious conflict in the first 4 years. 20% end in a buyout or forced exit. If you're in that conversation, here's how to do it cleanly.

## When a buyout is the right move

- Strategic disagreement that cannot be reconciled through discussion
- Role mismatch where the co-founder's stage-fit has passed
- Trust breakdown from specific behaviors (not vibes)
- Performance issue that peers have raised repeatedly
- Health, family, or personal circumstance requiring exit

## When a buyout is the wrong move

- One bad quarter
- Disagreement about a single decision
- Investor pressure without founder alignment
- Cash offer from outside that makes one party restless
- Anxiety about dilution post-next-round

## The conversation that starts it

Have it once, directly, when you're both calm. Not email. Not Slack. In person if possible.

> "I want to talk about how we're working together. Over the last [timeframe], I've noticed [specific behaviors]. I don't think we can continue as [structure]. I want to explore [options]: change in roles, bring in a mediator, structured buyout."

Listen to their response. Don't rebut for 48 hours.

## Structuring the buyout

### Price

- Use last round's preferred price as ceiling
- Apply common stock discount (typically 25–40% of preferred)
- Multiply by their vested and unvested shares (buyout may accelerate some)
- For unvested shares: typically repurchased at original purchase price

### Payment terms

- Lump sum is cleanest but strains cash
- Installments over 12–24 months more common
- Stock buyback (company buys shares, reduces outstanding)
- Cross-purchase (remaining founders buy directly)
- Third-party purchase (new investor buys their stake — rare)

### Vesting adjustments

- Accelerate unvested only in "termination without cause" scenarios
- Double-trigger acceleration reserved for change-of-control
- Extend exercise window for vested options (7–10 years post-termination is kind)

### Non-compete and non-solicit

- Non-compete: 12 months, narrow scope, with comp during period
- Non-solicit: 12–18 months for employees and customers
- Confidentiality: perpetual
- Non-disparagement: mutual

## Legal requirements

- Separate counsel for each co-founder (do not share a lawyer)
- Board approval required for stock repurchase
- Shareholder notice if material
- 409A consideration if share price materially changes
- Tax structure (qualified small business stock implications)

## Communication

### To the team

> "[Co-founder] is transitioning out of their role at [company]. We've aligned on a clear handoff plan. [Who is taking over what]. We both believe this is the right move for the company and for [co-founder]. I'm not going to share financial details, but I want you to hear it from us directly."

### To investors

- Lead investor: in person or video, before anyone else
- Other major investors: call within 24 hours
- Angels and smaller: email with offer to call

### To customers

- Only if co-founder was primary relationship for them
- Personal email or call from remaining founder
- Clear transition contact

### To press

- Unless one of you is public-facing, no press release
- Prepare holding statement in case of leak
- Coordinate with co-founder on language

## Legal agreements to sign

1. Separation and release agreement
2. Stock repurchase agreement or cross-purchase agreement
3. Amended or terminated founder agreement
4. Non-compete / non-solicit / confidentiality agreement
5. Mutual non-disparagement
6. Consulting agreement (if applicable, time-limited)

## Founder psychology

For the departing co-founder:
- Grief is real, especially if forced
- Identity crisis at 30–90 days
- Financial relief or pressure depending on terms
- Anger at decisions made without them

For the remaining co-founder:
- Survivor guilt
- Operational overload
- Team skepticism that must be earned back
- Loneliness at the top

Both should have therapists. Seriously. The decisions you make in this window affect the next decade.

## 90-day post-separation

- Team town hall: unfiltered Q&A
- Leadership reshuffle confirmed
- Replacement plan for critical roles
- Board meeting to re-baseline the plan
- Founder's personal reset (vacation, therapy, whatever restores judgment)

---

*Co-founder breakups are company-forming events. Handle them like you'd handle a divorce: with lawyers, with kindness, and with the goal that everyone can look back without shame.*
