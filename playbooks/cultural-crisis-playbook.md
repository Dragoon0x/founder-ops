# Cultural Crisis Playbook

Your company has a cultural crisis when a specific event (or accumulation of events) damages trust between leadership and the team. This includes: a public harassment claim, a Glassdoor exodus, a mass departure, a toxic leader's behavior coming to light, a layoff handled poorly, or a values breach that went unaddressed.

## Diagnosis

Not every bad week is a crisis. Real cultural crisis signals:

- Voluntary attrition spikes (3x baseline)
- Glassdoor rating drops >0.5 in a quarter
- Anonymous channels light up (Blind, public Slack)
- Press coverage or social media thread
- Multiple departures cite the same reason
- Candidates decline at final round citing reputation
- Referrals stop

## First week

### Listen first

Before announcing anything:

1. Skip-level 1:1s with 10–15 ICs across affected functions
2. Focus group (5–7 people) with HR partner present
3. Anonymous survey (use Culture Amp, Lattice, or just Google Forms)
4. Exit interview data from last 90 days
5. Slack / email sentiment (if your tools support it, ethically)

Ask:
- What is the biggest trust breakdown?
- What would need to be true for you to recommend working here?
- What has leadership done or not done that made this worse?
- What specific behaviors or events triggered this?

Spend 5–7 days listening before you propose anything.

### Name the problem

Do not soft-pedal. In all-hands:

> "I want to name what's happening. Over the last [timeframe], [specific events]. People are leaving. Morale is low. I've spent the last week listening. I want to share what I heard."

Share what you heard. Specifically. With their words.

### Commit to structural changes, not just words

Words without change make it worse. Announce:

- Specific process changes (hiring, performance, compensation)
- Specific people changes (who's in charge of what)
- Specific cadence changes (more all-hands, skip-levels, surveys)
- Specific accountability (what you're doing differently)

Give dates. Commit publicly.

## The 30 / 60 / 90 day repair

**Day 30**
- Listening tour complete
- Problem named publicly
- Initial commitments made
- If specific individuals were the source, decisions made
- Policy changes drafted

**Day 60**
- Policy changes implemented
- Skip-levels continuing
- Survey re-run, baseline vs 30 days prior
- Specific wins celebrated (but not performative)
- Management training if applicable

**Day 90**
- Survey re-run, trajectory clear
- If no improvement, deeper changes (exec changes, restructure)
- Public retrospective (internal, not PR)
- New normal established

## Who leaves

In a real cultural crisis, someone usually has to leave. Options:

1. **The bad actor**: the person whose behavior caused the breach
2. **The enabler**: the manager who didn't act
3. **The executive**: the leader who set the tone
4. **Yourself**: if you're the CEO and the culture is you

This is the hardest decision. But: if the root cause is a person, keeping them sends the message that behavior is tolerated. The cost of keeping them is more departures over the next 90 days.

## What not to do

- **Don't run a values-rewriting exercise** in the first 30 days. It looks performative.
- **Don't announce a diversity hire** in response to a racism or sexism issue. It's tokenism.
- **Don't fire the messenger**. The person who surfaced the issue is often the most valuable.
- **Don't silence via NDA**. It creates worse long-term reputation.
- **Don't do a company offsite**. Retreats do not rebuild trust.
- **Don't hire a consultancy** to tell you what to do. Your team will tell you if you listen.
- **Don't promote "culture" roles** in the middle of a crisis. It reads as substitution for accountability.

## The role of middle management

Middle managers are the trust translators. In a cultural crisis:

- Train them on what to say (and what not to say)
- Give them real authority to make local changes
- Protect them from blame-shifting
- Listen to them first; they know the team

## The role of HR

- Legal guardrails on decisions
- Process owner for investigations
- Not the face of change (that's the CEO)
- Not the defender of accused (that's their lawyer)
- Independent enough to push back on CEO

## External communication

- Generally: silent. Culture is internal.
- If press asks: brief factual statement, no apology theater
- If investor asks: honest about issue and actions
- Recruits ask: honest about what happened and what changed
- Customers: only if it affects them

## Founder psychology

Cultural crisis feels personal because it is. You built this. Some of it.

- Separate the facts from your shame
- Get a coach
- Don't over-apologize
- Don't under-apologize
- Take responsibility for what's yours
- Do not take responsibility for what isn't

Six months from now, you'll either be leading a stronger company or you'll be out. There's no middle path.

## Rebuilding trust

Trust rebuilds through:

1. **Consistent action**: doing what you said you'd do for 90+ days
2. **Accountability without theater**: owning mistakes, changing behavior
3. **Visible hard decisions**: letting people go when required, even if uncomfortable
4. **Giving away power**: structural changes that reduce founder control
5. **Transparency**: more information shared, not less
6. **Time**: 12–18 months minimum

*Culture is what your people say about the company when you're not in the room. Make it worth saying.*
