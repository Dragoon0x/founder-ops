# Playbook: Bridge Round

## When to Use This
You raised a round, ran it longer than planned, and need 6-12 months more
runway before you can raise the next round at a higher valuation.

## First: Is This Actually a Bridge?

A bridge is short-term capital to reach a specific milestone that unlocks
a bigger round. If you can't articulate the milestone, it's not a bridge —
it's life support.

**Real bridges:**
- "We need 6 months to get to $2M ARR which unlocks Series A"
- "We need 9 months to ship the product expansion that doubles ACV"
- "We need 4 months to close 3 enterprise deals in pipeline"

**Not a bridge (life support):**
- "We need to keep operating"
- "We need to buy time to figure out what to do"
- "We need to avoid layoffs"

If it's life support, the honest path is often: right-size the team,
extend runway to 18+ months, and raise when there's a real story.

## Who to Ask

**Insiders (existing investors)**: Always first. They have context, no
re-underwriting, can move in 2 weeks.

**New investors**: Harder. A new lead for a bridge signals existing
investors lost faith. Do only if: a) existing investors are tapped out,
or b) a specific strategic investor can add real value.

## The Math

Bridges are typically:
- **Size:** 20-50% of last round (e.g., $500K-1.5M bridge on a $3M seed)
- **Structure:** SAFE or convertible note
- **Discount:** 15-25% to next round
- **Cap:** Either no cap or cap at recent valuation

## Timeline (4-6 weeks for insider bridge)

### Week 1: Internal alignment
- Tell your board first. Before any outreach.
- Get your lead investor on a 1:1 call. Ask directly: "Would you participate?"
- If yes: get soft commit on size. This determines the round.

### Week 2: Pro-rata outreach
- Contact every existing investor with pro-rata
- Send updated plan, burn projection, milestone list
- Clear ask: "We're raising $X at [structure]. Your pro-rata is $Y."

### Week 3: Fill the round
- Close soft commits into signed docs
- If short, bring in 1-2 new angels
- Do NOT do a full external process for a small bridge

### Week 4-6: Document and close
- One lawyer, simple docs
- Wire dates staggered if multiple parties

## Messaging

### To investors (honest)
"We've made meaningful progress in [specific things] but growth has been
slower than planned on [specific metric]. To reach the Series A story
we're building toward, we need 8 more months. We're raising $X from
existing investors with [terms]."

### To team (reassuring but honest)
"We're extending our runway with additional capital. Business is
[candid assessment]. Here's our plan to hit [specific milestone] by [date]."

### To customers (no change)
Don't discuss fundraising with customers during a bridge. If they ask:
"We recently raised additional capital to support our long-term product
roadmap."

## Red Flags (Bridge Becomes Death Spiral)

- **Existing investors won't participate:** Strongest possible signal. Diagnose.
- **You've bridged before:** Second bridge means you haven't found the
  business. Don't raise a third — change course.
- **Team morale is crashing:** A bridge can feel like failure internally.
  Communication matters more than the money.
- **CEO is burnt out:** Money doesn't fix a burnt founder. Address first.

## Post-Bridge Plan

The day the bridge closes, you owe everyone:
1. A written plan with weekly milestones for the next 6 months
2. Weekly (or biweekly) updates to investors
3. A real "go/no-go" checkpoint at month 3

## Output
- `output/bridge-ask-[investor].md` — personalized asks
- `output/bridge-milestone-plan.md` — 6-month plan with weekly checkpoints

## Anti-Patterns
- Raising a bridge to "see how it goes" without a plan
- Taking money from existing investors at a higher valuation (looks insider-ish; blocks future rounds)
- Diluting heavily for a tiny bridge (do math — sometimes 4 months of cost cuts = same effect)
- Not talking to your board before the bridge
