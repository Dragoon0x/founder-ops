# Playbook: Respond to Competitor Launch

## When to Run
Within 48 hours of a meaningful competitor announcement:
- Major funding raised
- New product launched
- Major customer win announced
- Aggressive pricing change
- Significant feature shipped
- Category play (they're redefining the space)

## Phase 1: Information Gathering (First 24 hours)

### Step 1: Read Everything
Run `/founder-ops compete`

Read the actual announcement, not takes about it:
- Press release
- Product pages (screenshots)
- Pricing changes (compare to old)
- Their CEO's LinkedIn/Twitter
- Reactions from customers/market

### Step 2: Separate Signal from Noise
Most competitor launches are less significant than they look in the moment.

**Real signals:**
- Customers actively considering switching
- Journalists writing about it as a trend (not just an event)
- Your deals getting cited as comparisons
- Your team members asking what it means

**Not real signals:**
- VC Twitter noise
- One LinkedIn post
- A single journalist take
- Your imagination

### Step 3: Talk to Customers
Talk to 5-10 customers within 48 hours:
- "Have you seen [competitor] launched X?"
- "What did you think?"
- "Does it change anything for you?"

If most customers shrug → it's not a crisis.
If multiple are reconsidering → take it seriously.

## Phase 2: Strategic Assessment (Day 2-3)

### Step 4: Assess Impact on You
Run `/founder-ops strategy`

Specific questions:
- Does this threaten your positioning?
- Does this commoditize a feature you relied on?
- Does this change the category definition?
- Does this change your pricing power?
- Does this change investor/market perception?

### Step 5: Differentiate from Panic vs Real Threat

**Panic signs (you can relax):**
- You're the only one worried
- They launched something flashy but functionality-weak
- Their announcement focused on marketing, not customers
- Your customer retention is unchanged

**Real threat signs (plan carefully):**
- Win rates dropping in competitive deals
- Customers mentioning them unprompted
- Analyst reports shifting narrative
- Your team starting to doubt positioning

## Phase 3: Response Options (Day 3-5)

### Step 6: Choose Your Response Mode

**Option A: Ignore**
- When: Launch is irrelevant to your segment OR it's weaker than it looks
- How: Don't mention them, don't react publicly
- Risk: Seems like you're not aware

**Option B: Differentiate**
- When: Their launch validates the category but you play differently
- How: Reinforce your unique angle (not a direct response)
- Most common correct response

**Option C: Counter-attack**
- When: Their launch directly threatens your positioning
- How: Public content explaining why your approach is better
- Risk: Looks defensive, gives them airtime

**Option D: Pivot or Accelerate**
- When: Their launch changes the game entirely
- How: Modify roadmap, messaging, or strategy materially
- Risk: Reactive strategy is often wrong

**Option E: Embrace**
- When: Their launch expands the market in ways that help you too
- How: Publicly support the category; emphasize your version
- Often: best response for category plays

### Step 7: Customer Communication (if needed)

Only proactively communicate to customers if:
- Customers are already asking
- Your product directly addresses something they highlighted
- They specifically named you in comparisons

Don't send a defensive email to your whole customer base. It looks weak.

Template (for sales if customers ask):
```
Thanks for flagging [competitor]'s announcement.

Yes, we saw it. Here's our honest take:
- [What they did well]
- [Where we disagree / do it differently]
- [Why we still believe our approach]

Happy to discuss further. We're not reacting to [competitor] — we're
executing on the plan we had before they launched.
```

## Phase 4: Internal Communication (Day 3-5)

### Step 8: Team Communication
Run `/founder-ops internal-comms`

Address the team before rumors spread:
- Explicit acknowledgment of the launch
- Your reading of what it means
- What it doesn't mean (typically: "we're still on our path")
- Specific adjustments (if any)
- Any action items assigned

### Step 9: Board/Investor Communication
Run `/founder-ops update`

If significant:
- Brief update within 48 hours to board
- Summary of their launch
- Your assessment
- Any strategic adjustments
- Asks (intros, referrals, etc.)

Don't be panicky. Don't be dismissive. Be analytical.

## Phase 5: Execute (Week 1-4)

### Step 10: Specific Actions

Depending on response mode:

**Differentiate:**
- Sharpen sales talk track
- Create content that reinforces your unique angle
- Customer stories that show what you do they don't
- Update competitive battlecards

**Counter-attack:**
- One targeted piece of content (not 10)
- Customer proof points specific to the comparison
- Pricing/positioning sharpening
- Don't engage their CEO on social media (never works)

**Pivot / Accelerate:**
- Re-run `/founder-ops strategy`
- Communicate new plan clearly (internal and external)
- Move with conviction (not desperation)

### Step 11: Monitor Win Rates
Track win rates vs them for the next 90 days:
- Before their launch
- After their launch
- After your response

If win rates recover → response worked.
If win rates keep dropping → response wasn't enough.

## Phase 6: Long-Term Implications

### Step 12: Retro After 90 Days
Run `/founder-ops retro`

- Was our assessment right?
- Did our response work?
- What did we learn about our market position?
- What would we do differently next time?

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Bad |
|---------|-------------|
| Responding within hours (reactive) | Looks defensive, poorly thought through |
| Copying their features immediately | Signals you were wrong, commoditizes both |
| Engaging their CEO on Twitter | Never wins, always loses |
| Rewriting your entire strategy based on their launch | They're not right about the market |
| Ignoring when you should respond | Customers think you're asleep |
| Panicking internally | Team loses confidence |
| Silent treatment to your team | Rumors fill the void |
