# Playbook: Founder Burnout (recognizing and recovering)

## Why This Playbook Exists

Founder burnout doesn't show up as "I'm burned out." It shows up as:
- Decision fatigue (every choice feels equally heavy)
- Loss of curiosity about your product / market
- Snapping at the team
- Avoiding the things that used to be exciting (customer calls, product work)
- Physical symptoms: sleep issues, weight changes, no exercise

If you recognize 2+ of these in yourself: this playbook applies to you.

## The Diagnostic

### Energy levels (1-10, daily check)
Track for 7 days:
- Morning energy: ___
- Energy after first meeting: ___
- Energy at 3pm: ___
- Energy when family/friends call: ___

If average is < 5 for 7+ days: real burnout, not a bad week.

### Decision quality
Look at the last 5 important decisions you made.
- Did you take time to think them through?
- Did you make them with confidence or by default?
- Are you proud of them?

If most were defaults / hasty / not proud → cognitive overload.

### Relationship signals
- Are you canceling personal plans regularly?
- Are family/friends asking if you're okay?
- Are you reaching for substances (caffeine, alcohol, weed) more than usual?

These are often the most reliable signals because we hide it from ourselves.

## Immediate Interventions (this week)

### 1. Take 48 hours completely off
Not "work from home." Not "check Slack lightly." Off.

Tell the team: "I'm offline Saturday and Sunday. Slack me only for actual emergencies (define what counts as emergency)."

What "actual emergencies" means:
- Production down
- Major customer churning
- Legal/security incident

NOT emergencies:
- A deal that needs your input (it can wait Monday)
- A hire's start date question
- An investor wants to chat

### 2. Reintroduce one daily habit
Pick ONE:
- 30 min walk every morning
- 8 hours of sleep, every night
- One meal eaten away from a screen
- Phone-free for 1 hour after waking up

Don't try to fix everything. One thing. Sustained for 2 weeks.

### 3. Tell someone the truth
Pick one of:
- Co-founder
- Therapist
- One trusted advisor / friend who's a founder

Tell them how you're actually doing. Not "I'm tired but it's fine" — the actual truth.

Most founders haven't told ANYONE the real situation. The hiding is part of the problem.

## Structural Interventions (this month)

### 1. Audit your calendar
Look at last 4 weeks. Categorize every meeting:
- **Energizing** (you'd schedule it again happily)
- **Necessary** (not fun but needed)
- **Drainers** (you dread these, they don't produce much value)

Cancel or delegate 50% of the drainers.

### 2. Identify the "wrong job" tasks
What are you doing that isn't actually your job?
- Your CTO should review code, not you (after a certain stage)
- Your CRO should run sales calls, not you (after a certain stage)
- Your CFO/finance lead should run vendor invoices, not you

Make a list of what you're doing that someone else should be doing.
Pick the top 3 and offload them in the next 2 weeks.

### 3. Set work hours (yes, really)
Set them. Communicate them. Mostly stick to them.

"I'm working 8am-6pm M-F. I'll respond to messages outside that on Monday."

The world will not end. Customers will not leave. Investors will respect you more.

### 4. Build a real exec team or advisor circle
If everything routes through you, you'll burn out forever.

If you can afford it: hire a chief of staff or COO.
If you can't: build a board of 3-5 advisors who you trust enough to call when you're stuck.

## Long-Term Recovery (next 90 days)

### Therapy / Coaching
A founder coach (specialized) or therapist. Not optional. Most VCs will cover it.

### Physical health
- Resting heart rate (track it; rising trend = stress)
- Sleep (7-8 hours non-negotiable)
- Movement (any consistent activity)
- Nutrition (you know what you should be doing)

### Social connection
- Other founders going through similar things (Discord, in-person, masterminds)
- Old friends from before the startup
- Family

### Identity diversification
The danger of being a founder: your identity becomes 100% the company.
When the company has a bad week, YOU have a bad week.

Rebuild identities outside of work:
- Hobbies
- Communities you participate in (not for business)
- Causes you care about

## When to Take Real Time Off

| Situation | Time Needed |
|-----------|-------------|
| Bad month at the company | 1 weekend |
| Bad quarter / fundraise stress | 1 week (real vacation) |
| Crisis (firing, shutdown of major customer, near-death moment) | 2 weeks within 90 days |
| Sustained burnout (3+ months of feeling depleted) | 1 month sabbatical |

The thing nobody tells you: the company will be fine. Sometimes BETTER. Your team gets to step up.

## When to Step Down

Rare but real. Sometimes the best thing for the company AND for you is for you to not be CEO anymore.

Signs:
- You've been burned out 12+ months despite trying to fix it
- You no longer believe in the mission
- The company needs different skills (operator vs founder)
- You're staying out of obligation, not energy

This is not failure. Some of the best company outcomes happened when founders stepped into board / chairman / chief vision officer roles and let operators run.

## Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Problem |
|---------|---------|
| "I'll rest when this round closes" | The next thing always comes |
| "I have to be on / available 24/7" | You don't. Nobody does. |
| "If I take time off, it'll all fall apart" | If true, that's a structural problem to fix |
| "Other founders work harder" | They probably don't. They hide it like you do. |
| Performative hustle on Twitter | Counterproductive — find authenticity |
| "I'll fix it after [milestone]" | You won't. Fix it now. |

## A Note from the Trenches

Building a company is a marathon, not a sprint. The founders who win are
the ones who're still standing in year 7. That requires sustainability.

Take care of yourself. The company depends on you taking care of yourself.
