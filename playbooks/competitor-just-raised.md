# Playbook: A Competitor Just Raised $X

## Don't Panic

A competitor raising money doesn't change your reality. It changes the optics.
Most of what feels urgent is not. Here's how to think clearly.

## Hour 0: Read Carefully

Don't react. Read the announcement, then read the comments.

Extract:
- **Amount:** Real signal of investor confidence (or marketing inflation)
- **Stage:** Is this their seed, A, B? (Bigger raise = more pressure to grow)
- **Investors:** Tier 1 lead = social proof, but doesn't mean they win
- **Use of funds:** What they're explicitly going to do (often the most useful info)
- **Who they hired:** Recent senior hires telegraph priorities
- **Customer logos:** Are they overlapping with yours?

Then, pause for 24 hours before doing anything.

## Day 1: Honest Assessment

Ask yourself:

### Does this change your thesis?
- If your thesis was "this market is too small for VC funding" → it just changed
- If your thesis was "we're the only ones noticing this opportunity" → false now
- If your thesis was about a specific approach → does their approach validate or threaten it?

### Does this change your customer's reality?
- Will customers wait to evaluate them before buying you?
- Will customers ask "why not just use [Competitor]?"
- Will customers churn TO them? (Some always do — it's not catastrophic)

### Does this change your fundraising?
- If you're raising: it's probably easier (validated category)
- If you're not raising: it doesn't matter for 6+ months

## Day 1-7: Customer Sentiment Check

Reach out to 5-10 customers. Don't ask "are you switching?" Ask:

```
"Hey, I saw [Competitor] just raised. Curious what your reaction was —
does it change how you think about this space?"
```

Listen for:
- "Doesn't matter, you're solving [our problem] better" → good signal
- "Interesting, I was curious about them anyway" → at risk
- "Actually we were considering switching" → real risk, deeper conversation needed
- "Who?" → great signal, you're more relevant than you thought

Aggregate the responses. The truth is in the data, not in panic.

## Strategic Responses (pick AT MOST one)

### Response 1: Ignore
Default if: Their raise validates the category but doesn't materially change customer behavior.

What you do: Nothing. Continue building. Maybe one tweet acknowledging the news.

### Response 2: Sharpen positioning
If: They're trying to compete on the same axis, but you have a real differentiation.

What you do:
- Update landing page to emphasize differentiation
- Train sales/support on competitive talk track
- Publish a thoughtful "here's how we're different" piece (not a hit piece)
- Reference past customer wins where you beat them

### Response 3: Niche down
If: They're going horizontal/broad, you can win by going narrow/deep.

What you do:
- Pick a specific segment they can't serve well
- Restate your positioning around that segment
- Build features that serve that niche specifically
- Become "the [segment] choice" while they're "the everyone choice"

### Response 4: Counter-fundraise
If: You're already fundraising or about to. Their raise creates urgency for you.

What you do:
- Use the news to accelerate your own fundraise
- "Category is hot, we're the better option" is a real story
- Don't try to match their valuation — outperform on growth/efficiency

### Response 5: Collaborate
Rare but powerful: If: You serve different segments, partnership > competition.

What you do:
- Reach out to their CEO with a partnership idea
- Joint customers, integration, content
- Frame as "rising tide lifts all boats"

## What NOT To Do

| Reaction | Why Wrong |
|----------|-----------|
| Match their messaging | You become the follower |
| Drop your prices | Race to the bottom, kills unit economics |
| Hire 10 people to "match their team" | You don't have their funding; can't sustain it |
| Tweet attacks at them | Petty, hurts your brand |
| Pivot your roadmap to chase their features | Reactive, distracts from your customers' actual needs |
| Publish "why we're better" comparison | Defensive; customers don't trust it |
| Stop publishing your own roadmap publicly | Vacuum invites speculation |
| Email investors panicking | They've seen this 100x; calm response signals strength |

## The 30-Day Re-Check

After 30 days, measure:
- Did your sales cycle slow down? (Signal: customers waiting to see)
- Did your win rate against them change?
- Did any customer leave to go to them?
- Did your inbound interest change?

Most often: nothing changes materially. The competitor's raise was a 2-day story for everyone except the founders involved.

## Strategic Reminder

The companies that win their category are rarely the ones that raised first or most.
They're the ones that built the best product for their customers.
Stay focused on YOUR customers.
