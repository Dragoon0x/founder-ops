# Customer Lawsuit Playbook

If you sell to businesses or consumers long enough, you will be sued. Most suits settle. A small number go to judgment. Your job is to protect the company without bleeding it dry.

## Hour 0: receive the complaint

Complaint arrives via:
- Process server to registered agent
- Certified mail
- Email from opposing counsel (sometimes a preview)
- Pre-litigation demand letter (take seriously — ignoring it often triggers filing)

Do not:
- Reply directly to the plaintiff
- Post about it publicly
- Email the plaintiff saying "this is baseless"
- Delete any documents (spoliation is worse than the underlying claim)

## Hour 0–4: triage

1. Forward complaint to general counsel or outside counsel immediately
2. Notify insurance carrier (E&O, D&O, general liability as applicable)
3. Issue litigation hold to all potentially-relevant custodians
4. Pull the customer contract and note arbitration clause
5. Pull the customer's support tickets and email history
6. CEO, board chair briefed

## Common claim types for SaaS

- **Breach of contract**: SLA failure, outage, non-delivery
- **Fraud**: false statements in sales cycle
- **Negligence**: data loss, security failure
- **TCPA**: unsolicited calls/texts (1500 per violation, class action magnet)
- **Privacy statutes**: BIPA, CCPA, GDPR claims
- **IP infringement**: patent, copyright
- **Employment-related**: not customer but similar workflow
- **Consumer protection**: state unfair practices acts

## First strategic decision: fight or settle

Factors favoring settle:

- Claim has merit even if exaggerated
- Ongoing customer relationship matters
- Litigation cost exceeds settlement within 12 months
- Reputational risk of discovery is high
- Jurisdiction is hostile

Factors favoring fight:

- Claim is frivolous and will chill future claims if rewarded
- Precedent matters for your business model
- Arbitration clause is enforceable and venue is favorable
- Discovery asymmetry favors you
- Plaintiff is unlikely to fund a full case

## Litigation cost model

| Stage | Typical cost |
|-------|--------------|
| Pre-answer investigation | 25–75k |
| Motion to dismiss | 50–150k |
| Discovery | 200k–2M |
| Summary judgment | 150–400k |
| Trial | 500k–3M |
| Appeal | 150–500k |

For most startups, settlements in the 50–500k range are economically rational even when the claim is weak.

## Insurance coordination

- Notify within policy window (often 30 days)
- Use panel counsel if required (negotiate if you want outside firm)
- Understand deductible and limits
- Reservation of rights letters are common, don't panic
- Get coverage opinion from broker's coverage attorney

## Discovery protocol

Litigation hold notice goes to:
- Anyone who interacted with the plaintiff
- Product/engineering leads on relevant systems
- Sales team member of record
- Customer success owner
- Executive team
- IT / data custodian

Hold covers:
- Emails (specific keywords)
- Slack/Teams messages
- Support tickets
- Product usage data
- Financial records
- Notes and documents

Do not:
- Auto-delete unless explicitly preserved
- Export data "for backup" (looks like tampering)
- Ask employees to "clean up their inboxes"

## Internal communication

- Small circle of need-to-know (not full team)
- No Slack discussion of substance (discoverable)
- No "joke" emails about the plaintiff
- Written communication assumed to be read aloud in deposition
- Privilege protection via attorney CC only when genuinely legal

## Customer communication during suit

- Continue normal service (non-breach, non-termination posture)
- Route any inbound from plaintiff to counsel
- Treat them professionally
- Do not discuss the suit with other customers
- If they ask for a reference, polite decline

## Press and public

- No comment is almost always the right answer
- Prepared statement for reporters: "We believe the claims are without merit and we will defend vigorously." Nothing more.
- Social media silence
- Internal team: single briefing, no ongoing updates unless material
- Investors: mention in quarterly update if material ($, PR, or operational)

## Settlement negotiation

- Demand letter analysis: what's the real economic ask vs stated ask
- Non-monetary terms often matter more: NDA, reference, public statement, future business
- Structure: lump sum, monthly, conditional on silence
- Mutual releases, broad scope
- No admission of liability
- Court approval if class action

## Going to trial (rare)

- 12–24 month timeline from suit to trial
- Witness prep is 20–40 hours per witness
- Trial itself: 1–3 weeks
- Appellate path adds 12–24 months

## Post-resolution

- Update product/process to prevent recurrence
- Add contract language for future customers
- Insurance renewal conversation (premiums may spike)
- Internal post-mortem (privileged)
- Public statement only if required

---

*Litigation is a distraction from building. Resolve it efficiently, learn from it, and move on.*
