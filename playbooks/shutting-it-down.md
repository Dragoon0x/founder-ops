# Playbook: Shutting It Down

## When to shut down
- You've tried 2+ pivots and none worked.
- You can't raise more money.
- You've exhausted insider bridges.
- The team is running out of energy.
- You're unable to imagine a path to a real business in the next 18
  months.

Shutting down is not failure. It's a specific decision with specific
mechanics.

## The 90-day shutdown

### Phase 1: Decision (1 week)

- Founders align (not casual — a real, written decision).
- Meet with the board (separate meeting, not regular cadence).
- Align on: shutdown date, severance policy, customer comms,
  investor comms, asset disposition.

### Phase 2: Legal and administrative (2 weeks)

- Hire dissolution counsel ($5–15K).
- Notify key vendors of coming termination.
- Plan for final payroll.
- Plan for benefits continuation (COBRA).
- If any customers on annual contracts, plan refunds or handoffs.

### Phase 3: Team (4 weeks)

Week 1: Inform leadership 1-by-1.
Week 2: Inform team in a single all-hands (not slow drip).
Weeks 3–4: Support employees in job search. Write LinkedIn
recommendations. Make introductions. Be generous with time.

Severance: 4–8 weeks is typical, paid by any remaining cash.

### Phase 4: Customers (4 weeks)

- Personal email to every customer from founder.
- Offer: full refund for unused time, OR help migrating to alternative.
- Open-source the code if possible.
- Provide data export tools.
- Respond to support tickets until shutdown date.

### Phase 5: Investors (2 weeks)

- Personal call to every investor.
- What happened, what you're doing, what's next.
- If there's any remaining cash: return it pro-rata.
- If there are assets (IP, brand, domain): decide what to do with them.

### Phase 6: Dissolution (4 weeks)

- File dissolution paperwork (state of incorporation).
- File final federal and state tax returns.
- Cancel all contracts (hosting, SaaS, office).
- Close bank accounts.
- Keep IRS records for 7 years.

## The money math

| Item | Typical cost |
|------|--------------|
| Severance (4–8 weeks × team) | 30–60% of remaining cash |
| Legal dissolution | $5–15K |
| Final payroll tax | 10–15% of severance |
| Customer refunds | Variable |
| Outstanding AR (uncollectable in shutdown) | Write off |
| Final accounting | $3–8K |

Reserve at least 3 months of overhead for shutdown itself.

If remaining cash is < severance + legal + accounting, you have
a difficult conversation with employees and investors about what's
possible.

## What to do with IP and brand

Options:
1. **Return to shareholders** (complex, rarely chosen).
2. **Open source**. Good if the community would value it. Attaches
   your name in a positive way.
3. **Sell to acquirer**. Acqui-hire or asset purchase. Can recover
   some investor capital.
4. **Shelf** (keep the IP, let the company dissolve). Enforceable for
   trademarks but requires maintenance.

Most common: sell assets if possible, open source if not,
administrative dissolution otherwise.

## The founder emotional playbook

Shutting down is emotionally significant. What helps:
- Don't isolate. Stay in contact with other founders who've been
  through it.
- Give yourself 30 days of decompression before the next thing.
- Write a post-mortem for yourself (not for public).
- Talk to a therapist.

What doesn't help:
- Immediately pivoting into the next thing ("I'll start another one
  Monday").
- Pretending it's fine publicly when it isn't.
- Isolating from former team and investors.

## What to write publicly

A shutdown announcement should:
- Be honest but not self-flagellating.
- Thank customers, team, investors.
- Explain what's happening to data, accounts, services.
- Not speculate about "what could have been."

Do not:
- Blame cofounders, investors, employees, or market conditions.
- Overclaim lessons learned.
- Announce on social media before telling the team.

## The timing rules

- Tell leadership before the team. Always.
- Tell the team before customers. Always.
- Tell customers before investors (unless investor required for
  continuity). Usually.
- Never leak. Control the narrative.

## What happens to you after

Good news: shutting down a startup is common. Most founders have at
least one shutdown in their history. It's not a scarlet letter.

Investors don't hate it. They respect:
- Honesty in the process.
- Care for employees (severance, references).
- Care for customers (refunds, data).
- Communication during the shutdown.

Investors do remember:
- Founders who ghosted.
- Founders who kept spending into the shutdown.
- Founders who fought with each other publicly.

If you handle a shutdown well, many investors will back you again.

## The career playbook post-shutdown

Timeline:
- 30 days: rest, reflect, write your own postmortem.
- 60 days: informational meetings, explore what's next.
- 90 days: narrow to 2–3 next options.
- 120 days: make a choice.

Options:
- Start another company (most common for repeat founders).
- Join another startup as an executive.
- Join a bigger company in a strategic role.
- Take a sabbatical.

All are fine. There's no "right" next move.

## Final thought

A clean shutdown is a professional act. It preserves relationships,
enables what's next, and respects the people who trusted you. It's
not the outcome you wanted, but it can be the outcome you executed
well.
