# Playbook: First Hire

## The Question Before the Question
Most founders hire too late. A few hire too early. The right moment:
**when you've been working on the same thing for 3+ weeks and haven't
shipped because you're context-switching.** That's the signal.

## Step 0: Decide Role

Write it down: "In 90 days, this person will have done [X, Y, Z]."

If you can't name 3 concrete outcomes, don't hire yet — you don't know
what the role is.

### First hire archetypes

| Your strength | Typical first hire |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Tech + product | Second engineer OR GTM hire |
| GTM + sales | Technical co-founder OR senior engineer |
| Design + product | Engineer |
| Domain expertise | Builder (engineer) |

**The meta-rule:** Hire your weakness, not a copy of your strength.

## Step 1: Write the JD in 1 hour

Use `templates/jd-template.md`. Be specific. Describe:
- What they'll build (not what they're responsible for)
- What your current output is (so they know what they're stepping into)
- What makes the role hard (honesty attracts the right people)
- Comp range (yes, include it; see `library/compensation-bands.md`)

## Step 2: Build the pipeline

Don't post on job boards first. Do these in order:

1. **Your network** (5-10 people) — personal asks
2. **Second-degree network** (LinkedIn search + warm intros) — 20-30 people
3. **Target lists** (people at companies you admire who'd downshift) — 10-20 people
4. **Inbound** (Hacker News, specialized job boards) — last resort

Target: 30-50 names total. Talk to 15-20. Make 1 hire.

## Step 3: Interview with signal (not theater)

### Screening call (30 min)
- Do they get what you're building?
- Do they have the raw capability?
- Is the life fit real? (Equity-heavy compensation, remote, small team)
- Do they have ONE thing they've done that proves the skill?

### Work sample (critical)
- Paid — $500-1500 for 4-8 hours of work
- Real problem from your product
- Judge output quality and process

### Team / culture conversations
- Have them meet 2-3 people (even if it's your co-founder and an advisor)
- "Do you want to work with this person for 2-5 years?" is the question

### References
- ASK for 3 references, but also do 2 backchannels (someone who worked with them but isn't on their list)
- Ask: "Would you hire them again? If no, why not? What's the thing you had to manage around?"

## Step 4: Close

Founder sends the offer personally. Phone call, then written.

Offer structure:
- Cash: Below-market but fair (see comp bands)
- Equity: 0.5-2.5% for first few hires (varies by stage, role, and how critical)
- Vesting: 4-year with 1-year cliff, standard
- Signing: Something meaningful (even $5K) shows commitment
- Upside: Be clear on "this is a real bet on our success"

Give 7 days to decide. Not more. Urgency shows confidence.

## Step 5: Onboard in week 1

Don't dump them in and vanish. Week 1 plan:
- Day 1: Access, tools, shadow you for a day
- Day 2-3: Pick a small real project they can ship in week 1
- Day 4-5: Ship it. Debrief together.
- End of week: "What surprised you? What would you change?"

## What Can Go Wrong

**Mis-hire:** Happens to 30% of first hires even at best companies.
Signs at 60 days:
- Ship velocity is still founder-dependent
- They're constantly asking clarifying questions instead of driving
- Energy feels like an anchor, not propulsion
- Other team members (or customers) give subtle negative feedback

**Response:** Have the hard conversation early. Set a concrete 30-day
bar. If they miss it, let them go with dignity (see `playbooks/firing-someone.md`).

## Red Flags in Candidates

- Cannot name 1 thing they built that they're proud of
- Wants guarantees on title/promotion
- Too focused on equity negotiations in first call
- Slow to respond (will also be slow on the job)
- Speaks badly about past companies without nuance
- Gets defensive about critique in work sample feedback

## Green Flags

- Asked hard questions about the business in first call
- Did homework on your product before the interview
- Completed work sample above spec
- References volunteer specific, concrete praise
- Self-aware about weaknesses and how they compensate
