# Layoff Playbook: Reducing Headcount with Dignity

No founder wants to write this doc. But layoffs are part of building. Done well, they protect the company and respect the people leaving. Done poorly, they damage both.

## Before deciding to cut

Run the alternatives:

1. Hiring freeze (saves future burn, not current)
2. Salary reductions across leadership (15–25% voluntary)
3. Reduction in benefits (commuter, wellness, meals)
4. Delayed bonuses or equity refreshes
5. Contractor termination before FTE
6. Marketing / vendor spend cuts

If these don't close the gap and runway is under 12 months, layoffs are likely necessary.

## Sizing the cut

Rule of thumb: cut deeper than feels right. The worst outcome is a second round 60 days later. Target:

- Enough reduction to give 18+ months runway
- Leaves no team with "one person doing the work of three"
- Preserves functions that drive revenue and retention
- Eliminates bets that were not working

Most founders undercut by 20–30%. Model both the "safe" number and the "this is definitely enough" number. Usually do the second.

## Selection principles

- Role-based, not performance-based (unless performance was already being managed)
- Document selection criteria in writing before pulling names
- Legal review of selection for disparate impact (age, race, gender, pregnancy, disability, protected class)
- No selections made in the last 48 hours before notification

## Preparation week (do not skip any)

**Monday**
- Finalize list with CEO, COO, legal, HR
- Separation agreements drafted
- Severance budget approved by board
- Outplacement vendor selected

**Tuesday**
- Manager talking points prepared for each affected person
- Retention list for staying team reviewed
- IT access revocation plan ready

**Wednesday**
- Manager training (2 hour session on how to do the conversation)
- Communication plan to remaining team drafted
- Customer/investor communication drafted
- Press response prepared

**Thursday**
- All-hands scheduled for day of
- Dress rehearsal of CEO message
- Manager 1:1s pre-scheduled (calendars sent after notifications)

**Friday (notification day)**
- 9:00 — individual 1:1 notifications (15 min each, never in group)
- 10:30 — all-hands to remaining team
- 11:00 — IT access revoked (same time as 1:1s ideally)
- 13:00 — customer-facing communications sent
- 15:00 — manager 1:1s with staying direct reports

## Severance benchmarks (US)

- 2 weeks per year of service, minimum 4 weeks for any tenure
- Healthcare coverage for severance period (COBRA subsidy)
- Outplacement services (BetterUp, Randstad RiseSmart: 500–2000 per person)
- Accelerated vesting on next cliff (goodwill gesture, not legally required)
- PTO paid out regardless of policy
- References offered in writing

## What to say in the 1:1

> "Thank you for making time. Today is going to be a hard day. Due to [reason], we are reducing the size of the team. Your role is being eliminated, effective today. This is not a reflection of your performance. Let me walk you through what happens next."

Then:

- Severance details
- Healthcare continuation
- Equipment return process
- Final paycheck timing
- References offer
- Separation agreement walk-through
- Questions (genuinely pause)

Do not: negotiate, apologize excessively, blame circumstances, use corporate jargon, rush.

## Public communication

- Internal memo first, public statement second
- Do not hide the number
- Do not thank "the impacted" — thank specific people if possible, or acknowledge contribution plainly
- Do not say "making the hard decisions" or "we are excited about what's next" in the same note
- Offer to be a reference personally in founder's note

## Day after

- CEO available for 1:1s with remaining team
- All-hands Q&A (no script)
- Manager check-ins with everyone who stayed
- Celebrate zero — no drinks, no "we made it", that reads as tone-deaf

## 30-day follow-through

- Track outplacement placement rates
- Honor the "I will be a reference" offer every single time
- Revisit org chart with remaining team, make reporting changes fast
- Do not backfill for at least 90 days unless absolutely critical

## Founder aftermath

Laying people off is one of the hardest things you will do. You will second-guess for months. Accept:

- You cut too late and could have done this with more runway
- You will miss some of the people you let go
- Some will land better jobs and thank you in 2 years
- Some will be angry and that is their right

Respect the grief. Do not perform recovery.

---

*The company that remains owes it to itself to be worth the sacrifice.*
