# Case Study: Co-founder Breakup Pre-Launch

## Setup
- Stage: Pre-seed, no product launched
- Team: 2 founders (CEO and CTO), 50/50 equity split, 4-year vest with 1-year cliff
- Revenue: $0
- Runway: 18 months (on $500K angel round, closed 8 months ago)
- Situation: CTO wanted to return to FAANG. Communicated it in a 1:1 at month 8 of the vesting cliff, meaning no shares had vested.

## What happened
The CEO had 72 hours before the CTO's "last day" to figure out:
1. What to do with the CTO's unvested equity.
2. How to disclose to angels.
3. Whether to hire a replacement, find a new cofounder, or continue solo.
4. What to do with the IP the CTO had written.

CEO took these actions in order:
- **Hour 1–24**: Reread the stock purchase agreement. Confirmed 100% of CTO's 50% equity was still subject to repurchase at cost because the cliff hadn't been reached.
- **Hour 24–36**: Offered the CTO a "consulting transition" of 8 weeks at $8K/month plus 0.5% in new options that would vest over 2 years if they delivered the handoff. CTO accepted.
- **Hour 36–48**: Had a lawyer paper the repurchase. Received back ~49.5% of the company (CTO kept 0.5%).
- **Hour 48–72**: Called the three largest angels individually. Framed: "CTO is leaving. I've already recovered the equity. Here's my plan for the next 6 months. I'll update you monthly." No one asked for their money back.

For the next 6 months the CEO did NOT hire a replacement co-founder. Instead:
- Hired a senior contractor (ex-staff engineer) at $12K/month to finish the MVP.
- Paid the transition consulting to the old CTO for 8 weeks.
- Put 40% of recovered equity into an option pool for future hires.
- Kept ~10% as founder buffer.

## Outcome
- Shipped MVP at month 14 (delayed by 4 months).
- Hired a new CTO at month 18 with 4% equity + standard vest.
- Raised seed at month 22 ($2M at $10M post).
- Old CTO's 0.5% was worth ~$50K on paper at seed, $0 realized. New CTO's 4% was worth $400K on paper.

## What I'd do differently
1. **Paper the vesting correctly on day 1.** The 4-year/1-year-cliff was standard, but the CEO had never actually read their own docs until the day of the breakup. Know your docs.
2. **Don't split 50/50 if one person isn't 50% of the work.** With hindsight, the CEO was always going to carry the company. 60/40 or 70/30 would have been fairer to both.
3. **Tell angels faster.** The CEO waited 48 hours. Should have called within 24. Angels appreciate being told early, even with an incomplete plan.
4. **The transition consulting was worth it.** It kept the IP clean, it kept the handoff cooperative, and it was < 2% of runway.

## What I'd NOT do differently
- **Not hiring a new cofounder immediately.** A rushed cofounder hire is worse than being solo. Took 10 months to find the right new CTO.
- **Putting 40% into an option pool.** Everyone told them this was "too much" at pre-seed. It ended up being the right call because they had real equity to offer the replacement.

## Generalizable lesson
Cofounder breakups are common. 65% of startups with cofounders see a founder leave within 4 years (Wasserman, Founder's Dilemmas). If your vesting docs are clean, a breakup before cliff is survivable and often clarifying. The damage is usually not the equity, it's the disclosure handling, the IP handoff, and the narrative to investors. Handle those first, in that order.

## Checklist if this happens to you
1. Reread your stock purchase agreement within 1 hour.
2. Determine vested vs unvested with a lawyer within 24 hours.
3. Offer a dignified transition (consulting + small equity kicker for handoff).
4. Paper the repurchase immediately.
5. Call your largest investors personally within 48 hours.
6. Communicate to the team with clarity, once, not in drips.
7. Do NOT rush-hire a replacement cofounder.
8. Put recovered equity into an option pool, not your personal pile.
