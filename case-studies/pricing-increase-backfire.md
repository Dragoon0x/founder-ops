# Case Study: Pricing Increase That Backfired

## Setup
- Stage: Series A, $6M ARR
- Team: 30 people
- Situation: Founders raised prices from $99/mo to $149/mo for new customers, and pushed a 50% increase on existing customers with 90 days notice. Their rationale: "We undercharge. Look at our NPS. Competitors charge $200+."

## What happened
Week 0: Announcement emails sent to 1,200 existing customers.
Week 1: Support tickets up 8x. NPS dropped from 52 to 18.
Week 2: Churn notifications started. 4% of base churned in week 2.
Week 4: Major customer (12% of ARR) said they'd leave at renewal.
Week 8: 14% of customers had churned. New customer close rate dropped from 22% to 11%.
Week 12: Founders reversed the increase for existing customers. Kept the new price for new customers.

Financial impact:
- Gross ARR loss from churn: $860K.
- New ARR from higher pricing: $230K.
- Net: $-630K + support costs + reputation damage.

## What went wrong
1. **90 days notice was too short** for a 50% increase. B2B customers with annual budgets need 6–12 months to absorb a change that size.
2. **The increase wasn't tied to new value.** They just raised the price. There was no "and we're adding X, Y, Z."
3. **They announced to everyone the same day.** Top 20 customers should have heard individually from the founder a week before the mass email.
4. **The comparison to competitors was founder-only knowledge.** Customers didn't know competitors charged more. They knew they were paying $99 and now were asked to pay $149.
5. **NPS was a misleading signal.** High NPS means customers like you, not that they'll accept a 50% increase.

## What they did in the recovery
1. Reversed the increase for existing customers (back to $99/mo, locked for 18 months).
2. Kept the $149/mo price for new customers.
3. Apologized publicly in a blog post. Not a corporate apology — a real one.
4. Rolled out a "premium tier" at $199/mo with 3 real new features, 6 months later. 8% of base opted in.
5. In the next 12 months, grandfathered prices rose 10%/year via the premium tier, not via blanket increases.

## What I'd do differently
1. **Raise prices on new customers only, always.** Existing customers get grandfathered or see increases tied to new features, 1 year minimum.
2. **Announce individually to top 20% of customers by ARR, a week before mass email.** Every time.
3. **Pair any price increase with a visible new feature** released within 30 days of the announcement.
4. **Give 6 months notice, not 90 days, for increases over 20%.**
5. **Test the price increase on new customers first for 3 months.** If close rate holds, extend to existing. If close rate drops, you have your answer.

## Generalizable lesson
Existing customers pay for the company you were, not the company you are. Raising prices on them is extracting rent, not creating value. The better path is: raise prices on new customers, add new tiers, and let existing customers migrate up voluntarily.

Pricing is a product. Test it like one. Don't ship a 50% change to 100% of the base with no iteration.

## The price-change checklist
Before any price change:
- [ ] Modeled revenue impact at 5%, 10%, 20% churn scenarios
- [ ] Drafted the customer notification 6 weeks in advance
- [ ] Segmented customers by how the change affects them
- [ ] Plan for individual founder outreach to top 20% by ARR
- [ ] Named feature(s) justifying the change, shipped within 30 days
- [ ] 6+ months notice for increases > 20%
- [ ] Grandfather policy documented for existing customers
- [ ] Sales team trained on talking points
- [ ] Support team staffed for 5x normal volume for first 2 weeks
- [ ] Rollback plan if NPS drops > 15 points
