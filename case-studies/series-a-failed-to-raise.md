# Case Study: Series A Failed to Raise

## Setup
- Stage: Post-seed, 22 months in
- Team: 14 people
- Revenue: $1.4M ARR, growing 9% MoM
- Runway: 8 months at raise-start, 4 months at process-end
- Situation: Founders kicked off a Series A with 19 target funds. After 11 weeks, they had zero term sheets and were down to 4 months of runway.

## What happened — the process
- Month 22: Hired a banker. Built a data room. Crafted a $8M raise at $40M pre.
- Week 1: First meetings. Got strong partner interest from 4 funds.
- Week 3: Three funds passed after partner meetings. Reasons cited: "too early," "concentration risk," "growth rate decelerating."
- Week 5: Expanded target list to 34 funds. Most new meetings were with associates, not partners.
- Week 7: Down to 2 active processes. Both funds slow-rolling.
- Week 9: Both remaining funds passed.
- Week 11: Decision point — extend seed or shut down.

## What they did
- Called their seed lead. Asked for a $1.5M bridge extension at flat valuation. Got yes in 4 days.
- Cut 3 roles (2 engineers, 1 marketing) to extend runway from 4 months to 12 months.
- Revised the plan: focus entirely on ARR growth for 9 months, then retry Series A.
- Publicly: "We decided to wait on Series A and grow with seed extension first." True-ish.

## What went wrong in the original process
1. **Timing.** They started the raise at $1.4M ARR, 9% MoM growth. Series A in their sector required $2M+ ARR and 15% MoM growth. They were one quarter too early.
2. **Banker fit.** The banker was good at later-stage rounds, not Series A. Cost them $75K in fees and 3 weeks of wrong positioning.
3. **Concentration risk was real.** 38% of ARR from top 1 customer. They tried to hide this in the data room. Investors found it in week 2 of diligence. Credibility hit.
4. **Growth rate was decelerating.** 14% MoM six months prior, 9% at raise. They positioned it as "stabilizing" — investors read it as "losing steam."
5. **Too narrow a target list initially.** 19 funds was a signal of overconfidence. 40+ is standard for Series A.

## What they did in the 9-month rebuild
- Hired 2 AEs (replacing the 2 engineers they cut). Revenue focus.
- Landed 4 mid-market customers. Reduced top-customer concentration from 38% to 19%.
- Grew ARR from $1.4M to $3.1M. MoM growth restabilized at 13%.
- Refreshed the pitch. Removed banker. Led process themselves.

## Outcome of the second attempt
- Month 34: Raised $12M Series A at $60M pre. 3 term sheets from 28 target funds.
- The fund they accepted was on the original list of 19 and had passed in week 3 of the first process.

## What I'd do differently
1. **Don't start a Series A without 12+ months of runway.** They started with 8, which created desperation pricing.
2. **Don't hire a banker at Series A unless the round is >$20M.** Below that, the banker is noise.
3. **Fix concentration risk BEFORE the raise, not during.** Buying down a single-customer-concentration requires 6 months of sales effort.
4. **Track MoM growth as a rolling 3-month average.** A single bad month in the middle of a raise is catastrophic.
5. **Talk to 5 fund partners before starting the process.** They'll tell you if you're ready.

## Generalizable lesson
Most Series A failures are not about pitch quality. They're about metrics quality and timing. The market is wiser than founders think. If the answer is "no" from 10+ funds, the pricing is wrong or the metrics aren't there. Keep building until the market's "yes" is easy to earn.

## The "ready for Series A" checklist (bar has risen 2024-2026)
- $2M+ ARR (B2B SaaS)
- 15%+ MoM growth for 6+ months
- 100%+ net dollar retention
- <24 month CAC payback
- No customer >20% of ARR
- 12+ months of runway at kickoff
- 3+ months of consistent enterprise logos
