# Case Study: Down Round Decision

## Setup
- Stage: Post-Series A, 28 months in
- Team: 42 people
- Revenue: $4.2M ARR, 2% MoM growth (previously 12%)
- Runway: 5 months
- Last round: $18M Series A at $90M post (30 months ago)
- Situation: Need $15M to reach Series B metrics. Best offer on the table is $12M at $60M post (33% down from the Series A post-money).

## The options on the table
1. **Take the down round.** $12M at $60M post. Anti-dilution triggers. Option pool refresh to 15%.
2. **Take a bridge from existing investors.** $4M at last round valuation. Extends runway 8 months but doesn't solve the problem.
3. **Shut down.** Return ~$3M of capital to investors (what's left).
4. **Sell the company.** 2 acquirers circling. Offers would be $25M–35M, mostly stock.
5. **Extend runway via 30% layoff.** Get to profitability in 14 months. No raise needed.

## What they chose
Option 5 + Option 2 as a hybrid.

- Laid off 14 of 42 people (33% cut). Severance: 2 months base + COBRA. Total cost: $520K.
- Took a $3M bridge from existing investors at last-round valuation. Uncapped SAFE.
- Restructured the remaining team around 3 priorities: retain enterprise customers, ship one differentiating feature, get to profitability in 12 months.
- Told the team publicly: "We are no longer raising. We are building a profitable company."

## Why not the down round
The founders modeled it out honestly:
- Down round + anti-dilution meant they personally would go from 14% combined to 6% combined.
- Option pool refresh would dilute them further as new hires received grants.
- Employee morale impact: they estimated 20% attrition in the 6 months post-raise.
- The signal to the market: "This company couldn't hold its last valuation." Would make the next round harder regardless of metrics.

## Why not the acquisition
The stock offers were from companies they didn't fully trust to execute. The $25M–35M headline was misleading — with liquidation preferences eating the first $21M (2x on Series A), common shareholders (including employees) would have received essentially nothing.

## Why not shut down
They had $4.2M ARR and a path to profitability. Shutting down was premature.

## Outcome
- Month 33: Layoff complete. Team of 28.
- Month 42: Profitability achieved at $5.8M ARR, 40% gross margin.
- Month 52: Raised $20M Series B at $140M post (flat-plus from last effective valuation).
- Founder ownership: 12% combined (modest dilution from bridge and Series B pool refresh).

## What I'd do differently
1. **Would have cut sooner.** The growth deceleration was visible at month 20. They waited 8 months to act because they hoped metrics would recover.
2. **Would not have hired to 42 in the first place.** They grew the team 3x in 18 months based on optimistic forecasts. The "right" team size for their revenue was 25.
3. **Would have modeled each scenario with actual dilution math before the meeting.** The down round looked survivable until they ran the math on anti-dilution and pool refresh.

## Generalizable lesson
A down round is rarely the best answer when profitability is achievable within 18 months. The short-term pain of a layoff is smaller than the long-term pain of a bad cap table. But this only works if:
- You have real revenue (not <$1M ARR).
- Your unit economics work at smaller scale.
- Existing investors are supportive enough to bridge.
- Your customer base is retentive enough to survive the optics hit.

If any of those are missing, take the down round or sell.

## The down round decision framework
Model these four scenarios honestly:
1. **Post-down-round cap table:** Your ownership after anti-dilution + pool refresh + next round pool refresh. Often worse than founders expect.
2. **Post-profitability cap table:** Your ownership if you bridge, cut, and reach profitability.
3. **Post-acquisition distribution:** Actual $ to common after liquidation preferences. Often $0.
4. **Shutdown:** Capital returned. Your personal opportunity cost. Brand damage to future raises.

The winner is usually #2, but only if profitability is within 18 months.
