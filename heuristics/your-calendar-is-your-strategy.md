# Heuristic: Your Calendar Is Your Strategy

## The rule
Strategy is not what you say. It's what you spend time on. Look at
your calendar. That's your real strategy.

## Why it works

Founders talk about priorities ("our priority is customer success")
and then spend 70% of time on fundraising, 20% on press, and 10%
actually talking to customers. The gap between stated and revealed
priorities is where companies die.

A calendar audit reveals:
- What you actually invest in
- What you say you care about but don't
- Which commitments are vestigial
- Where you should be but aren't

## When the rule applies
- Monthly. Every month. For yourself and your exec team.
- Before any strategic offsite.
- When you're confused about why something isn't working.

## How to audit

Step 1: Pull last month's calendar.
Step 2: Categorize every meeting into buckets:
- Product / customer
- Revenue / sales
- Team / hiring
- Fundraising / investors
- Operations / finance
- External (press, partnerships, events)
- Internal comms
- Personal
- Garbage

Step 3: Add up hours per category.

Step 4: Compare to stated priorities.

Step 5: Notice the gaps.

## What you'll probably find

1. **"I prioritize customers" meets 10% customer time.** The gap is
   often 5x.
2. **"I need to hire" meets 5 hours of recruiting.** Not enough.
3. **"I'm focused on product" meets 0 hours of product reviews.**
4. **15 hours of recurring meetings that no one questions.** The
   Wednesday all-hands that no one prepares for. The Friday all-hands
   that replicates the Wednesday one. The internal weekly that was
   important 18 months ago.

## The fix

1. Delete recurring meetings you haven't prepared for in 3 months.
   (Or admit you don't need to be there.)
2. Block time for priorities. Actual calendar blocks, not
   aspirations.
3. Protect the top 3 activities by moving them earlier (when energy
   is higher).
4. Review monthly. The calendar drifts toward whatever is loudest,
   not what matters.

## When the rule breaks

- Short-term: fundraising, crises, launches. Calendar gets dominated
  temporarily. That's fine.
- Early stage: founder is doing everything, so calendar is fragmented.
  Still: look for patterns weekly.

## The exec team version

Run this with your exec team quarterly. Have each exec show their
top 5 calendar categories for the last month. Discuss gaps with
stated priorities.

You'll often find:
- Head of Sales spends 40% in internal meetings, not with reps or deals.
- Head of Product spends 50% in syncs, 10% in customer calls.
- CEO spends 30% on investor management at stages that don't require
  it.

Reallocation follows.

## The calendar hack

Color-code your calendar:
- Green: strategic, growth, customer
- Blue: internal alignment, management
- Red: reactive, firefighting, admin
- Gray: meetings that shouldn't exist

Every week, the green should be > 50%. If it's not, adjust.
