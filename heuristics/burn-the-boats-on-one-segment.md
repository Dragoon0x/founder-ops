# Heuristic: Burn the Boats on One Segment

## The rule
Pre-PMF, pick one customer segment and go all-in. Explicitly de-
prioritize others. Don't build features, write content, or attend
events for anyone else.

## Why it works

Early-stage founders want to serve "anyone who will pay us." This
creates:
- A product that's mediocre for everyone.
- Messaging that's generic.
- A roadmap pulled in 4 directions.
- No one telling friends about you.

Picking one segment means:
- Your product is excellent for them.
- Your messaging is sharp.
- Your roadmap is clear.
- Referrals compound in that segment.

## The specific test

Pick one segment so specific that:
1. You can name the exact job title of your buyer.
2. You can name 20 real companies that fit.
3. You know the budget cycle they operate on.
4. You know the 3 competitors they've considered.
5. You know their top 3 pains in rank order.

If you can't answer all 5, you're not narrow enough.

## When the rule applies

- Pre-PMF (< $1M ARR).
- During pivots.
- When launching new products from an existing company.
- Post-pivot.

## When the rule breaks

- Post-PMF with proven expansion motion.
- Marketplace businesses (need two sides).
- Platform plays (need multiple integrations).
- When the "one segment" is too small to support the business
  long-term. Then it's a beachhead, and the expansion plan matters.

## How to pick the segment

Look for:
- **Existing pull**: who's actually buying your MVP today?
- **Quantifiable pain**: they can measure what the problem costs them.
- **Budget**: they have discretionary budget, not just "would be nice."
- **Speed**: they can make decisions in weeks, not quarters.
- **Reachable**: you can find 50 of them without an ad budget.

The worst segments:
- "Small businesses" (too diverse).
- "Startups" (no budget, lots of churn).
- "Enterprises" (unless you have enterprise sales experience).

## Burning the boats in practice

- Rewrite landing page for this one segment. Specifics.
- Remove features/messaging aimed at other segments.
- Decline unqualified leads that don't fit.
- Write content only for this segment.
- Attend only their events.
- Interview only them.

Yes, it feels narrow. That's the point.

## The expansion moment

You know you've nailed one segment when:
- Inbound starts from the segment you targeted, consistently.
- NPS > 50 in the segment.
- Expansion revenue within the segment is > 20% of new.
- Referrals from the segment are > 30% of new pipeline.
- You can close 30% of qualified demos in the segment.

Now, and only now, consider the adjacent segment.

## The one-segment-per-12-months rule

Don't try to expand to 3 segments in year one. Pick one. Work it
for 9–12 months. Move to the second only when the first is humming.

## The failure mode

"Our product is for developers, PMs, and designers" — no. It's
for one of them. If you can't pick, your product isn't ready.

## Specific examples

**Too broad**: "marketing teams at startups"
**Burning boats**: "demand generation leads at Series A-C B2B SaaS
companies in the 50–200 person range based in the US"

**Too broad**: "engineering teams"
**Burning boats**: "infrastructure engineers at data-heavy consumer
apps with DAU 1M–50M"

The narrower version writes better content, ships better features,
and grows faster.
