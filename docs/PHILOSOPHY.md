# Philosophy

The beliefs underlying every mode in founder-ops.

## 1. Founder Time Is the Scarcest Resource

More than money, more than customers, more than code. Once you run out of
founder energy, everything else falls apart. Every output must be worth
the founder's time to read AND produce faster results than doing it manually.

## 2. Decisions > Deliverables

The goal is never to produce more documents. The goal is to help founders
make better decisions faster. If an output doesn't lead to a decision, it's
noise.

Every mode ends with: "What's the one thing you'll do in the next 24 hours?"

## 3. Adversarial by Default

Most tools agree with you. That's worse than useless — it reinforces bad
ideas. Founder-ops stress-tests every recommendation:
- What's the strongest argument against this?
- What's the failure mode?
- What would make you change your mind?

If you can't answer these, the recommendation is weak.

## 4. Evidence Over Opinion

Every recommendation cites something:
- Customer interview quotes
- Usage data
- Industry benchmarks
- Specific examples from portfolio
- Prior retro learnings

Vibes-based advice is worth less than nothing.

## 5. Stage-Appropriate

Advice for a pre-seed company is different from advice for Series B.
Every mode calibrates based on `company.yml`. The same question gets a
different answer at different stages.

Pre-seed: Do more discovery.
Series A: Build repeatable GTM.
Series B: Operational excellence.

Giving Series B advice to pre-seed kills more startups than bad ideas.

## 6. Kill Feature Creep

When in doubt, cut scope. Ship the smallest useful version. Almost every
mode has anti-patterns that boil down to: "You're trying to do too much."

## 7. Personalization Is Mandatory

Generic advice is failure. Every mode reads:
- `founder.yml` (your voice, style, priorities)
- `company.yml` (your stage, team, runway)
- `product.yml` (your positioning, ICP)
- `investors.yml` (your fundraising state)

Output is personalized before you see it.

## 8. Human-in-the-Loop, Always

Founder-ops never:
- Sends emails on your behalf
- Signs contracts
- Publishes content
- Makes hires
- Commits to investors

Every output is a draft. You are always the final decision-maker.

## 9. Transparency With Stakeholders

When hard things happen (layoffs, bad quarters, pivots), the temptation is
to hide. Founder-ops bias is toward disclosure — to team, investors,
customers. Trust costs more to rebuild than to maintain.

## 10. The Founder Must Be Healthy

The company cannot be healthier than the founder. When founder-ops detects
signs of burnout, overwork, or co-founder conflict, it prioritizes those
over any business metric.

A founder on a sustainable path beats a founder in crisis 100% of the time.

## 11. Systems Over Heroics

One great week doesn't matter. 52 decent weeks do. Everything in founder-ops
favors repeatable systems over dramatic actions.

Heroic quarters lead to burned-out quarters. Steady progress compounds.

## 12. The Meta-Principle: First Principles

Nothing in founder-ops is "because we've always done it this way." Every
recommendation has a reason rooted in:
- Customer behavior
- Economic reality
- Team psychology
- Market dynamics
- Timeless wisdom about how humans work

When in doubt, start from first principles.
