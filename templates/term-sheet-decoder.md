# Term Sheet Decoder

> **DISCLAIMER:** This is educational. Always have a lawyer review actual term sheets.

## Reading Order (top to bottom)

### 1. Round Size & Valuation
- **Pre-money valuation:** Company value BEFORE the round
- **Post-money valuation:** Pre-money + round size = post-money
- **Investment amount:** Total being raised this round
- **Lead investor:** Sets terms; usually largest check

**Math check:**
```
Pre-money: $20M
Investment: $5M
Post-money: $25M
New investor ownership: $5M / $25M = 20%
Existing dilution: ~20% (depending on option pool)
```

### 2. Security Type
- **Preferred stock:** What investors get; comes with rights ICs don't have
- **Series A vs B vs Seed:** Each has different rights (typically less for earlier)
- **SAFE / Convertible Note:** Pre-priced; converts in next priced round (less common at A)

### 3. Liquidation Preference
**Standard at seed/A:** 1x non-participating preferred

What it means: If company sells, investors get their money back FIRST, then the rest is split among everyone.

**Watch out for:**
- 2x or 3x preference (investors get 2-3x money back first) → BAD
- Participating preferred (they get money back AND share in remainder) → BAD
- Stacked preferences (multiple rounds layered) → COMPLEX

### 4. Anti-Dilution Protection
**Standard:** Broad-based weighted average

What it means: If next round is at lower valuation, this round gets adjusted for some protection.

**Watch out for:**
- Full ratchet (gives investor full re-pricing) → VERY BAD for founders
- Narrow-based weighted average → Slightly worse for founders

### 5. Pro-rata Rights
**Standard:** Yes — investor can maintain ownership in future rounds

This is reasonable. Don't fight it unless they want MORE than pro-rata (super pro-rata) → that's worth pushing back.

### 6. Board Composition
**At seed:** Often founder-only board, OR 1 founder + 1 investor + (sometimes) 1 independent

**At Series A:** 1-2 investor seats, founder(s), 1 independent

**Watch out for:**
- Investors getting majority before company is profitable → bad
- "Required board observer" rights from EVERY investor → meeting becomes huge

### 7. Protective Provisions / Veto Rights
What investors can veto. Standard list:
- Selling the company
- Issuing more stock
- Changing the certificate
- Increasing the option pool
- Taking on debt above $X

**Watch out for:**
- Vetoes on operational decisions (hiring, salary, budget) → BAD
- Required investor approval for next financing → CONSTRAINING

### 8. Founder Vesting
**Standard:** 4 years, 1-year cliff (sometimes existing equity is part-credited)

**Watch out for:**
- Full re-vesting from scratch (you've worked 2 years, now restart) → BAD
- No vesting acceleration on termination without cause → can be modified

### 9. Vesting Acceleration
**Standard at Series A:**
- **Single trigger:** Acquired → automatic vesting (rare, founder-friendly)
- **Double trigger:** Acquired AND fired → automatic vesting (more common, fair)
- **No acceleration:** Acquirer can dump you and keep your equity (BAD)

### 10. Option Pool
Investors will require pool expansion before the round (so it dilutes you, not them).

**Standard:** 10-15% post-money option pool at seed; refresh to similar at A.

**Watch out for:**
- 20%+ pool requirement (dilutes you significantly)
- Pool that goes UP if you don't hit hiring milestones

### 11. Drag-Along & Co-Sale
**Drag-along:** If majority sells, minority MUST sell (standard, fine)
**Co-sale (right of first offer / refusal):** Investors can buy your shares if you sell (standard, fine)

### 12. Information Rights
**Standard:** Monthly financials, annual audit, board materials

Usually not negotiable. Make sure you can deliver what you promise.

### 13. Closing Conditions
Things that must be true before money wires:
- Legal docs signed
- IP assignment from all founders
- Cap table cleaned up
- 409A valuation completed
- Sometimes: customer references, due diligence completed

## Negotiation Priorities

If you can only negotiate 3 things, prioritize:

1. **Valuation** (within reason — fighting too hard hurts the relationship)
2. **Liquidation preference** (anything other than 1x non-participating is a fight)
3. **Board composition** (don't lose control before Series B if possible)

Other things matter less than founders think:
- Pro-rata (let investors have it)
- Protective provisions (mostly standard)
- Information rights (you should be transparent anyway)

## Red Flags

- Multiple liquidation preferences
- Full ratchet anti-dilution
- Founder vesting reset
- Investor veto on operational decisions
- Requirement to use specific lawyers / accountants
- Excessive option pool requirements (>15%)
- "Pay-to-play" provisions (force founders to fund future rounds)

If you see these, push back hard or walk away.

## Tools

- **Captable.io / Carta:** Cap table modeling
- **NVCA Model Documents:** Standard term sheet language
- **Founder-friendly law firms:** WSGR, Cooley, Latham, Fenwick, Goodwin, Orrick

## After You Sign

Term sheet → Lawyers draft definitive docs (4-6 weeks) → Sign → Close (money wires).

During those 4-6 weeks:
- Don't talk to other investors (exclusivity is implicit)
- Don't make major changes to the company
- Respond to due diligence requests promptly
- Have your lawyer push back on changes to negotiated terms
