# Reference Check Template

Use for the final reference call on a candidate. Candidate provides referees; you find one additional reference on your own (backchannel) for senior roles.

---

## Context for the call

- Candidate: [Name, role applying for]
- Reference: [Name, current role, past relationship to candidate]
- Call length: 25-35 minutes
- Format: video or phone

Open by thanking them; remind them the conversation is confidential; share that you are trying to make the best decision for both parties, including the candidate.

---

## Opening (2 min)

"Thanks so much for the time. I'm hiring [name] for [role] at [company]. I want to make sure this is the right fit for them and for us, and candid feedback matters more than anything else here.

Confidentially: nothing you say goes back to [candidate]. Want to confirm that context before we start?"

---

## Relationship context (3 min)

- How did you work with [candidate]?
- For how long?
- How closely — direct manager, peer, report, adjacent?

*You want to calibrate: how much weight to put on this reference.*

---

## Work product and skills (10 min)

- What were [candidate]'s 2-3 greatest strengths on your team?
- What did they do that others couldn't?
- Give me an example of [candidate] solving a really hard problem.
- What kind of work environment do they thrive in?
- Where did [candidate] need support or development?
- What would they need from their next manager to do their best work?

*Listen for specificity. Generic answers ("great person, super smart") = weaker reference.*

---

## Behavior and collaboration (10 min)

- How did [candidate] handle disagreement?
- Describe a time [candidate] received tough feedback. How did they respond?
- Who on the team gave them the most productive push-back? What was the relationship like?
- How did they support peers vs. self-promote?
- When things went wrong, how did they show up?

---

## The ranking question (3 min)

This is the single most predictive question:

> "Of all the people you've worked with in a [role] in your career, where does [candidate] rank? Top 5%, top 10%, top 25%, better-than-average, average, below average?"

*Silence after. Let them think. Listen for hesitation.*

- If top 5-10%, ask: "Why?"
- If lower, ask: "What would have moved them up?"

---

## Wrap (5 min)

Two closing questions:

> "Would you hire [candidate] again?"

[Listen carefully; "in what role?" is revealing]

> "Is there anything you think I should know that I didn't ask?"

[Most valuable question in the whole call.]

---

## Red flags to listen for

- Vague praise without examples
- Hesitation on basic questions
- "I'm the wrong person to ask about that"
- Long pause before the ranking question
- Descriptions of conflict that sound like patterns
- Reluctance to confirm they would rehire

---

## After the call

- Write notes within 2 hours; memory fades fast
- Cross-reference with other references' answers
- Share structured summary with hiring panel
- If backchannel call, note that it's backchannel

---

## Decision framework

After 2-3 references:

- **All strong and consistent:** proceed with offer
- **Mixed:** one more reference; weight the pattern
- **Concerning pattern:** do not proceed, or have the panel debrief candidate on the concern in a structured way

---

## Template notes for the reviewer

- If a candidate asks their references to "prep," treat that as a yellow flag. The best references speak naturally without prep.
- Two references minimum for ICs; 3 for leaders; 4+ for exec.
- Always do at least one backchannel for senior hires — someone the candidate didn't list. LinkedIn + cold outreach works.

---

## Common mistake

Accepting a reference call as "yes this person is great." A good reference check is an opportunity to understand how to set them up for success, not to validate the decision.
