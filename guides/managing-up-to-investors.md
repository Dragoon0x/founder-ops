# Managing up to investors

Investors are not your boss. They're shareholders. But the relationship has
boss-like asymmetries (they have capital you need; they sit on boards; they
have networks you can't easily replicate). Managing them is a skill founders
underrate.

## What investors actually want

In rough order:
1. To not be surprised
2. To see the company succeed at the level they invested for (10x is the
   modal target for VC)
3. To be useful (most are; a few aren't)
4. Reputation among other founders (your referrals matter to them)
5. Reputation among LPs (their LPs matter to them)

The "not surprised" is #1. Most relationship damage happens when investors
learn bad news from someone else, late, or all at once.

## The monthly investor update

Send one. Every month. Even when it sucks. Especially when it sucks.

Format (1 page max, 5 sections, ~500 words):

1. **Headline** — 1 sentence on the state of the business
2. **The numbers** — MRR, growth %, runway, burn, key cohort metric (use the
   same numbers every month so they can compare)
3. **What's working** — 1-3 bullets on real progress
4. **What's not** — 1-3 bullets on real problems (this is what builds trust)
5. **What I need** — 1-3 specific asks (intros, hiring, advice)

Send by the 5th of each month. Set a calendar recurrence. Your existing
investors should hear from you more often than you'd think to send.

## What to share, what not to share

Share:
- All material business changes (positive or negative)
- Hiring plans before you make senior hires
- Strategy pivots (not after; before)
- Significant customer wins or losses
- Team departures (especially co-founders, key execs)
- Cap table changes

Don't share:
- Day-to-day operational drama
- Team interpersonal issues (unless it affects strategy)
- Financial models that haven't survived an internal pressure test
- Confidential customer info that violates NDAs

## Asking for help (the right way)

Specific > vague.

Bad: "Any intros to enterprise sales people would be great."

Good: "We're looking for a Director of Sales with 5-8 years of experience
selling devtools to engineering teams at companies of 200-2000 people, ideally
with a network at companies in [list]. Currently in conversation with [list],
need 5 more candidates in the funnel by end of month. Anyone you'd suggest?"

The good version makes it easy to help. The bad version creates work.

## How often to talk

| Stage | Cadence with lead investor |
|---|---|
| Just funded | Monthly written update + monthly call |
| 6 mo in | Monthly update + quarterly call |
| 12+ mo in | Monthly update + quarterly call + ad-hoc |
| Pre-next-round | Weekly informal check-ins |

If your investor wants more contact than this, push back. Their job is to add
value, not to manage you. If they want less, send the update anyway. They
read it.

## Disagreement and pushback

You will disagree. Often. About hiring, pivots, fundraising timing, pricing.
The investor's job is to share their view; your job is to make the call.

How to handle disagreement well:
1. Steelman their view in your own words before responding
2. Share what evidence would change your mind
3. Make the call. Don't outsource it. The investor doesn't run the company.
4. Document the decision and reasoning. If you turn out to be wrong, you both
   know why.
5. Don't ghost them after. Disagreement is fine; avoidance is corrosive.

## The hard conversations

You will have to deliver hard news: missed quarter, key person leaving,
fundraise stalling, fraud, layoffs, pivot. Rules:

- Tell them before you tell anyone else (board, team, customers)
- Tell them in person or by phone, not over Slack
- Bring the analysis, not just the news (what happened, why, what you'll do)
- Have a recommendation. Don't ask them what to do; tell them what you're
  going to do
- Be brief. They'll have follow-up questions. Answer those well.

## What to do when investors are wrong

They will be. Often. Their pattern-matching is based on other companies, not
yours.

What to do:
- Listen to the underlying concern, not the prescription
- Run your own analysis
- Make your decision
- Communicate the reasoning, not the decision-after-the-fact

What not to do:
- Take it personally
- Litigate the disagreement publicly
- Drop them from the conversation (they'll find out anyway)
- Defer to them out of insecurity

## When the relationship is broken

Some investor relationships sour. Symptoms: combative board meetings, public
trash talk, blocking on routine matters. Options:
1. Direct conversation about what's wrong (try first)
2. Escalate to the firm's senior partner (if your contact is junior)
3. Buy them out at the next round (rare but possible)
4. Wait for the firm to rotate the partner (sometimes)

A broken investor relationship is rarely fatal but always expensive. Address
early.
