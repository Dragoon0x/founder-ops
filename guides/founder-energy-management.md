# Founder energy management

You can manage time well and still burn out. Time is a constraint; energy is
the asset. The founders who last manage energy explicitly.

## The 4 axes of energy

Tony Schwartz framework (still useful):
1. **Physical** — sleep, nutrition, movement
2. **Emotional** — relationships, joy, processing of stress
3. **Mental** — focus, creative space, learning
4. **Spiritual** — meaning, alignment, sense of purpose

Founders default to optimizing time at the expense of all 4. The tradeoff
catches up. Usually after about 18-24 months of unsustainable pace.

## What burnout actually looks like

It doesn't look like the cinematic version (collapsed at desk). It looks
like:

- You used to enjoy customer calls; now they feel like obligations
- You used to ship by Friday; now you push to Monday and don't notice
- You used to read for fun; now you can't focus on a book
- Small annoyances feel like crises
- You're cynical about things you used to be enthusiastic about
- You're avoiding people who used to energize you

If 3+ of these are true, you're closer to burnout than you think. Address now.

## Daily floor

Non-negotiable minimums (set yours):
- Sleep: 7+ hours, on a schedule (no all-nighters; cost > benefit always)
- Movement: 30+ min daily, anything counts
- Eating: not skipping meals; not living on coffee
- Sunlight: 15+ min outside before noon

These sound like wellness clichés. They are. They also work. Founders who
violate the floor for >2 weeks straight pay for it for the next 2 months.

## Weekly architecture

Most founders have a calendar that looks like everything is the same
priority. Better: design the week with intentional rhythm.

Sample (adjust to your style):
- Monday: planning, internal meetings, light external
- Tuesday-Wednesday: deep work, customer calls, building
- Thursday: external (sales, partnerships, recruiting)
- Friday: review, write-up, lighter day, longer lunch
- Weekend: actually off (this is the controversial one — see below)

The pattern matters less than having one. Random calendars produce random
output.

## The weekend question

The myth: "real founders work 7 days a week."
The reality: founders who work 7 days for 6+ months produce worse work in
month 6 than founders who took weekends.

What works:
- One full day off per week, no email, no Slack, no thinking-about-work
- One weekend day for "easy" work (reading, planning, low-pressure)
- One weekend evening for friends/family/relationships

If you can't take a day off, you have a delegation problem, not a workload
problem. Solve the delegation problem.

## Energy debits and credits

Make a personal list:
- 5 things that drain you (debits)
- 5 things that recharge you (credits)

Both are personal. For some founders, customer calls are credits; for others
they're debits. Same activity, different person.

Audit your week against the list. If you're heavy on debits and light on
credits for >2 weeks, that's the burnout slope.

## The vacation rule

Take 2-3 weeks of disconnected vacation per year. Disconnected = no email,
no Slack, your team has explicit decision authority while you're gone.

Founders who don't take vacation:
- Become single points of failure
- Don't see clearly because they don't get distance
- Burn out and take 6-12 month "sabbaticals" that are actually breakdowns

The 2-week vacation is cheap insurance. The team gets stronger from running
without you. You come back able to see the company more clearly.

## The therapist question

Almost every founder benefits from one. Founders who say "I don't need a
therapist" are most often the founders who need one.

Find one who works with founders specifically (the dynamics are unusual).
Once a week minimum at first; biweekly once stable.

This is not a failure mode. It's basic equipment maintenance for the role.

## The exit ramp

Sometimes the right answer isn't "manage energy better." It's "this isn't
the right thing for you anymore." Signs:
- Multi-year sustained dread of going to work
- The work no longer aligns with your values
- The opportunity cost (other things you'd be doing) feels enormous
- You've stopped growing

It's okay to leave. The startup will outlast your individual tenure or it
won't. You can't will yourself to enjoy a thing that's wrong for you. Most
founders who white-knuckle through this regret it later.

## The compound habit

Nothing in this guide works once. All of it works as practice. Founders who
treat energy management as a quarterly emergency response burn out. Founders
who treat it as a daily practice have 10-year careers.
