# What to do in the dip

Every startup has a dip. Some have several. The dip is the period where
nothing is working, the metrics aren't moving, and the path forward isn't
obvious. It feels permanent. It usually isn't.

## What the dip looks like

- 3-6+ months of flat or declining metrics
- Customers churning faster than you're acquiring
- Team morale dropping
- Founders losing conviction
- Investors going quiet
- The press / community moving on to other companies

You're tempted to make big moves: raise more money, pivot, fire the team,
quit. Most big moves in the dip are wrong moves.

## What the dip is not

It's not the end. Most companies that survive go through 1-3 dips. The ones
that don't survive are usually the ones that made panic moves in the dip.

It's also not "just keep going" — that's the wrong advice if the underlying
business is broken. The dip is a signal to look hard, not to push harder
without thinking.

## The dip diagnostic

Run this before deciding what to do:

1. **Is the market real?** Is the underlying problem still unsolved? Is anyone
   actually paying for solutions in this category? If no, the answer might
   be "wrong market." If yes, continue.

2. **Are we losing to competitors or to no-decision?** If competitors,
   you're playing the wrong differentiation. If no-decision, the urgency
   isn't real or you're not articulating it.

3. **Is retention failing or is acquisition failing?** Different problems,
   different fixes. Retention failing = product issue. Acquisition failing
   = positioning / channel issue.

4. **Are we executing or are we coasting?** Honestly. Are people working
   hard on the right things? Or has motivation drained?

5. **What did we believe 6 months ago that we now know is wrong?** The
   things you've learned are the basis for what to try next.

## What to do in the dip

### Cut the burn

Reduce burn to extend runway. Not because you're failing, but because you
need time to figure out what to do. Specifically:
- Cancel SaaS subscriptions
- Pause hiring
- Negotiate vendor discounts
- Defer non-critical projects

Going from 18 months runway to 24 months runway changes what you can try.

### Get back in the field

The CEO going on 30 customer calls in 30 days is a cliché because it works.
You'll learn things you can't learn from the dashboard. You'll re-anchor on
why you started.

### Run the smallest experiment that can prove something

Big bets in the dip are dangerous. Small bets compound learning.

Examples:
- New positioning on the homepage (1 week to ship, retention/conversion data
  in 30 days)
- New onboarding email sequence (3 days to ship, conversion data in 14 days)
- New target persona (1 week to update copy, signal in 30 days)

The cycle: hypothesize, test, learn, iterate. Repeat until something moves.

### Re-engage the team

In a dip, the team feels it. Silence makes it worse. The CEO needs to:
- Acknowledge the state honestly (don't fake optimism; people see through it)
- Articulate what you're trying (give them a sense of direction)
- Show progress weekly (even small wins matter)
- Spend more 1:1 time, not less (energy recharges)

### Talk to peers

Other founders in the dip will normalize the experience. Group chats with 4-6
peer CEOs at the same stage. Y Combinator forums. Reforge / First Round
communities. The isolation makes the dip worse.

## What not to do in the dip

1. **Major fundraise** — your terms will be terrible. Bridge if you must;
   wait if you can.
2. **Big pivot without diagnosis** — pivots from panic almost always fail
3. **Hire more sales people** — won't fix a leaky funnel
4. **Re-org the team** — people get fired in re-orgs and morale tanks more
5. **Layoff to look responsible to investors** — only do this if the math
   actually requires it
6. **Quit because it's hard** — the dip's job is to make you want to quit;
   quit when the math says quit, not when emotions say quit

## The math of when to quit

Quit when:
- You've run out of hypotheses to test
- The market has moved decisively away from your wedge
- You have <6 months runway and no path to bridge or revenue
- The cost-of-staying (in your one wild and precious life) exceeds the
  expected value of the outcome
- Your relationships, health, or values can't survive another 12 months

Don't quit when:
- It's hard
- One investor said no
- A competitor raised a big round
- A customer churned
- You're tired this week
- You haven't tried 5 more things you could try

## After the dip

Most founders who get through a dip describe it as the period when they
became the founder they needed to be. The discipline forced by limited
resources and limited time produces clearer thinking than abundance ever
does.

Save your dip notes. You'll write a Twitter thread about it someday.
