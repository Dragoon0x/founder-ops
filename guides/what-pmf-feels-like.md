# What product-market fit actually feels like

PMF is over-discussed and under-defined. Marc Andreessen's classic line is
"you can always feel product-market fit when it's happening." That's true and
useless. Here's a more concrete description.

## The objective signals

You're probably at PMF when:

1. **Retention plateaus high** — your retention curve doesn't keep going down.
   The plateau is high enough to sustain a business (>30% M3 for B2B SaaS,
   >25% for paid consumer, etc.)

2. **Inbound starts** — people you don't know reach out. Not many, but a
   trickle. If you have to hunt for every customer, you're not there.

3. **Word-of-mouth contributes** — when you ask new customers "how did you
   find us," some say "a friend told me," not "Google ad."

4. **NPS or "very disappointed" survey trends up** — Sean Ellis test: ask
   your users "how would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"
   If 40%+ say "very disappointed," you have PMF.

5. **Sales cycle compresses** — what used to take 60 days takes 30. People
   need less convincing.

6. **Discount requests drop** — when people want it, they don't haggle as
   hard.

7. **You can describe ICP in one sentence** — and it's narrow.

## The subjective signals

These matter just as much:

1. **You feel pulled, not pushed** — customers ask for the next thing
   instead of you proposing it.

2. **You stop selling and start receiving** — most of your customer
   conversations become "how do I do X" instead of "why should I buy."

3. **The team is energized by progress, not exhausted by lack of it** —
   when the team is on the gas naturally, that's a sign.

4. **You can answer "what do customers love" specifically** — not vaguely.

## What PMF is NOT

1. Revenue. You can have revenue without PMF (paid acquisition into a leaky
   bucket; founder selling like hell to friends).

2. Growth rate. You can grow fast without PMF (paid spend) or have PMF and
   grow slowly (long sales cycles, niche market).

3. Press attention. Press is upstream of PMF, not evidence of it.

4. Investor enthusiasm. Investors fund pre-PMF often.

5. Awards or "best of" lists.

## False positives

Be suspicious of these:
- Your friends and network using it heavily (sample bias)
- Strong free-tier engagement that doesn't convert (engagement isn't value)
- Strong demo response that doesn't convert (demo love isn't fit)
- A few customers who LOVE you but are atypical (one-off, not repeatable)
- Inbound from one viral moment (not sustained)

## False negatives

Be suspicious of these too:
- Slow ramp ("we don't have PMF") when you have a 9-month enterprise sales
  cycle (you might have PMF; the motion is just slow)
- Low retention metric ("we don't have PMF") when you're tracking the wrong
  metric (re-define active before concluding)
- Founder anxiety ("we don't have PMF") when actual signals are good
  (impostor syndrome is real)

## Stages of PMF

PMF isn't binary. It's a spectrum:

| Stage | Description |
|---|---|
| Pre-PMF | Building, experimenting, no clear pull |
| Hint of PMF | One ICP is showing real signal; uncertain if repeatable |
| PMF in a wedge | Clear PMF in a narrow segment; expansion uncertain |
| PMF with expansion | PMF in wedge + clear expansion path with early evidence |
| Category leader | PMF + dominant in category |

Most "we have PMF" claims are at stage 2-3.

## When you don't have it

Don't pretend you do. Pretending costs you the chance to find it.

Things to do:
- More customer interviews (you're never doing enough)
- Cut the wedge narrower until you find the sub-segment that does pull
- Be honest with the team about the state of evidence
- Be honest with investors

The companies that find PMF eventually are the ones that keep iterating with
clear eyes. The ones that don't usually believed they had it before they did.

## When you do have it

The job changes. From "find the fit" to "scale the fit." Different muscles.
A common failure mode: founders who got to PMF by being excellent
discoverers fail at being excellent operators. The op-ex skill is
distribution, hiring, system-building. If you don't have it, hire it. Don't
white-knuckle it.
