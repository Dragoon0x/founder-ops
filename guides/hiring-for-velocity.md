# Hiring for velocity vs hiring for stability

Most hiring advice is for stable companies. Startups need velocity. The two
require different hiring filters and you have to be honest about which you're
hiring for.

## What "velocity hiring" looks like

- Bias toward generalists who can take on adjacent problems
- Bias toward people who've worked at fewer, smaller companies (less
  procedural baggage)
- Bias toward action over deliberation in interviews
- Smaller, scrappier teams (fewer specialists, more multi-skill people)
- Optimized for shipping in 90 days, not for being there in 5 years

## What "stability hiring" looks like

- Bias toward specialists with deep expertise in a narrow area
- Bias toward people from larger, more mature companies
- Bias toward judgment and process design
- Larger teams with role clarity
- Optimized for compounding output over years

## The mistake

Series B and beyond, founders who learned to hire for velocity in the early
days keep hiring that way. They end up with 100 generalists, no specialists,
and no scalable systems. Same in reverse: founders who hire stability-types
too early end up with a team that can't move fast.

## A rough rule

| Stage | Default mode |
|---|---|
| <10 people | Velocity always |
| 10-25 | Velocity, with 1-2 stability hires for critical infra |
| 25-50 | Mixed; 60% velocity, 40% stability |
| 50-100 | 50/50 |
| 100+ | Stability dominant; velocity hires for new initiatives |

## Filters for velocity hires

- "Tell me about the last time you took on something not in your job description"
- "What's a time you had to ship something with incomplete information?"
- "How do you decide when to stop optimizing?"
- "What's your current ratio of doing vs. planning?"

## Filters for stability hires

- "Tell me about a process you designed that outlived you"
- "How do you bring quality up when the bar is uneven?"
- "What's a system you built for someone else to operate?"
- "How do you handle the politics of cross-team work?"

## The compensation question

Velocity hires often take comp risk for upside (more equity, less cash).
Stability hires usually want closer-to-market cash and standard equity.
Trying to make a stability hire take a velocity comp package is one of the
most common hiring failures.

## The interview loop should match

Velocity hires: 3-4 interviews max, decision in 5 days. Slow loops kill the
candidate's enthusiasm and select for risk-averse people.

Stability hires: 5-7 interviews ok, decision in 10-14 days. Slower loops
let you check more dimensions; risk-averse candidates are fine here.

## The honest test

For your next hire, write down: "I'm hiring this person to..." Finish the
sentence in 1 line. If the line is "...solve X problem in 90 days," it's
velocity. If it's "...build the function for the next 3 years," it's
stability. Most founders write a vague line that's neither, and the hire
suffers because the role wasn't clear.
