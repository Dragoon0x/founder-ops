# Pre-seed to seed: what changes

Pre-seed is "we have an idea." Seed is "we have evidence." The difference is
not money. The difference is the burden of proof.

## What pre-seed investors are buying

- Founder quality (track record, domain credibility, raw intelligence)
- A non-trivial insight about a market
- A theory of the wedge
- A general direction, not a plan

You can raise pre-seed on a pitch deck and three reference calls.

## What seed investors are buying

- Evidence the wedge is real (early users, retention, qualitative signal)
- A theory of the path to Series A
- A team that can execute
- Unit economics that *could* work at scale (don't have to today)

You raise seed with the pre-seed pitch plus a data appendix.

## The shift in question

Pre-seed: "Could this become a big business?"
Seed: "Will this become a big business, given what we've learned?"

This is why most pre-seed pitches don't survive seed-stage scrutiny. Investors
ask harder questions because the goalposts moved.

## What to have ready by seed

| Asset | Pre-seed | Seed |
|---|---|---|
| Pitch deck | Vision-heavy | Vision + evidence |
| Customer references | 1-2 (early users) | 5+ (paying or deeply engaged) |
| Product | Demo or prototype | Working, in real use |
| Metrics | None expected | Cohort retention, basic conversion |
| Team | 1-2 founders | Possibly 1-2 hires too |
| Plan to next round | Vague | Quarter-by-quarter milestones |
| Use of funds | Categories (eng, GTM) | Specific roles + experiments |

## Common seed-round mistakes

1. **Raising too much** — $4-6M is the modal seed in 2024-2025. $10M+ seeds
   create Series A pressure most companies can't carry.
2. **Raising too small** — under $1.5M means you're back in market in 12 months
   with not much to show.
3. **Pricing too high** — $20M+ post on weak metrics is a trap. Series A
   investors will mark you down.
4. **Wrong investors** — pre-seed investors are pattern-matchers; seed
   investors are sector-specialists. Different pitch.
5. **Closing slowly** — a 6-month seed raise drains founder time. Compress to
   8 weeks of active fundraising.

## What you should NOT do at seed

- Hire a sales leader (too early, you don't know what you're selling)
- Hire a head of marketing (founder still owns this)
- Set up a board with independent members (lightweight is fine)
- Sign multi-year vendor contracts
- Move to a flashy office
- Buy any title that includes "Chief" except for founders

## Survival math

A typical seed round of $4M with a 6-person team should last 18-24 months.
That gives you ~12 months of building before you need to start the Series A
conversation.

If your seed gives you less than 18 months runway, you raised too little or
spent too fast. Either way, you have less margin for being wrong than you
think.
