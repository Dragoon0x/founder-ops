# Co-founder relationships under stress

Co-founder breakups kill more startups than any product or market issue. The
relationship is more like a marriage than a job. Treat it accordingly.

## The 4 most common stress patterns

1. **Workload imbalance** — one founder feels they're carrying disproportionate
   weight. Real or perceived, the pattern destroys trust quickly.
2. **Role drift** — the founder who was supposed to do X is now doing Y, and
   X isn't getting done. Common after Series A as roles formalize.
3. **Strategic disagreement** — pivoting, hiring, fundraising. Healthy
   disagreement is fine; unresolved disagreement compounds.
4. **Personal life intrusion** — health, family, mental health. Real and
   common; usually unspoken until it explodes.

## The weekly co-founder 1:1

Non-negotiable. 60-90 minutes. Same day every week. Different from operating
meetings.

Agenda (suggested):
1. Personal check-in (15 min) — how are you actually
2. State of the relationship (15 min) — anything off?
3. Big decisions on the table (30 min) — what we need to align on
4. Anything else (15 min)

If you can't answer "how is your co-founder relationship right now" honestly,
the relationship is in worse shape than you think.

## The pre-mortem (do this in year 1)

Have the explicit conversation:
- What would cause us to break up?
- What's the disagreement we'd struggle to resolve?
- What's the worst version of each of us under stress?
- How do we want to handle it if one of us wants out?

This is uncomfortable and often life-saving. The relationships that don't
have this conversation early have it under crisis later, when both parties
are at their worst.

## Equity, vesting, and the founder agreement

- Standard 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff is the floor. Skip at your peril.
- Reverse vesting on existing equity if you started without (yes, do it).
- Founder agreement that covers: roles, decision-making rights, IP
  assignment, what happens if someone leaves.
- Founder shares should not be 50/50 unless that's truly the contribution
  ratio. 50/50 is a tie-breaker hack, not a contribution honest answer.
  Better: 51/49 or 60/40 with clear roles.

## The role split that survives stress

The most resilient co-founder structures have:
- One CEO (one tie-breaker)
- Clear functional ownership (CTO owns eng, COO owns ops, etc.)
- Explicit decision rights (RACI for the top 10 decisions)
- Cross-functional respect (no founder vetoes another founder's call in
  their domain)

Co-founders who jointly own everything end up jointly owning nothing.

## When you disagree

Healthy:
- Disagree privately, decide together
- Present united externally
- Document the decision so you don't relitigate
- Set a date to revisit if either feels unresolved

Unhealthy:
- Disagree in front of the team
- Take disagreements to investors as referees
- Avoid the disagreement until it explodes
- Make decisions unilaterally in the other's domain

## When one of you wants out

Most co-founder departures are mismanaged. The right way:

1. Talk early. As soon as you're seriously considering it.
2. Don't disappear. The work doesn't stop just because you've decided.
3. Negotiate the buyback. Standard: company buys back unvested shares at the
   original price; vested shares stay with the leaving founder (potentially
   at a discount in negotiation).
4. Plan the announcement. To investors first, then the team.
5. Plan the transition. 30-60 days of overlap to handoff is the minimum.
6. Talk publicly. The leaving founder should be able to say "we agreed it
   was the right move," not "I was forced out." Both parties' reputations
   depend on a clean exit.

The companies that handle co-founder departures cleanly survive them. The
companies that don't, often don't.

## Therapy

Two-founder therapy (yes, like couples counseling) is increasingly common
and recommended. A few sessions per year with someone who works with
co-founders specifically. It's cheap insurance.

Recommended threshold: when one or both of you is dreading the weekly
1:1, find a therapist by next month.

## A note on friendship

Many co-founders were friends before. The friendship will change. Some
become deeper from shared trauma; others become casualties of work stress.
Neither is wrong. But the assumption that "we'll always be friends because
we were friends before" is naive. Treat the relationship like the high-
stakes professional partnership it is, and the friendship has the best
chance of surviving.
