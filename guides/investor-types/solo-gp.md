# Working with Solo GPs

Solo GP funds are one partner managing a fund. Examples: Harry Stebbings (20VC), Lachy Groom, Elad Gil (his own fund), Oren Zeev.

## What they're good at

- Speed of decision (no partnership)
- Direct relationship with decision-maker
- Specific value-add (often industry experts or operators)
- Flexibility on terms and check size
- Alignment (their money, their brand at stake)

## What to watch for

- Key person risk (they are the fund)
- Diligence often lighter than institutional
- Follow-on capacity typically limited
- Succession planning (what happens in 10 years)
- Time availability (often stretched thin)

## Before taking their money

- LP base stability (who funds them)
- Fund size and check size
- Follow-on capacity
- Time commitment to each portfolio company
- Reputation with other investors

## Questions to ask them

- "How many companies are you actively helping today?"
- "What would it take for you to meaningfully help us?"
- "How much time per month do you spend with your active companies?"
- "What's your follow-on strategy?"
- "Who's your succession plan if something happens to you?"

## Structuring the relationship

- Less formal than institutional
- Direct text/call access often available
- Board observer typical, not director
- Use for specific help (introductions, thematic expertise)
- Don't over-rely

## When it's not working

- They're spread too thin
- Value-add hasn't materialized
- They're not providing capital for follow-on
- Their brand isn't helping recruiting or customers

Solo GPs are often easy to fire (they don't have board seat). Maintain relationship but shift priority.

## Red flags from solo GPs

- Constant travel / low availability
- Portfolio too large (60+ active)
- No clear fund mandate or strategy
- Slow on paperwork / wire
- Inconsistent communication patterns
