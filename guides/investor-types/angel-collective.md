# Working with Angel Collectives

Angel collectives are groups of angels pooling capital. Examples: AngelList syndicates, Sequoia Scouts, SV Angel (historical), specific operator syndicates.

## What they're good at

- Network density (many angels = many networks)
- Access to specific expertise across portfolio
- Lower check fatigue than single angel
- Often fast decisions
- Ambassadors in market

## What to watch for

- Dilution of governance (many small investors)
- Communication overhead
- Signal dilution (many angels, less meaningful signal)
- Lead syndicate is often single-person bottleneck
- Follow-on capacity varies widely

## Before taking their money

- Lead of the syndicate (who are they, value-add)
- Size of check (pooled amount)
- Names of participating angels (do you want them all?)
- Information rights (usually lightweight)
- Pro-rata rights structure

## Questions to ask them

- "Who leads the syndicate, and what's your relationship with them?"
- "What's the typical participation rate in your syndicates?"
- "How do LPs get involved with portfolio companies?"
- "What's the communication structure (one update, or individual)?"
- "Can I see the LP list?"

## Structuring the relationship

- Communicate through lead, not individual angels
- One monthly update to the full syndicate
- Ask the syndicate for specific help (quarterly)
- Avoid individual 1:1s unless substantive
- Don't over-rely on collective signal

## When it's not working

- Syndicate is passive, no value-add
- Lead isn't maintaining the relationship
- Too many small check-size investors creating drag on cap table
- Angel drama bleeding into company

Usually not worth managing actively. Treat as passive capital unless specific value emerges.

## Red flags from angel collectives

- Undisclosed LP composition
- Unclear pro-rata mechanics
- Syndicate lead has conflicts (other portfolio companies)
- Complex carry structures
- Participating angels you wouldn't want on cap table
