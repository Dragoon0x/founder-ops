# Working with Seed-Specific VCs

Seed funds focus exclusively on pre-seed and seed. Examples: Precursor, Homebrew, Notation, K9 Ventures, Pear, Initialized, Weekend Fund.

## What they're good at

- Deep expertise in earliest stages
- Fast decisions (often under 2 weeks)
- High touch at small check sizes
- Founder empathy and access
- Thematic focus (some are narrowly thesis-driven)

## What to watch for

- Smaller checks can mean less leverage in subsequent rounds
- Follow-on reserves are limited (often pro-rata only)
- Signal risk if they pass on your A
- Portfolio size means limited attention per company
- May push toward "name-brand" A investors for signaling

## Before taking their money

- Ownership target (typically 5–15%)
- Follow-on behavior (will they participate in A?)
- Signal implications if they don't follow
- Their relationship with A-stage firms (do they introduce?)
- How their portfolio companies have fared at A

## Questions to ask them

- "What percentage of your portfolio raises their Series A?"
- "When you don't lead the A, do you still write a pro-rata?"
- "Who are your closest A-stage partners?"
- "What do you do with your seed portfolio between close and A?"

## Structuring the relationship

- Lighter touch than a lead; often board observer
- Monthly updates with specific asks
- Use for intros (hiring, customers, A investors)
- Use for tactical help on fundraise narrative
- Help them help you (be specific about what you need)

## When it's not working

- They're not responsive to updates or asks
- They pass on follow-on without explanation
- They don't connect you to promised intros
- Their portfolio signal weighs against you

Most seed investors don't need much management. If they're not adding value, they're usually not subtracting.

## Red flags from seed VCs

- Aggressive terms for a seed check
- Board seat demands at pre-seed
- Extensive due diligence for small check sizes
- "Fund mandate" requirements that feel uncommercial
- Pushing for complex instruments instead of standard SAFE
