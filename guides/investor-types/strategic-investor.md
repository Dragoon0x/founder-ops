# Working with Strategic Investors

Strategic investors are operating companies investing in startups for business reasons. Examples: Salesforce Ventures, Microsoft M12, Intel Capital, Google Ventures (mix).

## What they're good at

- Product distribution (their customer base)
- Technical partnership
- Market credibility (their logo)
- Industry insight
- Exit optionality (potential acquirer)

## What to watch for

- Slow decision making (committees, quarterly cycles)
- Conflicts with their core business
- Influence on your strategy (they're also potential competitor)
- ROFR (right of first refusal) in later rounds
- Board behavior (often representative, less hands-on)
- "Strategic signaling" to market can mislead

## Before taking their money

- Conflict check across their business units
- Terms that limit your optionality
- Exclusive relationships or non-competes
- Clear path to independent partnership
- Pro-rata and follow-on behavior
- Public vs private investment (disclosure implications)

## Terms to avoid

- ROFR on acquisition
- Exclusive partnership clauses
- Non-compete or field-of-use restrictions
- Information rights that create conflicts
- Board seat (information sharing risk)
- Tag-along to future investors

## Questions to ask them

- "How does investment relate to partnership agreements?"
- "Who at [parent co] would be involved in decisions?"
- "What happens if our strategies diverge from yours?"
- "What's your track record of holding through exits?"
- "How do you handle conflicts between portfolio companies?"

## Structuring the relationship

- Keep investor relationship separate from commercial relationship
- Different counterparties on commercial and investment
- Don't give them early product access beyond what makes commercial sense
- Treat them like any other investor for information
- Avoid sharing future roadmap details

## When it's not working

- Their strategy shifted away from you
- They're going to compete directly
- Parent co pressure on acquisitions or restructuring
- Their investment is stalling commercial deals

Exit options: sell their shares in secondary to another investor, or accept that their investment no longer serves you.

## Red flags from strategic investors

- Pushing for exclusive partnership alongside investment
- Restrictions on working with competitors
- Non-standard information rights
- Internal politics delaying decisions
- Changes in sponsoring executive
- Product overlap that's not transparent

## When strategic money is right

- Clear business value beyond capital
- Specific commercial deal alongside investment (coordinated but separate)
- Decision-maker is committed
- Terms are founder-friendly (no ROFR, no non-compete)
- Your product complements their core business
