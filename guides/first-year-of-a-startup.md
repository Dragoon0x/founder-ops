# The first year of a startup

The first year is not what you think it is. It's not building a product. It's
not raising money. It's not finding customers. It's running an experiment to
discover whether the problem you think exists actually exists, in the form you
think it does, for people you can reach, in a quantity that matters.

## What actually happens (a realistic timeline)

### Months 0-3: Frame the question

You start with a problem hypothesis. By month 3, you should have run 30+
customer conversations and your hypothesis should look different than when you
started. If it looks the same, you weren't listening.

The deliverable at month 3 is not a product. It's a 1-page positioning
statement that survives a hostile read by someone who knows your category. If
it can't survive that, you're building on sand.

## Months 3-6: Build the smallest provable thing

The MVP is not the smallest product you can build. It's the smallest thing
that proves or disproves your hypothesis. For some businesses, this is a
landing page. For others, a no-code prototype. For others, a single working
feature.

By month 6, you should have either:
1. 3-5 paying customers (B2B), or
2. A retention curve that plateaus above 20% at week 4 (consumer), or
3. Strong qualitative signal that you're solving the wrong problem

If you have option 3, you pivot. If you have option 1 or 2, you continue. If
you have ambiguous signal, you're probably option 3 in denial.

## Months 6-9: Find the second 5 customers (B2B)

The first 5 customers are friends, network, and warm intros. The second 5 are
the test. They're strangers. They have to want it enough to overcome the
friction of working with a half-built product from a no-name company.

If the second 5 take more than 3 months, your value prop isn't sharp enough yet.
Go back to interviews.

## Months 9-12: Build the case for the next year

By month 12, you should be able to answer:
1. Who exactly do we sell to? (1-paragraph ICP, not a paragraph of vague
   adjectives)
2. What problem do we uniquely solve? (1 sentence, no jargon)
3. What does the unit economics look like at scale? (rough but defensible)
4. What would have to be true for this to be a $100M business?
5. What would kill us in the next 12 months?

Whether you raise more money depends on these answers, not on your MRR chart.

## What founders waste the first year on

- Building a fancy product before validating the problem
- Hiring more than 3 people in the first 6 months
- Going to conferences instead of doing customer calls
- Optimizing internal tools (Notion, Slack, project management)
- Designing a logo
- Setting up complex compensation structures
- Reading more startup books

## The single most useful daily habit

End each day writing 3 sentences:
1. What did I learn today?
2. What will I do tomorrow that's directly tied to that?
3. What did I not do that I should have?

Most first-year time is wasted on motion that isn't progress. The 3-sentence
journal forces the question.

## A note on optimism

You will be wrong about almost everything. That's not a sign you're a bad
founder. That's a sign you're learning. The founders who fail aren't the ones
who are wrong. They're the ones who refuse to update.
