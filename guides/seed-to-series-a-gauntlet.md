# Seed to Series A: the gauntlet

The Series A is the hardest round to raise. The pre-seed and seed are
investments in possibility. The Series A is an investment in business momentum.
Investors are looking for a company, not a project.

## The bar (rough, varies wildly)

| Metric | Below bar | At bar | Above bar |
|---|---|---|---|
| ARR (B2B SaaS) | <$500k | $1M-$2M | $3M+ |
| Growth rate (T3M annualized) | <100% | 200-300% | 400%+ |
| Net revenue retention | <100% | 105-115% | 120%+ |
| Gross margin | <60% | 65-75% | 80%+ |
| Burn multiple | >3x | 1.5-3x | <1.5x |
| Founder-led sales fading | still 100% | 60-80% | <50% (real sales motion) |
| Logo count | <20 | 30-100 | 100+ |
| Sales cycle p50 | undefined | 30-90 days | <30 days |

You don't need to be above the bar on every dimension. You need to be at-or-
above on enough that the story is "we've cracked the code, we just need fuel."

## The 3 narratives that get funded

1. **The growth narrative** — "We're growing 20% MoM with 130% NRR. We need
   capital to pour gasoline on a working motion." Easiest sell.
2. **The wedge-to-platform narrative** — "We've dominated [niche]. With this
   round, we expand to adjacent markets where the wedge gives us unfair
   advantage." Harder, but lucrative if true.
3. **The category-creation narrative** — "We've defined a new category. Early
   metrics validate. We need to own the category before competitors copy."
   Hardest, biggest-upside.

You should know which narrative you're telling and rehearse the version of it
that survives 5 hostile questions.

## What kills Series A rounds

1. **Mediocre retention** (<100% NRR for B2B SaaS) — investors interpret as
   "no real PMF"
2. **Concentration** (top 3 customers >40% of revenue) — single-customer risk
3. **Founder still selling everything** — no proof a team can do it
4. **Blurred ICP** ("we sell to anyone") — no scalable acquisition motion
5. **No category leader emerging** — investors back leaders, not contenders
6. **Burn multiple > 3** — capital efficiency matters more in 2024-2025 than
   it did in 2021

## The process (compressed timeline)

Week 1-2: Tighten deck, build data room, refresh references
Week 3-4: Soft-circle 3-5 partner meetings via existing investors
Week 5-8: Active outreach phase, 30-50 partner meetings
Week 9-10: Down-select, partner meetings deepen
Week 11-12: Term sheets in hand
Week 13-16: Diligence + close

Total: 12-16 weeks. Compress further at your peril; the rounds that close in
4 weeks are the ones with very strong inbound (not the norm).

## Negotiation anchors

- Know your minimum acceptable terms (price, board, control, founder shares)
  before the first meeting
- A clean term sheet at slightly lower valuation usually beats a complex term
  sheet at higher valuation
- Liquidation preference >1x is a yellow flag at this stage
- Participating preferred is a red flag
- Board: 5-person (2 founders, 2 investors, 1 independent) is the modal
  Series A board

## What to do if you don't hit Series A bars

Three options, in rough order of preference:
1. **Raise a seed extension** — 12 more months at flat or slightly up to
   prove the metrics. Most common path. Existing investors usually lead.
2. **Raise a bridge** — convertible note that converts at the next round.
   Smaller, faster, more dilutive but defers the price negotiation.
3. **Get to default-alive** — cut to a sustainable burn, grow more slowly,
   skip the Series A. The hardest path culturally but creates leverage.

There's no shame in not raising a Series A. There's enormous risk in raising
one you can't grow into.
