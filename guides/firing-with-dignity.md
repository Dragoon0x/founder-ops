# Firing with dignity

Firing well is a leadership skill. Firing badly is a leadership failure that
poisons the team for months. Most founders avoid the act, then do it badly
when they finally can't.

## When to fire

The earlier the better. The founders who regret firing decisions almost
universally regret waiting too long, never firing too soon. Reliable signals:

1. Performance gap that hasn't closed despite explicit feedback
2. Values violation (the manager-of-managers test: would I be proud to point
   to this behavior as how we operate?)
3. Trust gap with peers (asked privately, multiple peers wouldn't choose to
   work with this person again)
4. You wouldn't hire this person again knowing what you know now

If 2 or more of these are true, you should be in a serious conversation
within 30 days, and a decision within 90.

## The performance plan question

If the performance gap is real, do you put them on a PIP (performance
improvement plan) first?

Yes if:
- The gap is skill-based and you genuinely believe coaching can close it
- They've never had explicit feedback that this was the gap
- You'd hire them again at a different level
- You want a paper trail (sometimes you do)

No if:
- The gap is values or trust based (no plan fixes those)
- You've already given the feedback multiple times
- You wouldn't keep them even if they improved
- A PIP would be theater (everyone, including the employee, would know)

## The firing conversation (15 minutes)

Format:

1. Open with the decision (don't lead in for 5 minutes; it's worse). "I have
   difficult news. We're ending your employment with the company effective
   [date]." Then stop. Let them respond.

2. Brief reason. One sentence. "The performance plan didn't show the
   improvement we needed in the timeframe we needed it." Don't litigate.
   Don't relitigate the past.

3. Logistics. Last day. Severance. Healthcare. Equity vesting. Reference
   policy. References to a written packet you give them right after.

4. Ask if they have questions. Most won't process anything in the moment.
   Tell them they can email you with questions in the next 48 hours.

5. Wrap up. "I'm sorry. This isn't the outcome any of us wanted." Mean it.
   Then end the meeting.

The conversation should not exceed 15 minutes. Long firing conversations are
worse, not better.

## Logistics done with dignity

- Severance: minimum 1-2 weeks per year of service for ICs, 1-2 months for
  managers, more for execs (negotiate separately). Not legally required in
  most US states; do it anyway.
- COBRA / health continuation: cover for the duration of severance.
- Equity: vested shares are theirs. Vesting cliff matters legally.
- Acceleration: if no cause and >1 year tenure, consider some acceleration
  for senior hires (negotiate).
- Reference policy: be honest about whether you'll provide one. If yes, what
  it'll say. People deserve to plan their next move.

## What to tell the team

Same day or next day. Brief. Respectful.

Template:
"[Name] is leaving the company effective [date]. We thank them for [specific
contribution]. I'm not going to share more about the circumstances. If you
have questions, please come to me directly. Their work is being picked up
by [name], and we'll have a fuller plan by [date]."

Don't trash them. Don't elaborate. Don't apologize for them. Don't apologize
for the decision. Don't make it a meeting where people speculate.

## What you'll feel

Guilt. Doubt. Sometimes anger at yourself for waiting. Sometimes anger at the
person for putting you in this position. All normal.

What helps:
- Talk to a peer CEO who's done it. They've felt all of it.
- Don't drink it away. Don't avoid the office the next day.
- Write down what you'd do differently next time. The lessons compound.

## What you should not do

- Fire on a Friday (worst day; they have all weekend to spiral, no resources
  to access until Monday)
- Fire over Slack or email (shameful, even at remote-first companies)
- Fire before delivering severance paperwork (logistics matter)
- Lie about why
- Tell other employees the reason
- Ghost them after (a brief check-in 30 days later is humane)

## The asymmetry

Firing is hard for the person fired. It's also hard for everyone watching.
The team gets a referendum every time someone leaves: did the company handle
this well? If yes, trust deepens. If no, fear spreads.

The best teams have a high firing standard and a humane firing practice.
Both at once. Neither alone is enough.
