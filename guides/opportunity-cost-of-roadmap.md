# The opportunity cost of every roadmap item

Every yes on the roadmap is a no to something else. Founders often forget
this and end up with a roadmap that reads like a wishlist and ships like
molasses.

## The roadmap as a portfolio

Treat the roadmap like a financial portfolio. Each item has:
- Expected upside (revenue, retention, strategic moat)
- Expected cost (engineering weeks, opportunity cost of other things)
- Confidence level (do we know this will work)
- Time-to-payoff (this quarter, next year, never)

The mistake: founders rank by upside. Better: rank by upside-per-cost,
weighted by confidence.

## The 70/20/10 rule

A healthy roadmap allocation (rough, varies):
- 70% on known winners — improvements to things you already know work
- 20% on adjacent bets — extending the product to new use cases
- 10% on long-shots — bets that change the company if they work, kill little
  if they don't

If you're at 100% on long-shots, you're a research lab. If you're at 100%
on known winners, you're optimizing toward irrelevance. Both fail.

## The opportunity-cost question for each item

Before adding to roadmap, answer:
1. What does this displace? (Be specific. "X engineering weeks that would
   otherwise be spent on Y.")
2. What's the next-best use of that capacity? (Compare directly.)
3. If we don't do this, what's the cost? (Often: less than you think.)
4. Is this the cheapest version of solving the problem? (Often there's a
   30% solution that costs 10% as much.)

If you can't answer all four, the item isn't ready for the roadmap.

## The "kill 1 to add 1" rule

When something new gets added to the roadmap, something gets removed. Period.
This forces real prioritization. Without it, the roadmap accumulates
quarter-over-quarter and shipping velocity drops.

The exception: when capacity grows (new hires, new tools). Even then, the
default should be "spend on improving execution of existing items," not
"adding new items."

## Common high-cost / low-value items

These are the things that show up on most roadmaps and often don't deserve
to:

1. **Major UI redesigns** — high cost, often net-zero impact on retention
2. **Migration to a new framework / language** — engineer-attractive,
   business-neutral
3. **"Platform" plays** — enabling features that no one's asking for yet
4. **Integrations with low-volume partners** — N hours per integration,
   most produce <1 customer
5. **Internal tools** — make the team feel productive, rarely move the
   business
6. **Reporting / analytics inside the product** — table stakes, but rarely
   drives retention

These can all be the right call sometimes. But they're often added without
the opportunity-cost analysis.

## Common low-cost / high-value items that get neglected

The flip side:

1. **Activation flow polish** — small UX wins compound for every new user
2. **Onboarding emails** — embarrassingly easy to implement, big retention impact
3. **Pricing page rewrites** — direct revenue impact, days of work
4. **Help docs / changelog** — reduces support burden, builds trust
5. **First-week customer call follow-ups** — improves retention 10%+
6. **Killing dead features** — reduces complexity, support burden, bug surface

These don't feel like "roadmap items" because they're small. That's exactly
why they don't get done. Add them as their own line.

## The roadmap meeting

Run quarterly. Output: a 1-page roadmap with maximum 5 items per quarter.
More than 5 = you're not actually prioritizing.

Format:
- Now (this quarter): 3-5 items, committed
- Next (next quarter): 3-5 items, intended
- Later: ideas for future, not committed

Anything not on the roadmap doesn't get worked on. Side projects are visible
or they're noise.

## Saying no to roadmap items

Hardest skill. Customers ask. Investors suggest. Team members propose. The
default needs to be no, with the path to yes:
- "Bring 3 more customer requests for the same thing and we'll add it"
- "Build a prototype on a Friday and we'll review"
- "Show how this reaches our north star metric and we'll add it"

The ones that come back through these gates are the ones worth doing.
