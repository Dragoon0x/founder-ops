# Series A to B: the management transition

Series B is when the company stops being a cult and starts being a company.
This is the transition that breaks the most founders.

## What changes at Series B

| Dimension | Pre-B | Post-B |
|---|---|---|
| Headcount | 20-40 | 50-150 |
| Layers | Founder + ICs | Founder + VPs + managers + ICs |
| Decision speed | Hours | Days |
| Information asymmetry | Founder knows everything | Founder learns through reports |
| Cultural reinforcement | Osmosis | Explicit (handbook, rituals) |
| Hiring | Network-based | Process-based |
| Performance management | Vibes | System (perf cycles, leveling) |
| OKRs | Optional | Necessary |

## The 3 founder roles you must learn (or hire around)

1. **Capital allocator** — deciding which bets get how much fuel. This was
   easy at 10 people; you said yes to everything that mattered. At 80 people
   you have to say no to most things, and the saying no is the work.
2. **Manager of managers** — your direct reports are now managers themselves.
   You manage them differently than ICs. If you've never done this, hire a
   coach or read [High Output Management].
3. **Public face** — fundraising, hiring, press, customer escalations. You're
   the company's brand whether you want to be or not.

## The 5 hires that almost everyone gets wrong (in this order)

1. **VP Sales** — usually too early or too late. Right time: when founder-led
   sales is at $2M+ ARR and the founder is the bottleneck. Wrong: a "VP" who's
   really a senior AE.
2. **VP Engineering** — usually a person who can't write code anymore. Wrong:
   prioritizes process over product.
3. **VP Marketing** — usually hired to "do demand gen." Wrong: hired before
   the company knows what to say.
4. **VP People / HR** — usually hired too late. Right time: 30-40 employees.
5. **CFO / VP Finance** — usually hired too late. Right time: $10M ARR or
   16 months pre-IPO.

For each hire, the right interview includes a 90-day plan exercise where they
present what they'd do. The plan tells you whether they understand your stage.

## The cultural fork

At 20 people, every employee was hand-picked, talked to the founder daily, and
absorbed the values through proximity. At 80 people, half the employees have
never had a 1:1 with you, and the values they absorb come from their manager.

What you have to do:
- Write the values down (handbook)
- Repeat them in every all-hands (ritual)
- Make them legible in performance reviews (reinforcement)
- Fire fast when someone violates them (consequences)
- Promote loudly when someone exemplifies them (signal)

The companies that don't do this end up with a culture they don't recognize
by Series C.

## Information architecture

You will lose visibility. Information has to flow up and across in a system,
not in your head. Minimum:
- Weekly written status (every team)
- Monthly business review (every department)
- Quarterly board meeting
- Annual strategy offside

Without this, you'll lead the company by anecdote, and anecdote is biased
toward the loudest.

## What founders most regret about this transition

1. Hiring too slowly (almost universal regret)
2. Tolerating senior people who weren't great (always too long)
3. Not writing things down (handbook, decisions, plans)
4. Avoiding hard performance conversations
5. Not building a peer network of CEOs at the same stage to talk to

## The personal cost

Series B is when most founders' marriages, friendships, and health start
slipping. The work expands; the day doesn't. You have to make space for the
non-work life intentionally or it disappears. See `guides/founder-energy-management.md`.
