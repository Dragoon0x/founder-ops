# How to pivot without breaking the company

Pivots get romanticized in retrospect. Slack pivoted, Twitter pivoted, Instagram
pivoted. What gets edited out: most pivots fail. The ones that work follow a
pattern.

## When to pivot

Real reasons:
1. After 6+ months of effort, retention curves don't plateau
2. CAC consistently exceeds LTV with no clear path to fix
3. Customer interviews reveal you're solving a wish, not a need
4. The TAM in your wedge is much smaller than you thought
5. A 10x better solution exists that you can't compete with

Bad reasons (these aren't pivots, they're escapism):
1. Sales is hard (it always is)
2. Product is hard (it always is)
3. A new shiny opportunity appeared (FOMO is not strategy)
4. An investor suggested it (their job is to brainstorm, not to set strategy)
5. A team member is bored

## Types of pivot

| Type | What stays | What changes |
|---|---|---|
| Customer pivot | Product | Who you sell to |
| Problem pivot | Customer | What problem you solve for them |
| Solution pivot | Customer + problem | How you solve it |
| Business model pivot | Product | How you make money |
| Wedge pivot | Long-term vision | Entry point |
| Total pivot | Almost nothing | Almost everything |

The smaller the pivot, the higher the success rate. Total pivots usually
become new companies in a sweater.

## The 90-day pivot framework

### Days 0-14: Diagnose

- Why exactly is the current path failing? Not vibes; evidence.
- What did you learn that's salvageable for any new direction?
- What would have to be true for the current path to work?
- Run 10-15 customer interviews specifically about the failure mode
- Talk to 3-5 trusted advisors

Output: a 1-page document titled "Why we are pivoting." If you can't write
this in 1 page, you don't understand the failure yet.

### Days 14-30: Frame the alternatives

- Generate 3-5 candidate directions (with the team, not in isolation)
- For each: what's the wedge, who's the customer, what's the unique insight,
  what would the first product be
- Pre-mortem each: how does this fail?
- Get external read on the top 2

### Days 30-60: Make the call

- Commit to one direction
- Communicate to the team. Honest. Why we're pivoting. What we're pivoting
  to. What changes for each person.
- Communicate to investors. Email + call. Same content; longer Q&A.
- Communicate to existing customers (if any). Be honest about whether you'll
  continue to support them.
- Write the new positioning, ICP, and 90-day plan

### Days 60-90: Show progress

- The team needs to see the pivot is working before doubt sets in
- Aim for 1-2 paying customers in the new direction within 60 days of pivot
- Aim for measurable signal (retention, conversion) within 90 days
- Daily standups; weekly all-hands; tight feedback loops

If at day 90 you don't have any of: paying customers, qualitative signal, or
team enthusiasm — the pivot isn't working. Pivot again or wind down.

## What you'll lose

- Some team members will leave. Plan for it.
- Some customers will churn. Be honest with them.
- Some investors will be skeptical. Show progress fast.
- Some momentum (the worst kind of loss; people remember what you used to
  do, not what you do now). Manage public messaging carefully.

## What you can keep

- Distribution (sometimes — depends on if the new audience overlaps)
- Brand (sometimes — depends on if the brand was tied to the old product)
- Team skills (mostly)
- Code (rarely; usually less than founders think)
- Customer relationships (if you handle the transition well)

## Communicating the pivot

To the team (in person, all-hands, day of decision):
1. What we're doing (in one sentence)
2. Why (honest, brief)
3. What changes for the company (strategy, roadmap, hiring, runway)
4. What changes for individual people (roles, expectations, comp if relevant)
5. What's not changing (values, equity, employment)
6. Q&A (long, honest)

To investors (email + call within 24 hours of team announcement):
1. Decision and rationale
2. New plan, new milestones, new burn
3. Updated runway analysis
4. What you need from them
5. Schedule a call for deeper Q&A

## After the pivot

Do not relitigate the previous direction publicly. The team has moved on.
Talking about "the old us" creates ambiguity about which one is real now.

Do learn from the previous direction. Privately. Write down what you learned,
keep it for next year.
